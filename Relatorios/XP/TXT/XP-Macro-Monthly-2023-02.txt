Foreword – Risk of Impact
In our previous Brazil Macro Monthly - Fiscal and monetary policies on a collision course - we drew
attention to the risks of an expansionary fiscal policy in a moment of high government debt and
elevated inflation. On this background, we wrote that “We anticipate increasing tension between the
Executive branch and the Central Bank.”
This risk remains. In its latest monetary policy meeting, the Central Bank pointed out that “The current
scenario, particularly uncertain on the fiscal side and with inflation expectations drifting away from the
inflation target on longer horizons (…) raises the cost of the disinflation that is needed to reach the
targets established by the National Monetary Council”. In any event, the Central Bank recognized in
the minutes of its latest Copom meeting that the fiscal measures announced by the Ministry of
Finance may help to reduce inflation risks ahead.
In the same week, President Lula said that the central bank must explain the reason for such high
rates and that the 3% inflation target is too low for Brazilian needs. The President reinforced that
fiscal and quasi-fiscal policies should be set to foster investment.
In our view, this diverged signaling between monetary tightening and fiscal expansion tends to add
pressure on inflation expectations, making the central bank’s task even more challenging. We keep
our view that the Selic rate will remain at current level throughout 2023, with an increasing probability
that the government proposes an increase in the inflation target for next year, hoping for an (unlikely)
room for rate cuts.
Having said that, Brazilian asset prices have recently benefited from a relatively positive global
environment, driven by the reduction of the Fed’s tightening pace and China’s economic reopening.
We believe that global disinflation will continue, though it remains uncertain whether the US and
Europe will fall into a recession (and how deep it might be).

Global backdrop: Improvement 
in the global growth outlook 
should be taken with a pinch of 
salt
Commodity prices are accommodating at high levels. We see 
commodities prices relatively stable ahead, supported by demand 
pressures coming from China´s reopening and supply constraints. 
The largest risk to our global disinflation narrative is that China´s 
reopening forces a stronger-than-expected impact on gasoline 
prices, causing inflation to become stickier. Still, this information 
appears to be priced in already, and we expect Brent prices to 
fluctuate around USD 90 per barrel throughout the year.
Resilient global activity, although a slowdown is still expected. The 
global economy has shown a stronger-than-expected performance 
in recent months, and major economies have resisted entering 
deep contractionary territory. Fear of a global recession has eased, 
and the strong growth expected for China and India should offset 
the weakness in economic activity in the rest of the world. Still, we 
believe that it is still too early to rule out the possibility that the US 
and Europe may enter a period of recession, given that the effect 
of contractionary monetary policy on aggregate demand will 
become increasingly evident throughout the year.
The Fed decelerated the tightening pace, although pressures 
remain. The FOMC decided to decelerate the tightening pace to 
25 bps in the February meeting, taking the upper bound of the Fed 
Funds rate to 4.75%. Chairman Powell reiterated concerns 
regarding inflation and the “extremely tight” labor market, although 
authorities recognized that the disinflationary process has already 
begun, suggesting that the tightening cycle is approaching its end. 
We believe the upcoming inflation numbers will be significantly 
disinflationary, although the persistent tightness in the jobs market 
and high services inflation may pressure the Fed to hike rates by 
25 bps at least once more.
Resilience in Europe, although it is too early to rule out the 
possibility of a recession. A few months back, it appeared that 
Europe would face one of deepest recessions among major 
economies. Recent data have shown strong resilience of 
economic activity in the region amid improvements in the energy 
crisis. Moreover, China´s reopening is expected to bring a positive 
tourism effect during the summer. Still, monetary policy is 
becoming increasingly restrictive with the shift of the ECB towards 
a much more hawkish stance, and tighter financial conditions pose 
considerable downside risks to the growth outlook in the Eurozone.
China to partially offset weakness in global activity. We believe that 
China´s GDP will grow by around 5.5% in 2023, supported by the 
removal of covid related restrictions and the effects of the 
expansionary policies delivered throughout the whole of last year. 
The reopening of the Chinese economy is also expected to have 
positive spillover effects on other regions, mainly for China´s trade 
partners.

Economic Activity – Widespread 
slowdown
Stagnant domestic activity at the end of 2022. The Brazilian 
economy has cooled down in recent months, mainly reflecting 
the impact of the restrictive monetary policy. According to our 
calculations, headline GDP stayed flat in Q422 from Q322 (up 
2.2% versus Q421). The material economic growth in 2022 – 
estimated at 3% – was due to a strong performance in the first 
half.
Tightening of credit conditions, following the script. Credit 
concessions have been slowing down gradually, as expected. 
Delinquency rates – especially for individuals – continue on an 
upward trend given the increased household debt service ratio. 
Credit statistics are expected to worsen further in the coming 
months, but not severely, in our assessment. In fact, some credit 
lines showed stabilization of delinquency rates at the end of last 
year.
Economic slowdown hit the labor market. The main employment 
indicators have lost strength in the recent period, after several 
quarters on the rise. According to CAGED report, the net creation 
of formal jobs dropped from a monthly average of around 
200,000 between January and September to 65,000 between 
October and December, discounting seasonal effects. The 
national unemployment rate – PNAD data – rose slightly to 8.3% 
in November from 8.2% in October (monthly estimates), after 
eight straight declines. On the brighter side, labor earnings 
remain on an upward path, approaching the levels recorded 
before the pandemic (in real terms).
We continue to expect a 1% growth for 2023 GDP. 
Contractionary monetary policy and the end of the ‘post-Covid’ 
impulse are the main reasons for the sharp economic slowdown 
compared to 2022. The loss of momentum has spread across 
sectors. For example, total industry retreated in the last quarter, 
while retail and services activities advanced only modestly.
Strong expansion in Agriculture and Livestock as “salvation of 
the crop” for headline GDP. As per our estimates, the 2023 GDP 
rate of change would be close to zero if it weren’t for the strong 
performance of agricultural output and the positive statistical 
carry-over effect from last year’s GDP (estimated at 0.4pp). 
Regarding the primary sector GDP, we anticipate an annual 
increase of 8% (contribution of 0.5pp to overall GDP), especially 
due to the jump in the soybean crop. For instance, we predict a 
0.6% QoQ expansion for Q123 GDP. Excluding the Agriculture 
and Livestock GDP, which is expected to climb around 8.5% 
QoQ, we calculate a virtual stability for headline GDP (-0.1% 
QoQ). In addition to these factors, the additional rise in the real

The government registered a surplus of 1.3% of GDP in its 
primary balance in 2022. Breaking down, the central government 
posted a surplus of BRL 54.9 billion (0.6% of GDP), the first 
positive result since 2013. States and municipalities, recorded a 
surplus of BRL 64.9 billion (0.7% of GDP), thanks to a positive 
result accumulated in the first semester. State-owned companies 
had a surplus of BRL 6.1 billion.
The positive performance, however, should not be seen in 2023. 
As highlighted in our previous Brazil Macro Monthly, the increase 
in central government spending for this year is estimated at BRL 
210 billion, or 2% of GDP, especially due to the approval of the 
transition constitutional amendment. Our initial estimate, 
considering the effects of this expansion, was a deficit of 
BRL167,2 billion, (1.6% of GDP).
Government announced a revenue-focused fiscal package. 
Considering the scenario described above, the government 
announced a package aimed at reducing the projected deficit for 
the year. Out of the announced measures, some of them were 
already accounted in our estimates, such as the return of 
PIS/Cofins on fuels. However, other measures such as the 
exclusion of ICMS from the PIS/Cofins credit base, the use of 
resources from the PIS/PASEP fund and some spending 
reduction through a detailed assessment  of the Bolsa-Familia 
welfare program could result in an improvement of BRL83 billion

That said, there are significant risks regarding tax collection this 
year. A major part of the revenue gain last year is a direct product 
of the oil price shock, which sustained higher gains in 
IRPJ/CSLL, royalties and special participations and dividends. 
Although our estimates consider that oil prices should remain at 
current levels, there are significant risks of revenue decline if 
these prices fall.
Additionally, revenue gains could be offset by increased 
spending proposals. Discussions are still underway on raising, 
as of May, the minimum wage to BRL1,320 and the personal 
income tax allowance to two minimum wages. If approved, the 
changes could have an impact of BRL5 billion and BRL10 billion 
this year, respectively.
Local government accounts should also bring increased 
concerns. State and municipal government finances had a 
significant deterioration at the end of 2022, and also represent a 
risk this year and next. The results for the last three months of 
the year showed a deterioration in subnational government 
accounts, registering the three worst historical results since 
2002. In our view, this reflects the impacts of the complementary 
law that reduced ICMS rates on electricity and fuel – approved in 
June 2022. For 2023, 12 states have already increased the base 
ICMS rates to offset these losses. We expect the deterioration to 
continue, with the local government´s primary surplus falling to 
0.1% of GDP in 2023 and reaching a deficit of 0.1% of GDP in 
2024.
Public debt fell in 2022, but we expect it to rise again in the 
coming years. The reversal of the primary balance and the higher 
nominal interest, resulting from an expected higher Selic rate for 
longer, should cause the public debt to resume the upward trend 
this year and next. Our updated forecast stands at 73.5% of GDP 
in 2022, 77.2% in 2023, and 81.0% of GDP in 2024.
Debt stabilization is highly contingent on a new credible fiscal 
framework. Uncertainty regarding stabilizing public debt has led 
inflation expectations to levels above their target for the coming 
years. As a result, the Central Bank will likely find no alternative 
but to keep interest rates higher for longer (see monetary policy 
section), which has the side effect of raising the cost of public 
debt. To break this vicious circle, it is essential to approve a new 
– and credible – public accounts framework that creates the path 
for fiscal sustainability in the medium term.
Finally, discussions on a much-needed tax reform tend to 
progress but may be tainted by fiscal adjustment. At first, we 
understand that the national VAT proposal embodied in PEC nº 
45/2019 should be the path to be followed for a reform of indirect 
taxation. On the other hand, there are still little signs on the path 
ahead regarding changes to the income and payroll taxes. There 
is, however, the risk that the need for fiscal adjustment leads to 
increased tax burden proposals, which could contaminate 
structural discussions.

Inflation – Short-term slowdown, 
pressure ahead
This month, we lift our 2023 IPCA forecast to 5.7% from 5.4%. 
The adjustment was concentrated on two items - car licensing 
and gasoline. Our core inflation forecasts remained unchanged.
The relatively benign short-term trend continues. Industrialized 
goods inflation is falling on the back of global disinflation, rising 
food supply and cooling domestic demand. The 3-month 
seasonally adjusted annualized moving average of the core 
IPCA decelerated significantly. We project that this trend will 
continue throughout the first half of this year.
Tax increases are a risk for the coming months. We already 
consider the return of Pis/Cofins (federal taxes) on gasoline and 
ethanol, although it has yet to be confirmed. Moreover, States 
are seeking solutions to compensate for the ICMS tax break 
brought by Law 194/2022 by raising the threshold tax rate on fuel 
and communication or increasing rates for other products, such 
as medicines. These measures could add up to 60 bps to our 
2023 IPCA estimate.
For 2024, we raised our forecast from 4.0% to 4.5%. The most 
recent signaling of fiscal and parafiscal policy and tax 
uncertainties should make the disinflation process even slower 
in 2024. In addition, in a recent interview, President Lula da Silva 
said he believes 4% inflation is more suitable for Brazil than the 
current 3% target.
In our view, raising the inflation target would bring uncertainty 
regarding the commitment to low inflation. According to our 
estimates, expectations would rise above the new target, with an 
impact of approximately 150bps in the Central Bank’s current 
estimation for 2024.
External Sector: Volatile exchange 
rate and even more robust balance

US dollar and the reopening of the Chinese economy (loosening 
pandemic-related restrictions). The exchange rate has varied 
between BRL/USD 4.95 and BRL/USD 5.45 since the beginning 
of the year.

Net inflows in FDI (Foreign Direct Investment) totaled USD 90.6 
billion in 2022 (4.8% of GDP), the best result since 2012. This 
amount was significantly better than the result registered in 2021 
(USD 46.4 billion or 2.8% of GDP), reflecting a combination of 
factors: (I) strong GDP recovery (annual economic growth

the beginning of the year); (II) significant portion of 
sectors/companies very sensitive to commodity prices, which 
remain at high levels; (III) economic and political weaknesses in 
other major emerging economies that generally compete with 
respect to attracting foreign capital; and (IV) great potential for 
investments in the ‘green economy’ (mainly in the renewable 
energy segment). Looking ahead, we expect net inflows in FDI to 
reach USD 80 billion in 2023 (3.9% of GDP) and USD 75 billion 
in 2024 (3.6% of GDP). In other words, levels below those 
recorded for 2022, in line with the slowdown in economic activity, 
but still close to all-time highs.
The widening of the current account deficit last year stemmed 
from the normalization of activities in the ‘post-pandemic’ period, 
but we anticipate a reduction going forward. The current account 
balance ended 2022 at USD -55.7 billion (-2.9% of GDP), down 
from USD -46.4 billion in 2021 (-2.8% of GDP). We draw attention 
to the larger deficits in the Services and Primary Income 
accounts over the past year. Regarding the former, the economic 
reopening and subsequent normalization of the consumption 
pattern have driven expenses with international travel, transport 
services and equipment rental. On the other hand, the 
merchandise trade balance continues to hover at high levels 
(USD 44.4 billion in 2022 from USD 36.4 billion in 2021), owing 
chiefly to elevated commodity prices and firm external demand 
for goods exported by Brazil. This dynamic should not be 
reversed in the short term, in our view, especially given better 
economic growth projections in China. Besides, total imports are 
expected to grow at a more moderate pace in the current year 
amid the cooling of domestic demand.
Therefore, on the heels of more encouraging prospects for the 
value of net exports, we revised our forecasts for the current 
account balance: to USD -50.5 billion (-2.5% of GDP) from USD 
-64.5 billion (-3.2% of GDP) in 2023; and to USD -53.0 billion (-
2.6% of GDP) from USD -67.0 billion (-3.3% GDP) in 2024.

Monetary Policy – Selic rate stays 
flat; we expect the pressure on the 
Copom to increase
We continue to see short-term inflation on a downward trend. 
Global disinflation and domestic economic deceleration should 
keep consumer inflation on a falling path. Recent IPCA data 
show decompression even in stickier groups, like services.
However, fiscal uncertainties and the discussion on the inflation 
target tend to put further pressure on inflation expectations. 
Indeed, medium term market forecasts are already some 30% 
above the target, even with current inflation trending down (see 
chart).
The Copom reinforced this risk in its latest post-meeting 
statement. “The current scenario, particularly uncertain on the 
fiscal side and with inflation expectations drifting away from the 
inflation target on longer horizons (…) raises the cost of the 
disinflation that is needed to reach the targets established by the 
National Monetary Council”
In our view, the recent developments and the signaling from the 
Copom reinforce our scenario that the Selic rate will remain at 
current contractionary levels throughout this year.
As economic activity loses steam and government debt dynamics 
worsen, the pressure on the BCB to cut rates increase. The 
Copom, on its turn, has kept the focus of its official 
communication on reaching the target, but also recognized that 
the fiscal measures announced by the Ministry of Finance may 
help to  reduce inflation risks ahead.
For 2024, we now see higher inflation and, therefore, higher 
nominal interest rates. Due to rising inflation expectations, we 
raised our IPCA forecast for 2024 to 4.5%. Thus, we now see 
lower room for nominal rate cuts next year. We adjusted our Selic 
forecast to 11.0% for 2024 yearend. If inflation expectations 
continue to rise, the room for rate cuts tends to be even lower.

Foreword – Inflation outlook improves but risks remain
Brazilian inflation outlook is finally improving. The BRL has appreciated some 10% YTD, partially due to elevated domestic 
interest rates. Local demand is starting to decelerate, owing chiefly to credit-related segments. Recent IPCA (consumer price 
index) readings have been in line with or below expectations, with a benign breakdown. Producer prices have accumulated 
5.2% deflation over the past five months, what suggests that consumer inflation (IPCA) will remain subdued in the coming 
months.
Also on the favorable front is the increased likelihood that the Fed should resume its easing cycle earlier than expected.
That said, it is still early for the Brazilian Central Bank (BCB) to pop the cork. First, the BRL positive performance may well be 
partially reverted if the USD picks up. Second, domestic labor market remains heated, with unemployment at record lows and 
real earnings posting solid gains. Third, government’s growth supportive measures are in the pipeline to support the economy 
next year.
Thus, our IPCA forecast for 2026 remain at 4.5%, the upper bound of the target range. We reduced our terminal Selic rate to 
12.00% from 12.50% but still believe the BCB will wait until early next year to start the easing cycle.
We kept most of our forecasts unchanged, apart from a minor increase in the current account estimate for 2025 and 2026 
due to changes in the historical series.
Finally, two additional topics are worth mentioning.
Tariffs imposed by US government on Brazilian exports created some noise, but tend to have little economic impact – 
particularly after the long list of exceptions (some 45% of total exports to the US).
Fiscal revenues remain strong and received additional boost with the Financial Transaction Tax (IOF) rate increase and 
revenue from future oil field auctions (allowed by a law recently passed by Congress). Thus, we now see the government 
meeting the fiscal targets in 2025 and 2026.

restrictive measures imposed last month by making public 
statements through third-party social media accounts.
The measure gave Bolsonaro’s allies room to reinforce claims of 
political persecution — especially because the arrest was 
ordered in a separate case from the one investigating the 
January 8 events, prior to a final conviction, and on Moraes’ 
individual decision.
The rise in political tensions — beyond bolstering the Bolsonarist 
narrative — allowed the opposition to paralyze Congress in the 
first week after the legislative recess. The disruption had 
consequences for the government’s economic agenda. The first 
public hearing of the committee analyzing Provisional Measure 
(MP) 1,303 — which alters investment fund taxation and would 
have included Finance Minister Fernando Haddad — had to be 
canceled due to the lack of a conducive political environment, 
delaying the legislative schedule by at least a week. Similarly, 
confirmation hearings for some nominees to regulatory 
agencies scheduled for next week were also canceled, along 
with the installation of the committee that will examine the MP 
for the electric sector.
Efforts to unblock the congressional agenda now hinge on 
opposition demands to advance votes on the Amnesty Bill, the 
impeachment of Justice Moraes, and a constitutional 
amendment (PEC) removing the Supreme Court’s power to try 
members of Congress — none of which currently enjoy majority 
support. The congressional leadership is expected to avoid more 
polarizing proposals and attempt to ease tensions in the coming 
weeks.
While the group’s ability to mobilize may weaken over time, it 
continues to hinder the government’s plans to accelerate its 
agenda in the second half of the year. In this context, the 
opposition hopes to exploit the volatile political environment to 
delay revenue-raising measures — aiming to undermine the 
government’s fiscal capacity and, consequently, its ability to 
propose public policies that could boost its popularity in an 
election year.
Two particularly important items that will require close attention 
from the government to move forward as planned by the 
economic team are: (i) the income tax reform — already 
approved in the special committee of the Lower House but still 
pending votes in the House of Representatives and the Senate, 
and (ii) MP 1,303, which still needs to be discussed in a joint 
committee before heading to both legislative Houses.
On the electoral front, the abrupt decision to arrest Bolsonaro — 
and the subsequent escalation in rhetoric against the Supreme 
Court — exposed lingering divisions among his supporters 
regarding the configuration of the 2026 presidential race. At the 
height of the turmoil, there appears to be little incentive for 
Bolsonaro to publicly abandon his own political project for 2026, 
although the possibility of endorsing a candidate outside his 
immediate family remains open.

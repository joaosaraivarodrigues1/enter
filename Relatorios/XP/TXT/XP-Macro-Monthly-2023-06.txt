Foreword – Calm before the storm? 
 
Brazilian financial markets have been performing well in recent weeks. The exchange rate strengthened to 5.00 reais per 
dollar and is hovering around this level with relatively low volatility. The Ibovespa reached 110k points for the first time in 
four months, and long-term interest rates dropped some 120 bps in the second quarter.
Do fundaments back this improvement, or are we living a “calm-before-the-storm” moment?
We have been arguing that the global environment became more favorable for Brazilian assets. We keep this optimistic 
view. The monetary tightening cycles in DMs are approaching their end, and commodity prices remain relatively high. We 
see the dollar weakening somewhat in the coming months, benefiting other DMs and commodity currencies.
This positive environment should continue ahead, though political and geopolitical risks need close monitoring. The 
sustainability of China´s economic recovery is also a risk for Brazil.
Domestically, recent news is also positive: growth forecasts have been revised up, while short-term inflation expectations 
have dropped. The Lower House approved the new fiscal framework, and the central bank has maintained its hawkish and 
independent speech.
There are reasons to believe that those positive developments could be short-lived. The upward revision in growth forecasts 
was mostly due to much better-than-expected agriculture output. Deflationary pressures are concentrated on volatile items 
like gasoline and meat. Fiscal policy will remain expansionary going forward even with the new framework, and political 
pressures on the Central Bank will only increase as Governor Campos´s mandate approaches its final year.
Still, it does not seem to be a rupture scenario for Brazil, neither externally nor domestically. It means that recent 
improvement in asset prices tends to be permanent, though it will not necessarily continue ahead.
We recently revised our 2023 yearend forecast for the BRL to 5.00 reais per dollar (5.30 before). We continue to see the 
central bank beginning its easing cycle in August, though we recognize that so far, the monetary authority did not provide 
any signal that a rate cut is approaching.

Global backdrop: central banks to 
remain vigilant
Francisco Nobre 
 
Latest data consistent with our call of no rate cuts in the US this 
year. Inflationary pressures persist, and the labor market remains 
tight, with 80% excess demand for workers. Since the reopening 
of the US economy post Covid, consumption has shifted strongly 
towards services, which typically require more workers. This is 
the main factor driving the tightness of the jobs market and 
inflationary pressures, primarily concentrated in the services 
category. In fact, economic activity has also been surprising to 
the upside, driven by the strength of this sector. Consequently, 
market bets have shifted towards higher rates for longer, moving 
closer to our call of the maintenance of the Fed Funds rate at 
current levels until yearend. Still, the possibility of another 25-bps 
hike cannot be ruled out, and policymakers are expected to 
remain data-driven. 
 
USD resilience amid easing tail risks and the expectations of high 
interest rates for longer. First, the fear that the recent banking 
turmoil could lead to a systematic issue is easing. In fact, bank 
deposits have started to rise again, showing that the fear of bank 
failure has abated. Second, markets have started to price higher 
rates for longer. Lastly, the US government has reached a deal 
to suspend the debt ceiling until 2025 while implementing 
spending restrictions for the current government. The agreement 
has ended a weeks-long political stand-off that risked an 
unprecedented default of the US government. These factors 
have driven the USD index to rebound in May, despite this year´s 
perspective of depreciation amid falling interest rates differentials 
against other developed economies. On the downside, with the 
debt ceiling suspended, the US Treasury will rebuild its cash 
balances and drain some USD 500 billion in liquidity from the 
private sector, which is expected to negatively impact asset 
prices. 
 
In Europe, the tightening cycle is not over yet. The ECB has 
maintained a hawkish stance, and despite the May CPI data 
providing some relief, more evidence that inflation is converging 
towards the 2% target will be required before pausing the 
tightening cycle. In the United Kingdom, despite the not-so-
hawkish stance from the BoE, the latest inflation data has 
surprised the Central Bank´s (pessimistic) expectations, and 
policymakers are unlikely to be able to defend the end of rate 
hikes. Higher rates in the region, along with the positive current 
account balance expected for this year, are likely to strengthen 
the euro against the dollar going forward.   
 
Slower than expected economic recovery in China. Despite the 
recent marginal improvements in high-frequency economic data, 
China´s economic rebound is proving weaker than anticipated. 
Industrial production and retail activity have disappointed lately, 
and the property sector – China´s primary growth driven in recent 
years – remains weak. Personal consumption is expected to 
rebound strongly due to high pent-up demand and accumulated

Economic Activity – we raised our 2023 GDP 
growth forecast to 2.2% from 1.4% 
 
Rodolfo Margato 
 
Brazil’s GDP grew above expectations in 1Q, boosted by 
agricultural production. The domestic economy expanded by 
1.9% QoQ in 1Q, above projections (XP: 1.4%; Street 
consensus: 1.2%). The stronger-than-anticipated performance 
stemmed mainly from the jump in the Agriculture and Livestock 
GDP. The primary sector skyrocketed around 22% QoQ last 
quarter, driven by the sharp increase in soybean output (the grain 
harvest will likely reach a new all-time high in 2023).
Economic activity should slow down throughout this year… We 
have seen a cooling local economy when excluding the 
contributions of sectors less sensitive to the economic cycle 
(agriculture, livestock, and mining industry as protagonists). For 
example, the manufacturing industry fell for the third consecutive 
quarter in 1Q, a trend that should not be reversed in the short 
term. Industrial output dropped more than expected in April, 
mostly due to significant declines in the categories of capital 
goods and durable consumer goods. Additionally, civil 
construction remains on a downward trajectory, while the 
services sector has been growing at a more moderate pace in 
the recent period. The dissipation of the “post-Covid” impulse, 
the worsening credit conditions, and the high indebtedness of 
families and companies weigh on domestic demand.
… albeit at a slower pace than initially thought. The increase in 
real household disposable income, in the wake of the resilient 
labor market and higher government cash transfers, supports 
consumption in the short term. In this regard, employment and 
income indicators were positive in April. The national jobless rate 
fell for the third straight month, to 8.3% from 8.0% (monthly 
seasonally adjusted estimates), whereas labor earnings continue 
to recover. Similarly, the Ministry of Labor’s report (CAGED) 
showed a net creation of around 165,000 formal jobs in the 
moving quarter up to April. That is, the balance of job creation 
remains at high levels, despite the slowdown compared to March.
Therefore, we raised our projection for 2023 GDP growth, to 
2.2% from 1.4%. The sharper-than-anticipated economic growth 
in 1Q and signs of further advance in 2Q (although at a modest 
pace, given the effect of the high base of comparison) support 
the upward revision to our base scenario for domestic activity.

Public finance: The House approves the new 
fiscal framework, but primary balance remains 
far from its target   
 
Tiago Sbardelotto 
 
Recent results continue to show a slight deterioration. The 
consolidated public sector recorded a primary surplus of BRL 
20.3 billion in April. On the breakdown, the central government 
and subnational governments had a surplus of BRL 16.9 billion 
and BRL 4.0 billion, respectively, while state-owned companies 
had a deficit of BRL 1.7 billion. Despite the surplus, April’s 
outcome represents a drop of almost 50% in real terms 
compared to the same month last year. In turn, the 12 months 
accumulated surplus reached BRL 56.2 billion (0.6% of GDP), a 
drop of 0.9pp when compared to April 2022.
The central government should maintain a negative trajectory. 
We expect that the drop in commodity prices and the slowdown 
in economic activity will negatively affect tax collection in the 
coming months, more than offsetting the measures already 
announced by the government at the beginning of this year. On 
the expenditure side, the increase in the minimum wage and the 
readjustment for civil servants should start weighing from May 
onwards. As a result, we changed our primary result forecast 
from a deficit of BRL 119.6 billion (1.1% of GDP) to a deficit of 
BRL 122.9 billion (1.2% of GDP) in 2023. But if we consider the 
“pooling” effect (expenditures committed but not paid), which 
should be between BRL 20 billion and BRL 30 billion this year, 
the deficit could be lower than BRL 100 billion (0.9% of GDP).

Inflation – We reduced our inflation forecast for 
2023 and 2024; wholesale prices indicate 
further disinflation throughout the year 
 
Alexandre Maluf 
 
We revised our 2023 and 2024 exchange rate and inflation 
forecasts. For 2023 IPCA, we reduced our number to 5.4% from 
6.2%. The downward revision to our forecast was driven by 
Petrobras´s decision to cut prices of gasoline, diesel and cooking 
gas on May 16, which we attribute as definitive, and is consistent 
with a more appreciated exchange rate and the current Brent 
prices. As a result, our forecast for monitored prices edged down 
to 9.3% from 11.1%. 
 
We also reduced our projection for the 'food at home' group to 
2.2% from 3.5%, reflecting the relevant drop in grains and 
proteins seen so far this year. For industrial goods, our forecast 
fell to 2.6% from 2.9%, because of a more appreciated average 
exchange rate. Finally, the Services group fell to 6.0% from 6.3%. 
For more details, see the full report (link).

compared to April, below the market expectations (0.64%) and 
our forecast (0.67%). It is worth mentioning that we consider this 
inflation reading to be the most encouraging published so far in 
2023, given the widespread disinflation seen in its breakdown.

Monetary Policy – Gradual and (likely) limited 
easing cycle ahead 
 
Caio Megale 
 
We see reasons for monetary policy to be somewhat less 
restrictive ahead. i) Producer prices are falling in many countries, 
including Brazil (FGV’s Producer Price Index-M inflation is at -
7.54% year over year); ii) the domestic credit market is cooling 
down and delinquency rates are on the rise, particularly for 
companies; iii) the exchange rate is 5% stronger year to date; iv) 
inflation expectations (according to BCB’s Focus Survey) edged 
down for 2023 and stop rising for 2024; v) the Minister of Finance 
has been signaling to be in favor of keeping the inflation target in 
3.0%, though formally extending the convergency horizon seems 
likely. 
 
Thus, we see the Central Bank beginning an easing cycle in 
2H23. Our baseline scenario considers a 25-bps cut in August, 
followed by sequential 50-bps cuts until the Selic rate reaches 
11.00% by mid-2024. The Central Bank may opt to wait a bit 
more (until September) before start cutting rates, to be more 
confident that the inflation outlook has indeed improved. 
 
However, expansionary bias from fiscal policy implies limited 
room for interest rate cuts. As pointed out in the Fiscal session 
above, the new framework allows expenditure to grow some 
2.5% in 2024 and 2025, after the 20% increase between 2021 
and 2023. It tends to keep final demand and debt dynamics under 
pressure, which would be consistent with a high interest rate 
scenario for the years to come.

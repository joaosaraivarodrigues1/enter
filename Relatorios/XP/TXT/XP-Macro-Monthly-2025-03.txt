The global economic environment continues to be risky – though not necessarily negative – for 
Brazil. It is too early to assess the effects of a likely global trade war. On the positive side, recent 
indicators point to a slowdown in the US, reducing the risk of rate hikes by the Fed this year.
• 
In politics, March brought three relevant points: signaling the end of the budget amendments 
imbroglio; the first steps towards a ministerial reform; and definitions on the 2025 budget vote.
• 
Brazil’s GDP increased by 3.4% in 2024, with signs of weakening in Q4. The economic slowdown 
is expected to continue, although gradually. Thus, we maintain our projection of 2.0% for 2025 
GDP growth. The expectation of 1.0% for 2026 is biased upwards.
• 
The government should have no difficulty meeting this year's primary result target, but a 
significant adjustment will still be necessary to comply with the spending limit. For 2026, we 
believe the surplus target of 0.25% of GDP will be maintained, despite the adverse scenario.
• 
The BRL has performed well this year, but risks remain. We expect the exchange rate at 6.00 reais 
per dollar for YE 2025 (previously 6.20) and 6.20 for YE 2026 (previously 6.40).
• 
We have slightly reduced our 2025 IPCA inflation forecast from 6.1% to 6.0%, reflecting the 
scenario of a less pressured exchange rate. For 2026, we kept our projection at 4.5%, amid a 
scenario of significant monetary contraction and expansionary fiscal policies.
• 
Despite easing exchange rate pressures and weaker economic activity, we still see the Selic rate 
at 15.50% by June. If the economic slowdown intensifies, the Copom may stop raising rates 
sooner than expected. Still, we do not see inflation close to the target in the foreseeable future.

Foreword – Fragile and volatile equilibrium
Last month, we argued that the positive performance of Brazilian markets at the beginning of the year was supported by 
fundamentals, but the equilibrium looked fragile. It remains the case.
First, it is still not clear that tighter monetary policy in Brazil is leading to a more balanced economy. December activity 
indicators came out softer than expected, but January and February data suggest some recovery. IPCA inflation stood a bit 
below expectations in the first bimester, but its core measures remain under pressure and wholesale price inflation rebounded, 
pointing to further pressure for consumer prices ahead.
Second, the government announced fresh measures to boost domestic demand, such as the release of FGTS funds and 
payroll loans for private sector workers. It reinforces the view that fiscal and/or quasi-fiscal policies may react if economic 
growth moderates ahead.
Third, balance of payments results are showing that the current account deficit was larger than initially expected last year, 
due to strong domestic demand boosting imports and international travel expenditures. It suggests limited room (if any) for 
the BRL to strengthen from current levels.
Last but not least, the global environment remains unstable, with uncertain effects of President Trump’s measures on several 
fronts (import tariffs, war negotiation, immigration). Consequently, spikes of global risk aversion could happen frequently, 
keeping market volatility elevated. Moreover, one should monitor the escalation of tensions between Brazilian and US 
institutions.
Against this backdrop, we have made only minor adjustments to our (conservative) macro forecasts. We have slightly reduced 
our year-end exchange rate projection to 6.00 from 6.20 reais per dollar, still anticipating some depreciation ahead. 
Accordingly, we slightly trimmed our 2025 IPCA inflation forecast, to 6.0% from 6.1%.
We kept our GDP growth forecast at 2.0% this year, with the potential impact of government stimulus measures offsetting 
the downward surprises in the latest GDP release. Our expectation for the terminal Selic rate remains at 15.50% (after 100-
75-50-bp hikes in the next three Copom meetings).

Political environment – The beginning of the 
second biennium
Paulo Gama, Bianca Lima e João Paulo Machado
The month of March will mark the beginning of the political year. 
After a month of parliamentary recess and pending issues in the 
relationship among the branches of power that led to 
slowdowns in activities, March brought three important points: 
a signal that the impasse over pork barrel policies had been 
resolved; the first steps towards a ministerial reform; and clearer 
definitions regarding the voting schedule for the 2025 public 
budget, which has been pending since last December. All three 
steps were considered necessary for the political year to truly 
begin.
Once these issues have been resolved, attention should shift to 
the two measures already announced by the government as

priorities for this year. The first is the creation of a new model 
for payroll loans for private sector workers. The second is the 
Income Tax reform, which, according to the initial design already 
presented by the economic team, will expand the Income Tax 
exemption threshold for those earning up to BRL 5,000 per

tend to be held in Congress beyond the first quarter.
Both measures are part of a context of low government approval 
ratings recorded by the main polls, whose repercussions are 
also observed in other aspects that tend to reverberate in the 
actions of the government in the coming months. These actions 
will also be discussed in a scenario of more explicit concern with 
the 2026 elections, in which the government has reorganized its 
communication and the Planalto Palace got closer to the PT 
nucleus, with the appointment of Gleisi Hoffmann, President of 
the political party until then, to the Secretariat of Institutional 
Relations, responsible for the political articulation of the 
government.
The first consequence of the drop in the approval of the 
government is the concern about food inflation. Palliative 
measures were already announced at the beginning of the 
month, but the possible persistence of relevant results could 
reignite the debate. In the same direction, and even without the 
support of the economic team, it is possible that stimulus 
measures, such as payroll loans and the release of FGTS 
payments, will continue to be suggested by government 
members – which would add difficulty to the effort that has been 
undertaken by the Ministries of Finance and Planning to 
convince the government to tolerate the possibility of GDP 
growth below that recorded in 2024.
Back on the legislative agenda, March may also bring 
discussions on presidential vetoes that are pending analysis by 
Congress – something that may be postponed until the middle 
of the following month. Among the most relevant vetoes that 
could be discussed are the one that deals with the regulatory 
framework for offshore wind farms; the one that removed the 
exemption for real estate funds (FII) and agribusiness funds 
(Fiagro) from the Tax Reform regulations; and those related to 
the Propag, the new program for renegotiating state debts.

Monetary Policy – The Copom still has work 
to do
Caio Megale
As described in the sections above, we have slightly reduced 
our projection for 2025 IPCA inflation, due to a stronger BRL 
forecast. It was not enough, however, to reduce our terminal 
Selic rate forecast.
First, although lower than before, our 2025 IPCA forecast is 
twice the target (6.0% versus 3.0%). Inflation is not elevated 
only because of short-term shocks, but rather as a result of 
imbalances between aggregate supply and demand, which 
should demand a significant monetary effort to correct.
Fiscal and quasi-fiscal policies remain expansionary, which 
may be intensified with measures that the government has 
been debating. For example, the release of FGTS funds, payroll 
loans to workers of the private sector, and the income tax 
exemption for workers that make up to BRL 5,000 per month, 
among others.

• 
In Brazil, economic activity has shown weaker dynamism in recent months but is 
expected to gradually recover. We continue to forecast GDP to grow by 1.5% in 2024. 
We lifted our projection for 2025, to 2.0%, in line with the scenario of further interest 
rate cuts.
• 
We expect a public sector deficit of 2.3% of GDP in 2023. The approval of the 
government's package of measures to increase revenue reduced the expected deficit 
to 0.6% of GDP in 2024, still far from the primary balance target.
•

anticipate BRL/USD 4.70 for YE 2024. Our projection for YE 2025 stands at BRL/USD

its historical average.
• 
The benign disinflation process advances. Further global disinflation led us to cut our 
projection for the 2024 IPCA to 3.7%, given lower exchange rate and commodity 
prices. Our 2025 IPCA forecast remains at 4.0%.
• 
Stronger BRL and lower IPCA forecasts open room for the Selic rate to reach the 
neutral level earlier: we now expect 9.00% in 2024 - 100 bps below our previous 
forecast. For 2025, we see the policy rate stable at 9.00%. The expansionary bias of 
fiscal and quasi-fiscal policies remains the main risk for the monetary policy outlook.

8% since late September (see chart), driven by gasoline, 
sugar, milk, among others. We had not anticipated this fall 
in our baseline scenario, which, in turn, considered that 
commodity prices would remain stable.

In this context, and considering the recent dovish signaling from Chair Powell, we brought forward our expectation 
of when the Fed will begin its easing cycle from Q3 to Q2. We also see a somewhat weaker US dollar globally, 
particularly against EMs.
Lower commodity prices could be bad news for exporters like Brazil. But Brazilian import prices are falling even 
more than export prices, which, in turn, has been supported by higher iron ore prices. Additionally, the Brazilian 
quantum exported is ballooning, driven by record-high agriculture output in 2023 and rising oil production.
Under those circumstances, we reduced our 2024 IPCA inflation forecast to 3.7% (4.1% before) and now see a 
stronger BRL (4.70 reais to the dollar vs. 4.85 before). It opens room for the Central Bank to extend the easing 
cycle, reaching the neutral level sooner: we now expect the Selic rate to reach 9.0% in 2024 - 100 bps lower than 
before. For 2025, we see the policy rate stable at 9.0% (same as before).
We are still worried about the expansionary bias of the fiscal policy both domestically and globally. This tends to 
put pressure on inflation expectations, not only because of the impact of higher expenditures on aggregate 
demand, but also due to rising public debt over time (historically, Brazil faces higher inflation to adjust for 
deteriorating fiscal dynamics). But considering the fresh external impulse and some recent improvements on the 
revenue side (see fiscal session below) – the negative effects of unbalanced fiscal policy should appear later, most 
likely in the second half of the presidential mandate.

Francisco Nobre
The latest drop in commodity prices should favor global 
disinflation. Commodity prices engaged in a downward 
trend in 4Q23 after being roughly stable for more than one 
year. If persistent, the recent drop tends to be beneficial for 
the final stage of global consumer price disinflation. The 
CRB All Commodities Index, for instance, fell by 8% since 
late September (see chart), driven by gasoline, sugar, milk, 
among others. Still, commodity price levels remain 
historically high, which should keep terms of trade favorable 
for their exports. Going forward, we believe that energy and 
agricultural commodities should fluctuate at around current 
levels, while we attribute a downward bias to metals.

The Fed turned more dovish. The December FOMC meeting 
changed the expected path for monetary policy in 2024. The 
committee removed its hiking bias and opened the 
discussion on when rate cuts should start. Thus, we brought 
forward our forecast for the kickoff of the easing cycle to 
2Q24 from 3Q24. We now see the Fed cutting rates by a 
total of 150 bps in 2024, starting in May. This implies that 
the Fed funds rate will end next year at 4.0%, still in 
contractionary territory amid a sticky final stage of 
disinflation and a resilient labor market.
We expect the policy rate to stabilize between 3.0%-3.5% in 
2025, which we consider the new neutral level for the 
medium term (unchanged from our prior estimate). We 
believe the deteriorating US government fiscal outlook will 
lead to higher rates than those seen in the post-2008 
financial crisis period.  Our base case scenario entertains 
25-bps cuts throughout the easing cycle, although the Fed 
may opt to accelerate the pace should economic activity 
weaken more than expected.

Rodolfo Margato
Activity indicators have surprised negatively in recent 
weeks. Real service revenues fell for the third consecutive 
month in October, with a widespread worsening across 
categories. Retail sales also dropped in the month, 
frustrating recovery projections. Meanwhile, industrial 
production data confirmed the stagnation of the 
manufacturing sector.
Accordingly, our tracker for 4Q GDP – high-frequency 
estimate – points to -0.1% QoQ. This represents a 
downward bias to our official projection of a 0.1% QoQ gain 
in the period and, consequently, to the 3.0% increase 
expected for the full-year 2023 GDP. In a nutshell, recent 
data have proven the slowdown in domestic activity, with 
virtual stability of GDP in the second half following a strong 
expansion in the first half.

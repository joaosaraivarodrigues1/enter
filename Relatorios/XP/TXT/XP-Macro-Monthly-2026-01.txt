Global outlook remains uncertain for EMs, particularly after the US action in Venezuela. Still, 
market prices have remained relatively calm recently. The announcement of the Fed’s new 
Chair is another key development in the coming weeks.
• 
The October presidential elections are set to dominate political discussions in Brasília 
throughout 2026. Mounting tensions across Latin America add an additional layer of 
complexity to the political chessboard.
• 
A tight labor market and fiscal impulses should keep GDP growth close to potential. We 
forecast 2.3% in 2025 and 1.7% in 2026. Nonetheless, political uncertainties, fiscal adjustment 
measures, and still restrictive financial conditions are likely to weigh on 2027 GDP, for which 
we anticipate 1.2% growth.
• 
The government should meet its primary result targets for 2026 and 2027 with additional 
revenue-boosting measures. However, we believe that spending reforms will be essential from 
2027 onwards to ensure the sustainability of the fiscal framework and to stabilize public debt.
• 
The expected reacceleration of GDP this year, together with lower commodity prices, should 
keep the current account deficit elevated, at 3.0% of GDP. In 2027, softer domestic demand is 
likely to curb imports of goods and services, bringing the deficit down to 2.4% of GDP.
• 
High risk premia and current account deficit will prevent a further exchange rate appreciation. 
We forecast 5.60 reais per dollar in YE 2026 and 5.80 in YE 2027.
• 
We have lowered our projection for the 2026 IPCA inflation from 4.2% to 4.0%, in line with well-
behaved wholesale prices and lower oil prices. We also forecast inflation of 4.0% in 2027, 
reflecting the persistent de-anchoring of medium-term expectations.
• 
The central bank should start a monetary easing cycle in March. We see five consecutive 50-
bp cuts until the Selic rate reaches 12.50%, followed by a pause for evaluation. In a scenario 
of insufficient fiscal adjustment in 2027, we see limited room for additional cuts, to 11.00%.

Foreword – Looking through 2026
Every January, we present for the first time our forecasts for the year “t+1” (2027 this time around). It is normally difficult to 
make forecasts two years in advance in a volatile emerging market economy like Brazil. The 2026 presidential elections – that 
will probably be as tight as it can be – make this exercise even more challenging.
Global outlook, which has been positive for EMs, is reaching new levels of uncertainty after the US actions in Venezuela. Despite 
all the noise and discussions, market prices haven’t changed much.
In Brazil, the outlook for 2026 seems a relatively predictable one. Economic activity is losing steam gradually, and inflation is 
trending down, helped by global disinflation (energy, food) and stronger BRL. Despite some depreciation in December, the 
Brazilian currency closed 2025 10% up against the USD.
That scenario will allow the Central Bank to start a monetary easing cycle this year, most likely in March. The monetary authority 
will remain cautious to ensure that current inflation and inflationary expectations continue to converge to the 3.0% target. We 
see the Selic rate at 12.50%, after five consecutive 50-bp cuts.
Fiscal policy also tends to face a relatively calm 2026. The government’s targets for this year and next are not very challenging, 
especially after the approval of the bill proposal reducing tax benefits (Comp. law No. 224/25). The debt-to-GDP ratio will keep 
rising, probably by another 3-4 pp of GDP. But this is “priced in”. More important will be the perspective of fiscal reforms for the 
coming administration.
Fiscal reforms should indeed take center stage during the presidential election campaign. A reformist approach, that aims to 
curb expenditure growth over time, should lead to a more optimistic scenario in 2027 and onwards (lower interest rates, 
predictable tax system, inflation under control). A populist approach, in turn, may lead to fiscal crisis and higher inflation at 
some time in the future, as public debt would keep rising.
Our base case scenario lies in the middle. Some measures will probably be taken to curb spending growth – but not enough to 
provide fiscal sustainability. This scenario of “insufficient adjustment” may work for a while, depending on the economic policy 
team that takes office in 2027. In any event, this would mean an unsustainable equilibrium.

BRAZIL – 2027 reforms gain relevance 
amid positive short-term outlook
Political environment – 2026 begins with 
elections on the radar, and attention focused 
on the crisis in Venezuela
Paulo Gama and XP Politics
The political year of 2026 begins focused on the presidential 
elections and the implications for Brazil of the US military 
action on Venezuela.
On the electoral front, attention remains on Flávio Bolsonaro's 
pre-candidacy and the senator's ability to demonstrate 
whether he can be a competitive name in the race for the 
Planalto Palace. We expect increasing public exposure in the 
coming months, with the senator presenting himself as his 
father’s successor on the one hand, while seeking signals of 
greater moderation than the former President on the other. 
Rejection rates — the main obstacle to a potential second 
round – should be the primary variable observed by Brasília 
and is expected to dictate the course of other center-right 
parties for the election.
Meanwhile, President Lula has recovered his approval rating 
since the tariffs imposed by Donald Trump. He now enters the 
re-election year with competitive popularity levels — even 
though the inclusion of public security in the debate agenda 
has shown limits to this recovery.
It remains to be seen whether the public policies to be 
implemented by the Planalto Palace in 2026 will be sufficient 
to force a resumption of Lula's growth curve or if the resistance 
represented by the debate over violence will impose a more 
prolonged period of attrition for the President. Regarding the 
outcome of the presidential race, the trend is for the scenario 
to remain open until the final stretch of the election.
January will also mark the initial effects of the renewed Income 
Tax framework, following the expansion of the tax base 
exemption for monthly earnings up to BRL 5,000. The 
government sees the tax reform, which also reduced the 
burden for those earning up to BRL 7,350, as its main electoral 
tool – reinforcing the speech of tax justice and the fight against 
privileges. The latter is supported by the increase in dividend 
taxation, which also composed the reform.
The reform is likely to boost perceived income level, but the 
government's approval rating depends on other variables, such 
as the cost of living and others that may overshadow the tax 
change. Beyond the effect on the President's popularity, 
markets will monitor the risk of renewed fiscal expansion 
policies, despite current fiscal rules and electoral legislation.

Brazilian economy slowed down in 2025. Domestic activity lost 
steam throughout last year, driven by contractionary monetary 
policy and elevated indebtedness of firms and households. In 
addition, fiscal policy contributed less to economic growth, 
especially in the first half. By contrast, sectors less sensitive to 
the economic cycle — agriculture and extractive industry — 
expanded strongly, reflecting the record grain harvest and 
higher oil output. Overall, we estimate that GDP grew 2.3% last 
year, following four consecutive years of growth above 3.0%. 
Excluding the contribution of the two primary sectors 
mentioned above, GDP would have increased by 1.4% in 2025, 
substantially below the 3.6% rise in 2024.

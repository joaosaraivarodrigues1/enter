Geopolitical tensions and political noise across several countries - especially in the United 
States - marked 2025. Even so, the global economy showed resilience. We see heightened 
risks ahead, particularly due to fiscal deterioration in developed economies.
• 
The government is working to secure approval of the 2026 budget bill amid rising institutional 
tensions among the branches of power. At the same time, attention remains on President 
Lula’s approval ratings, who seeks to strengthen his narrative and electoral positioning.
• 
Economic activity has been cooling, in line with contractionary monetary policy. That said, we 
have raised our projection for 2025 GDP growth, from 2.1% to 2.3%, due to upward revisions 
in Agriculture & Livestock data earlier this year. We kept our forecast of 1.7% for 2026 GDP.
• 
We anticipate that the government will meet the primary balance target this year and is close 
to reaching the lower bound of the primary target range next year. Despite that, public debt 
should continue to rise, reaching 83.7% of GDP in 2026.
• 
Our FX forecast remains at 5.30 reais per dollar for 2025 YE and 5.50 for 2026 YE. Strong 
exports and FDI should offset high levels of imports and primary income deficit in external 
accounts. However, the expected reacceleration in domestic demand is a risk for next year.
• 
Prices of food and industrialized goods continue to slow down. We have reduced our 
projection for the 2025 IPCA inflation from 4.5% to 4.3%. For 2026, we continue to project 4.2%.
• 
Slowing economic activity and benign current inflation suggest that monetary policy may 
become less contractionary. We maintain our projection that the Copom will begin a gradual 
monetary easing cycle in March. However, we do not rule out an earlier start in January.

Foreword – Calm before the volatility?
The Brazilian economy had a good performance in 2025. GDP grew around 2.0%, in line with expectations, cooling 
compared to the previous years. The unemployment rate nears an all-time low. IPCA Inflation should end the year much 
lower than expected, mainly due to BRL appreciation. Our 2025 IPCA forecast in January was 6.1%. Now, we believe it will 
close at 4.3%.
Solid economic activity kept tax revenues at a good clip, enabling the government to meet its fiscal target. Exports grew 
briskly, even amid higher tariffs from the US, which prevented the current account deficit from deteriorating further (after 
having widened considerably in 2024).
Those sound macro indicators, along with a benign global environment, boosted Brazilian asset prices in recent months.  
Given the aforementioned better inflation dynamics, we believe the central bank will find room to reduce the Selic rate next 
year.
Beneath the surface, however, imbalances persist. Disinflation was uneven, concentrated in durable goods and foodstuff. 
Services and non-durable inflation remain at high levels, mirroring the tight spare capacity of the economy. Looking ahead, 
we see limited room for further disinflation, considering that i) the government has announced several growth-supportive 
measures for 2026, and ii) the global environment may not favor another round of appreciation in EM currencies (see 
Global Backdrop session below).
On the fiscal front, meeting the targets is proving not enough to stabilize the public debt-to-GDP ratio. Even with revenue 
at record-high levels, the primary deficit remains close to 0.5% of GDP. Adding financial expenditures, the nominal deficit 
will reach around 8.0% at YE 2025, among the highest in the world.
Thus, we believe that more important than the macroeconomic results in 2026 – which will probably continue to be 
relatively positive – will be the perspective of macroeconomic reforms that will be conducted by the election winner.
Throughout 2026, the short-term calm may give way to market volatility as local drivers become more relevant than global 
forces.

BRAZIL – Activity cools, but Copom 
remains cautious
Political environment – Amid heightened 
tensions among the branches of power, the 
administration seeks to secure approval of 
the 2026 Budget Bill
Paulo Gama and XP Politics
December is expected to be marked by the administration’s 
efforts to secure approval of the 2026 Budget Bill. This takes 
place against a backdrop of deepening institutional tension 
among the branches of power. It also occurs at a moment 
when attention remains focused on President Lula’s approval 
ratings.
Brazil’s inclusion in the tariff package imposed by President 
Donald Trump had initially created an opportunity for the 
opposition. Their strategy to capitalize on the issue, however, 
was unsuccessful. Following this episode, President Lula 
regained the positive approval levels seen last year. Several 
factors contributed to this rebound, including: (i) the easing of 
inflation, (ii) progress on the Income Tax reform proposal – a 
structural tax reform under discussion in Congress, and (iii) the 
absence of a strong opposition figure since Tarcísio de Freitas 
stepped back from the national political scene.
That upward trend was interrupted after the political fallout 
from a police operation conducted in Rio de Janeiro. The event 
had a negative impact on the President. The key question now 
is whether Lula’s approval ratings will resume their upward 
trajectory. Such a rebound would signal greater resilience 
among voters. Alternatively, the opposition may succeed in 
keeping the issue alive in the public arena over the coming 
weeks.
During this period, the administration has worked to 
reintroduce topics that reinforce its narrative and electoral 
positioning. Lula will benefit from the approval of the Income 
Tax exemption for individuals earning up to BRL 5,000. This 
was highlighted in his national address in late November. The 
measure is expected to anchor the government's campaign 
next year.
In Congress, December will be shaped by developments in the 
ongoing tensions among the branches of power. Several 
recent episodes illustrate this dynamic. The Speaker of the 
Chamber of Deputies, Hugo Motta, has broken with the leader 
of the Workers’ Party (PT) in the Chamber. The President of the 
Senate, Davi Alcolumbre, has expressed dissatisfaction with 
the nomination of Jorge Messias to the Supreme Federal 
Court (STF). More recently, Justice Gilmar Mendes of the STF 
ruled that only the Office of the Prosecutor-General (PGR –

Amid this tense environment, the opposition sees a renewed 
window of opportunity. It aims to intensify pressure around 
proposals such as the amnesty for individuals involved in the 
events of January 8th. The debate gains further relevance as 
former president Jair Bolsonaro is currently serving his 
sentence at the Federal Police Superintendency in Brasília. 
This issue could strengthen the strategy of Bolsonaro-aligned 
groups that wish to preserve the former president’s influence 
over the 2026 elections. It may also delay a decision regarding 
whom he will endorse for next year’s race.
Against this backdrop, the administration is focusing on the 
revenue-raising measures still under review in Congress. 
These proposals are essential for generating an additional BRL 
30 billion in revenue to ensure meeting the formal primary 
balance target in 2026.
The first measure expands taxation on online betting 
platforms, raises the Income Tax rate on interest on equity 
(JCP – juros sobre capital próprio, a Brazilian mechanism for 
remunerating shareholders), and increases the Social 
Contribution on Net Profit (CSLL) applied to fintechs. The bill is 
sponsored by Senator Renan Calheiros. It also includes a 
provision extending the deadline for distributing tax-exempt 
dividends until April 2026. This stands in contrast to the 
deadline set under the new Income Tax law, which is 
December 2025. The Senate has approved the proposal, but 
the Chamber of Deputies is expected to pose greater 
resistance, particularly regarding higher taxes on betting 
platforms and fintechs.
The package also includes the bill establishing a linear 10% 
reduction in tax expenditures. The revenue generated from this 
measure is crucial for the government’s plan to meet the 2026 
primary surplus target. However, the proposal remains far 
from a consensus. The administration continues to negotiate 
with congressional leadership to refine the text.
Despite the challenges, the government does not intend to 
revise the fiscal target. The use of additional regulatory 
measures (infralegal measures — i.e., measures implemented 
without legislative approval) to close the fiscal gap remains a 
possibility.

Budget execution should face fewer constraints in 2026.

on tax compensation (Law No. 15,265/25) allied to the

means that the government would still need additional revenue 
or spending cuts of BRL 12.8 billion to reach the lower bound of

Lower pressure on the spending side as well. The increase of

inclusion of the high school students benefit program (“pé de

the likelihood of new spending blocks. In fact, our estimates 
indicate room for increased discretionary expenditures if the 
government manages to boost revenue.

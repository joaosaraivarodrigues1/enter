Foreword – Pulling the brakes
The Brazilian Central Bank’s task is becoming more challenging. Inflation and inflation expectations remain on the rise, pushed 
by tight spare capacity in the economy, FX depreciation, and higher domestic food prices. Our IPCA forecasts are above the 
target range for 2024 and 2025. If the BCB´s willingness to bring inflation to the target in 2026 is for real – and we believe it 
is – monetary policy response has to be (even) more impetuous.
Thus, we now see the Copom stepping up the tightening pace again to 100 bps in December. We have also lifted our terminal 
Selic rate to 14.25% from 13.25%. We believe the Copom will prefer to be bolder in the short turn in order to put the train back 
on track in a timely fashion rather than trying to smooth the cycle.
A fast approval in Congress of the measures to curb mandatory expenses should also help to tame market volatility and 
inflation expectations.
Fiscal policy could be more helpful, but the expenditures will continue to grow at least 2.5% above inflation with or without 
the fiscal measures. Moreover, we see quasi-fiscal measures gaining momentum as the 2026 elections approach.
In the report, we present our 2026 forecasts for the first time. The main hypothesis is that the more restrictive economic 
policy (tighter monetary policy and less expansionary fiscal policy) will allow the economy to rebalance. In this scenario, we 
see IPCA inflation coming back to the target range and the Copom gradually and carefully cutting rates. Domestic demand 
tends to lose steam through 2025 and 2026, but GDP growth will remain positive, particularly sustained by record-high 
agriculture output.
On the currency, considering that the FX is already weak vis-à-vis its fundamentals - terms of trade, purchasing parity, interest 
rate differentials – we see the parity BRLUSD moving roughly sideways in nominal terms in 2025 and 2026, amid considerable 
volatility created by both domestic and external reasons.

economic activity (and inflation) should reduce the pace of 
revenue growth. On the other hand, higher inflation in 2025 will 
lead to a stronger rise of the spending limit for 2026. With this 
combination, we estimate a deficit of BRL 114.2 billion (0.8%

raising measures for 2026's budget.
States results improve, but municipalities register a record-
high deficit. While states have accumulated a surplus of 
almost BRL 35 billion this year, municipalities have 
accumulated a deficit of BRL 16.6 billion, the worst result for 
the period in the historical series. This result causes concern, 
because any imbalance in subnational entities may require 
new financial aid from the Union. Finally, we estimate a deficit 
of BRL 33.0 billion in 2024 (0.3% of GDP), BRL 76.5 billion in 
2025 (0.6% of GDP) and BRL 93.4 billion (0.7% of GDP) in 2026 
for the consolidated public sector.
Debt is expected to keep rising in the years to come. Despite 
the recent improvement, the primary balance for this and the 
next few years will not be enough to guarantee stabilization of 
the public debt. Moreover, we see additional pressure on 
nominal interest costs due to the increase in the Selic rate (see 
monetary policy section) and the risk premium. For this reason, 
we estimate that the ratio of general government gross debt to 
GDP should reach 78.0% in 2024, 82.0% in 2025 and 85.6% in 
2026. Public sector net debt, meanwhile, is expected to grow 
more moderately thanks to currency devaluation. We project 
that the DLSP/GDP ratio will end this year at 62.7% and next 
year at 66.0% in 2025 and 68.9% in 2026.

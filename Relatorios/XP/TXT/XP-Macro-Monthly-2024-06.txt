Foreword – Will ends meet?
We did not make substantial changes to our Brazilian economic forecast this month. We still see growth at around potential 
this year and in 2025, inflation should remain above the 3.0% target but below the upper bound of the target range (4.5%), the 
BRL should behave similarly to other EM currencies, and the terminal Selic rate will probably be a bit above what we previously 
forecasted (10.50% vs 10.00%).
Despite those minor changes to the baseline forecasts, some underlying trends raise questions about whether the Brazilian 
economy is indeed balanced.
Government expenditures are growing well above inflation, and this trend will continue in the years to come. The fiscal deficit 
remains stubbornly high despite astonishingly strong tax revenue growth. The debt-to-GDP rate is on the rise, with no signs 
of stabilization.
It has nothing to do with the need to help Rio Grande do Sul state overcome the dramatic effects of the recent flooding. As 
was the case during the pandemic, government programs focused on the event are more than welcome. But they should not 
be an excuse to overspend in other areas (this risk remains).
On the back of expansionary fiscal policy, labor market is getting increasingly heated. This is good news but may not be 
consistent with a neutral monetary policy and stable inflation. Will the central bank have the tools to act when (and if) inflation 
surpasses the target range?
Another relevant concern, globally this time around, is the escalation of geopolitical tensions and of political populism. This 
is not our specialty, but it will certainly impact the economic balance between relevant regions of the world.
These and other potential imbalances keep volatility high and increase the challenge of anticipating market and economic 
trends. Still, we try our best in this report to shed some light on possible paths ahead for Brazil.

Monetary policy challenges have increased since the last 
Copom meeting. Inflation expectations rose - drifting further 
away from the 3.0% target – due to averse global scenario, 
domestic fiscal uncertainties, tight labor market and credibility 
issues from the central bank. This means that monetary policy 
has to be somewhat tighter than previously thought in order to 
keep inflation close to the target in the BCB´s model.
The importance of a consensus. Another split vote, particularly 
if it mirrors the 5-4 score seen in the last meeting, would be a 
negative outcome and could affect the Copom’s credibility. It 
would be reasonable to try to achieve a more consensual 
agreement. It looks more likely that the Copom should build a 
consensus around a pause now, and set a “wait and see” 
approach going forward, considering that i) Copom’s members 
seem to unanimously agree that it is important to react to 
rising inflation expectations and ii) the more orthodox 
members of the board will probably vote for a pause.

policy approach.

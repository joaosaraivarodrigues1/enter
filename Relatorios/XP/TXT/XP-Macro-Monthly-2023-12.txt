Foreword – External accounts to remain solid, fiscal accounts to 
remain uncertain
The Brazilian economy performed relatively well in 2023. GDP is poised to grow almost three times the initial 
expectations, consumer inflation fell more than expected, the BRL strengthened, and the trade surplus is 
reaching all-time highs. The good performance stemmed from the combination of domestic and global factors 
(see more in our year-ahead report here).
Looking ahead, we still see the global environment as positive for Brazil, with developed economies cutting 
interest rates and commodity prices remaining at current elevated levels.
On the local front, the outlook is more challenging. The primary fiscal deficit in 2023 will be almost twice as large 
as initially expected, spurring doubts about fiscal sustainability. Indeed, achieving the consolidation path 
announced by the fiscal framework seems less likely by the day.
The economy is decelerating, posing an additional risk for tax collection. Will the government be willing to cut 
expenditures when it becomes clear that the fiscal adjustment is in jeopardy? With economic growth losing 
steam? Hard to say, but possibly not.
Theoretically and empirically (in the case of Brazil), fiscal imbalances put pressure on inflation expectations. 
Thus, it is likely that medium-term IPCA forecasts will remain above the 3.0% target. Will the central bank be 
willing to slow down – and ultimately interrupt – the easing cycle if the inflation target is in jeopardy? Probably 
yes, even considering that pressures on the Committee tend to increase throughout the year.
Thus, despite seeing ample room for the Copom to cut rates in the short run, we forecast the easing cycle ending 
with the Selic rate at 10.00% – above market forecasts – by mid-next year. Political pressure tend to increase 
again, bringing volatility to asset prices.
Having said that, global conditions will likely be a cushion for Brazil. Despite ongoing uncertainties, commodity 
prices should remain elevated, and developed markets’ central banks will probably change gears in monetary 
policy, starting a gradual easing cycle by around midyear. Thus, we see the Brazilian Real hovering around 
current levels, or even strengthening somewhat.
In sum, we see weaker economic growth, lower inflation, and relatively stable FX in the months to come, which 
should keep doors open for the Central Bank to reduce interest rates. Fiscal uncertainties, however, remain 
elevated and may limit policy stimulus next year. Thus, political pressure on economic policy tends to increase, 
bringing some market volatility. But we do not expect a deterioration in economic fundamentals or asset prices.

The recent improvement is not enough to reverse the trend. 
In October, federal revenue grew 0.1% YoY after five 
months of decline. This largely reflected the revenue-
raising measures taken by the government, as well as non-
recurring events. As a result, the central government 
recorded a surplus of BRL 18.3 billion, while the public

The 2023 deficit estimate has increased considerably. Our 
estimate last month was for a primary deficit of BRL 123 
billion (1.2%). Since then: 1) the government postponed to 
2024 the inflow of BRL 15 billion related to judicial deposits 
in favor of the Union at Caixa Econômica Federal (CEF); 2) 
expenses related to minimum spending on health and high 
school programs increased by BRL 10.3 billion outside the

incorporated the recent results, which reduced revenue by 
BRL 4.5 billion. These factors alone would already raise the 
expected deficit to BRL 152.8 billion (1.4% of GDP).
The primary deficit should reach 2.5% of GDP with the 
settlement of court-ordered debts. The Supreme Court

debts from 2022 to 2024, as well as the amount that

outside the restrictions imposed by the spending limit and 
primary result target. We believe that the accumulated 
liabilities will be paid later this year, leading to a worsening 
in the primary balance. Considering that the appropriation 
of funds from the PIS/Pasep fund (BRL 26 billion) was not 
included in the Central Bank's statistics, we expect a deficit 
of BRL 274.5 billion (2.5% of GDP) in the below-the-line 
result.

Monetary Policy – Reinforced view: positive in 
the short term; risks further ahead
Caio Megale
Recent developments reinforced the view for Brazilian 
monetary policy that we have been defending for a while: 
comfortable room for easing in the short term, and 
limitations over 2024.
Inflation and growth are heading south. Consumer price 
inflation has been falling gradually but consistently, for both 
supply and demand reasons. Economic activity is losing 
steam, reinforcing the disinflationary trend ahead. 
Considering that monetary policy is still quite restrictive 
with the Selic rate at 12.25%, we see no reason for the 
Copom to consider reducing the pace of interest rate cuts, 
let alone interrupting the easing cycle anytime soon.
Fiscal policy expansionary bias reinforced.  News flow on 
the fiscal front suggested an effort to increase spending 
through new government programs or to reduce the 
enforcement on cutting expenses if the fiscal target is at 
risk. The government is also facing headwinds in Congress 
to approve the proposed measures to boost revenues. 
Those signals reinforce the uncertainty about the 
sustainability of fiscal accounts.

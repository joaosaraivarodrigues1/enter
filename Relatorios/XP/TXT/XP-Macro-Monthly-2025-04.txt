Foreword – Activity takes center stage. Here, there, and everywhere
Those able to accurately predict economic activity in the coming months will most likely get market movements right.
Globally, the hottest debate is whether the ballooning political uncertainty arising from much higher tariffs announced in 
the US and geopolitical tensions will push the world economy into a recession. The effects of this scenario on the US dollar, 
commodity prices, and emerging market currencies are difficult to predict.
Activity in China is also key for EM and Brazil. The economy has been weakening since much before Trump’s election. 
Chinese authorities introduced a significant amount of stimulus, but household consumption remains fragile. Trump’s 
trade war could hit the only Chinese engine still running briskly: exports.
In Brazil, the pace of economic deceleration in response to tight monetary policy has been the most frequently asked 
question among market participants. The signs are mixed so far, although the economy is definitely softer than last year. 
Moreover, recent government measures on credit and tax exemptions could dilute the impact of high interest rates.
We stand on the more hawkish camp on that: we see the economy losing steam ahead, but still growing at around potential. 
Thus, the output gap should remain in the inflationary camp, demanding caution on monetary policy.
The Copom will most likely interrupt the tightening cycle with the Selic rate within the 15.00-15.50% interval. But, if we are 
right on growth, it won’t be enough to bring inflation back to the target range in 2026.
After incorporating recent stimulus measures, we raised our GDP growth forecast for 2025 to 2.3% from 2.0%; and for 
2026 to 1.5% from 1.0%. And we lifted our IPCA inflation forecast for 2026 – the current monetary policy relevant horizon 
– to 4.7% from 4.5%.

BRAZIL – Activity takes center stage in

Political environment – Government sending 
the income tax reform to Congress
Paulo Gama, Bianca Lima e João Paulo Machado
April begins with a focus on the programs presented by the 
government in the context of President Lula's falling popularity 
since the beginning of the year.
At the end of March, what has been considered the main bet 
of the Planalto Palace for the attempt to win the elections in 
2026 arrived in Congress – the reform of the Income Tax, 
which aims to exempt those who earn up to BRL 5,000, with 
partial exemption in the range between BRL 5,000 and BRL 
7,000. The compensation presented for the fiscal waiver was 
the imposition of a minimum effective tax rate on those with 
income above BRL 600,000 per year, with progressive taxation 
of up to 10% for those earning BRL 1.2 million or more per year. 
To this end, there will be a withholding tax of 10% on dividends, 
with a reduction linked to the effective tax already collected by 
the company. The government also proposed the exclusion of 
income from exempt securities from the amount on which the 
minimum effective tax rate is charged - but these investments 
will be considered in the previous stage, to determine the 
income and the respective tax rate to be charged.
The government has used its own polls to argue that there is 
massive popular support for the narrative of charging the 
richest to pay for the exemption for the poorest.
Although there is also support among congressmen for 
expanding the exemption threshold and it seems difficult for 
Congress to oppose the narrative of the proposal presented by 
the government, the time required to discuss the matter is not 
expected to be short. There is an understanding that the 
importance given to the text by the government creates a 
positive environment for negotiations, but there is also 
resistance from sectors that will now have higher taxes than 
they currently do. In this context, the expectation in Congress 
is that the processing of the text will continue throughout 2025.
The risk during this period is that the compensation suggested 
by the government will end up being relaxed and that, in its 
place, alternative sources will be presented – with less security 
about the potential for revenue collection. The economic team 
has been working to avoid an outcome in this direction. 
Attention may also be paid to sectors in which Congress could 
impose compensation if it softens the government's proposal 
to tax the richest.

The proposal includes a full tax exemption for those who earn 
up to BRL 5,000 and a partial exemption up to BRL 7,000 - offset

No discussion on changing the 2026 fiscal target. In April, the 
government should submit the 2026 Budget Guidelines Bill 
(PLDO, in Portuguese) to Congress, setting the primary balance

on revenue increases until the budget proposal is presented in 
August. As noted earlier, approximately BRL 110 billion in 
additional revenue will be required to meet the target in 2026. 
The government is likely to include another revenue package in 
the budget, similar to previous years. This highlights the absence 
of structural fiscal consolidation: budgets start unbalanced and 
rely on extraordinary revenues to meet targets because 
expenditure growth exceeds what is necessary for structural 
adjustment. As we have argued recently, breaking this cycle will 
require structural reforms and changes to the spending limit 
growth parameters.

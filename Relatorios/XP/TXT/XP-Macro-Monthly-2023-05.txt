Foreword – Fiscal challenges remain amid still benign 
global outlook
Despite the uncertainties, particularly in the US banking system, we see the global economy getting
more becoming for Brazil. US and Europe will probably not hike rates much further. Recession, if
controlled, tends do be positive news, as it helps to bring inflation down (bear in mind that Brazil has
quite a limited trade flow with developed economies). China and India, in its turn, are recovering, which
should provide support for soft commodity and energy prices.
Domestically, though, challenges continue. The government sent the new fiscal framework to Congress,
but the bill lacks the enforcement needed to balance the budget. Minister Haddad has emphasized that
the government will increase revenues by reducing tax benefits for the rich. It is not clear if he will
succeed. We believe, ceteris paribus, tax collections will increase somewhat, though not sufficient to
stabilize debt-to-GDP dynamics.
But we are not in a “ceteris paribus” world. Economic activity is losing steam – though by less than
expected - which has already hit tax collections. Indeed, recent fiscal results came out below
expectations. This means that if the new fiscal framework passes Congress without changes, fiscal
policy will put pressure on inflation in the years to come.
Thus, we see only limited room for the central bank to ease monetary policy in the coming quarters. As
global inflation is falling and domestic activity is decelerating, there is no need for monetary policy to
remain at the same tight level it was at the beginning of the year (around 8.0% real interest rates). So,
we continue to see the BCB starting a gradual easing cycle in August, but with a terminal Selic rate still
at double digits - 11.0% by the first semester of 2024.
Against this backdrop, we anticipate that tensions between monetary and fiscal policy will continue
going forward. Whether it will reflect on Brazilian assets’ risk premium will depend on whether the global
outlook continues to be benign, as described above.

Tail risks persist, although a mind recession in the US is the base 
case. The risk of a “no landing” scenario in which the US economy 
would not decelerate, and inflation would not converge to the 
target seems increasingly unlikely. Activity indicators have been 
deteriorating, pointing to below-trend growth, and are consistent 
with a recession in the US. Meanwhile, concerns of a financial 
crisis persist, although it does not appear to be a systematic issue. 
Our base case scenario suggests that inflation dynamics 
converge to the 2-3% range amid a moderate recession. 
Recession risk and stabilizing monetary policy have partially 
reverted the USD´s recent appreciation (graph).
We believe the Fed has paused the tightening cycle. The Fed hiked 
rates by 25 bps in May, leaving doors open for upcoming 
meetings. This decision took the upper bound of the Fed Funds 
rate to 5.25%, which we consider sufficiently restrictive to 
guarantee the convergence of inflation. Despite market pricing 
suggesting rate cuts in the second half of 2023, we believe 
authorities are unlikely to ease monetary policy this year. Core 
inflation has remained significantly pressures, especially on the 
services side, and the labor market remains tight. These 
developments will likely keep the Fed hawkish for some time. We 
expect the FOMC to gradually start cutting rates in 1Q24, ending 
the year with the Fed Funds rate at 3.5%. Real interest rates will 
likely return to its neutral level in 2025 (around 0.5%), although the 
nominal level will largely depend on where inflation stabilizes.
The ECB will stay hawkish for longer. The ECB has continued its 
tightening cycle by increasing its three key interest rates by 25 
bps. Cor inflation in the Eurozone has shown no relief yet, keeping 
policymakers hawkish. ECB officials have said they expect to 
continue tightening monetary policy until inflation dynamics show 
clear signs of convergence toward the 2% target. We expect the 
ECB to hike another 50-75 bps before ending the tightening cycle,

Latam: tightening cycles are ending, but easing should take a 
while. Rate cuts are expected at different times later this year or 
early in 2024, but the path toward more normal rates (neutrality) 
will likely be longer than expected amid persistently high inflation. 
Brazil, Chile, and Peru already ended their tightening cycle last year 
or earlier this one (in the case of Peru), while Mexico and Colombia 
are close to reaching their terminal rates. Also, different points in 
the business cycle and the disinflation process will likely make 
central banks start cutting rates at different times. Uruguay was 
the first country in the region to begin cutting rates in a cautious 
manner. Brazil was one of the first in the region to begin raising 
rates and should find room to start cutting in Q3, also cautiously. 
We believe Chile and Peru will be the next ones to kick off their 
own easing cycles in 3Q23, while Colombia and Mexico may have 
to wait a bit longer.
Brazil: In pursuit of additional revenue
Economic Activity – We raised our forecast for 
2023 GDP growth, to 1.4% from 1.0%
Activity indicators have surprised positively in recent months. The 
labor market showed resilience in early 2023, with emphasis on 
the significant net addition of formal jobs (around 430k in 1Q, 
discounting seasonal effects). Furthermore, service activities 
have grown above expectations, while agricultural production 
estimates have improved further. Our updated projection for 1Q23 
GDP growth stands at 1.1% QoQ (2.9% YoY) – we were 
anticipating an increase of 0.8% a month earlier. This expectation 
is mainly due to the strong recovery of Agriculture GDP, boosted 
by the soybean crop. Total GDP would grow by 0.3% QoQ if it were 
not for the contribution of the primary sector. 1Q23 GDP results 
will be published on June 1st.
Household consumption and exports continue to play a major 
role. As we argued in our latest monthly report, real household 
disposable income will rise substantially in 2023 (4.2% as per our 
forecasts), on the heels of rising wages and higher government 
cash transfers. Regarding the latter, the expansion of the “Bolsa-
Família” welfare program is the highlight, given the amount of 
nearly BRL 170 billion this year. Income impulses will soften the 
slowdown in household consumption going forward. Moreover, 
Brazilian exports are expected to increase sharply, owing to the 
solid external demand and high commodity prices. In this context, 
we call attention to the material contribution from sectors less 
sensitive to the economic cycle, such as the agriculture and 
mining industries.
Therefore, we revised upwards our projection for 2023 GDP 
growth, to 1.4% from 1%.
We continue to anticipate a weakening of domestic activity 
throughout the year. The positive surprises in recent months do 
not change our view that the economy will lose momentum in 
coming quarters. Financial conditions remain tight, while the

Fiscal Policy – Fiscal balance to remain 
negative even with additional revenues
Recent data show a deterioration in fiscal results. Federal tax 
collection continues to slow down, driven by tax cuts 
implemented last year, the fall in international commodity prices 
and a cooling economic activity. On the expenditure side, there 
was a significant increase in the Bolsa-Familia cash-transfer 
program and discretionary expenses. In 12 months, the public 
sector surplus dropped from BLR 93.2 billion (0.9%) in February to 
BRL 74.8 billion (0.7%) in March.
We project the negative trend to continue. Revenues should 
improve with recent government measures, but not to the point of 
reversing the recent slowdown trend. Expenses, driven by the 
Transition PEC, should grow by almost 7.0% in real terms 
compared to last year. Thus, we maintain our forecast of a BRL 
119.6 billion deficit for 2023 (-1.1% of GDP).
Symmetric risks ahead. The effectiveness of government 
measures on revenues is still uncertain since some of them need 
to be discussed in the judiciary and/or in Congress. The final 
impact can be significantly higher or lower than we currently 
estimate. On the expenditure side, doubts remain regarding the 
“speed limit” for next year (that will be established by the new 
fiscal framework) and the definitions of minimum-wage increase 
and civil servants' salaries policies.
The new fiscal rule was formally presented to Congress. The 
proposal combines a real spending growth limit between 0.6% 
and 2.5% and primary balance targets. As it was presented, the 
primary balance targets are not binding constraints for 
expenditures to grow. It reduces the effectiveness of the rule as a 
fiscal anchor for the medium term.

Public debt on the rise. After a mild drop in January, the general 
government's gross debt rose back to 73.0% in March. We expect 
the debt to continue an upward trend throughout this and next 
year. It is important to point out that, in the absence of additional 
measures to increase collections in the coming years, we do not 
foresee debt stabilization.
Fiscal rule and tax reform are key fiscal themes for Congress. Our 
base hypothesis is that the proposal for a new fiscal rule will be 
approved with minor adjustments. The discussion on the tax 
reform should start next, but its progress is uncertain given the 
pressures from companies and sectors. The approval of the 
proposals is essential for sustainable and stronger growth ahead.

Deceleration of annual inflation (mainly) due to the base 
effect. The March IPCA cooled down to 4.65% year-on-year 
(compared to 5.60% in February), below our estimates and market 
consensus. The drop is related to the base effect (tax cuts during 
the election period) and global cost deflation. Core measures and 
services inflation remains under pressure. 
 
Underlying services, the subgroup most linked to economic 
activity and labor-intensive items, remained stable at 5.0% (3M 
SAAR). In fact, the April mid-month consumer inflation IPCA-15 
showed an acceleration in this group. For now, it is hard to say 
whether this indicates a trend change or a temporary rise. 
 
In fact, economic activity and the labor market have been more 
resilient than expected, in line with our upward revision for the 
2023 GDP (see the activity section above). As of now, we project 
a slight deceleration in the services metric from 7.6% in March to 
6.2% at the end of 2023, which increases the challenge for the 
monetary authority to bring inflation toward the target.

External Sector: Global environment brings 
appreciation bias to the BRL
The Brazilian exchange rate has strengthened (moderately) in 
recent weeks, led by external factors. The pair fluctuated around 
BRL/USD 5.20 in the last quarter. In our view, the recent FX 
appreciation largely reflected the global weakening of the US 
dollar, amid still high commodity prices and the prospect of less 
pressured interest rates in the US. Fiscal and political 
uncertainties in the domestic environment seem to act in the 
opposite direction.     
 
We maintain our exchange rate forecast of BRL/USD 5.30 for YE 
2023, with an annual average of BRL/USD 5.15. There is a 
downward bias (stronger Real) if the US dollar devaluation trend 
persists.   
 
The balance of payments remains robust, with emphasis on the 
strong trade surplus and massive FDI (Foreign Direct Investment). 
The Brazilian trade balance continues on an upward trajectory, 
driven by the sharp increase in exports. The grain harvest is at an 
all-time high, solid external demand – economic acceleration in 
China plays an important role – and favorable terms of trade will 
likely lead to a record trade surplus this year. Moreover, the 
deficits in the services and primary income accounts have been 
gradually decreasing, in line with the cooling of economic activity. 
Consequently, we anticipate a current account deficit of USD 40 
billion at the end of 2023 (1.9% of GDP) and USD 48.0 billion at the 
end of 2024 (2.3% of GDP), against the USD 55.7 billion at the end 
of 2022 (2.9% of GDP). In turn, net inflows in FDI are expected to 
remain at historically high levels and record USD 80 billion this 
year (3.9% of GDP) and USD 75.0 billion next year (3.6% of GDP), 
slightly below the USD 90.6 billion (4.8% of GDP) reported last 
year.

Monetary Policy – Gradual easing in 22H2
The Copom has been keeping a hawkish tone in its recent official 
communication, and core inflation remains stubbornly high. Thus, 
we believe that the Selic rate will continue at current contractionary 
levels for some more time, which is consistent with Copom’s 
signaling to keep the policy rate at 13.75% for “a long period.”
At the same time, evidence is mounting that economic activity is 
losing steam. The unemployment rate has edged up. Credit 
markets are finally feeling the effects of monetary tightening. 
Additionally, exogenous events (such as the problems with Lojas 
Americanas) hit markets over and above what would be expected 
by tight monetary policy, particularly for SMEs.
The government’s new fiscal framework does not stabilize the 
debt-to-GDP ratio but brings some visibility, as it sets limits for 
fiscal expansion ahead. Thus, the framework tends to be helpful 
for monetary policy management; though it does not necessarily 
open room for a lower interest rate over time since the fiscal 
stance will remain expansionary.
All considered, we believe the Copom will start a gradual easing 
cycle in the second semester. We see a 25-bps cut in the August 
Copom meeting, followed by sequential 50-bps cuts until the Selic 
reaches 11.00% by the first half of 2024.
Given that we believe fiscal and quasi-fiscal policies will remain 
expansionary in the coming years, and global neutral interest rates 
tend to be higher than in recent years1, we forecast a terminal Selic 
rate (11.0%) that is considerably above pre-pandemics levels.

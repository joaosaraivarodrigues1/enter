Foreword – raising global tide amid pragmatic domestic economic 
policy
Inflation pressures are fading globally, suggesting that interest rates in developed markets will not rise much 
further. Commodity prices remain at relatively high levels, benefiting their exporters.
This combination spurred optimism to risky asset markets, particularly in Latin America. As we have been 
pointing out in our reports, the global environment has been benign for Brazil this year. Part of the positive 
performance of Brazilian assets relates to that.
We see the other reason related to a pragmatic approach to domestic economic policy. So far, we haven’t seen 
a reversion of important reforms from recent years – as highlighted by Fitch Rating as it upgraded Brazil’s 
sovereign rating; additionally, we’ve seen a push for further ones, like the tax reform.
Against this background, we forecast now a somewhat stronger BRL at the end of 2023 and 2024, which will 
help inflation to continue falling (though not to the target) and should allow a terminal Selic rate a bit below what 
we expected before (10.00% vs. 10.50%).
Expansionary fiscal policy and the uncertainty regarding the effectiveness of the tax-increase measures needed 
to balance the budget are, in our view, the main risk limiting further loosening of monetary policy.

Global backdrop: Monetary policy at a 
turning point with differences between 
regions
Francisco Nobre
We believe the Fed will no longer hike rates this year. The 
Fed hiked 25 bps in its June meeting and left the doors 
open for upcoming meetings, reinforcing its data 
dependent approach. In a new study we published last 
week (Fed Hawk-Dove Index), our model suggests that 
pressures on monetary policy have eased significantly 
throughout the first semester, consistent with a turning 
point and the end of the tightening cycle. Thus, we think 
that unless conditions deteriorate, the Fed will not hike 
again in this cycle. We expect the FOMC to start cutting 
rates in the third meeting of 2024, albeit gradually, ending 
the year at 4.0%.
No more automatic rate hikes in the Eurozone, but the story 
is different in the UK. We think that the ECB will hike 
interest rates one last time given that risks to inflation are 
higher than in the US, while interest rates are lower. In its 
turn, rates are still expected to rise somewhat further in the 
UK, given that inflation remains the highest among 
developed economies. The BoE raised rates by 25 bps in 
its August meeting to 5.25% and is expected to take it to 
6.0% by yearend.
In Latin America, earlier tightening will allow for earlier 
easing. Central banks in Latin America started to raise 
rates in 2021, around one year earlier than developed 
countries. Central banks are confident that inflation is 
trending down, and the recent downside surprises to CPI 
data reinforce this view. Brazil, Chile, and Uruguay have 
already started cutting rates. We believe that the next to 
start their own easing cycles will be Peru (August), 
Colombia (October) and Mexico (November).
But rate cuts do not mean expansionary monetary policy 
anytime soon. Monetary policy appears to be at a turning 
point amid the improving inflation outlook, and sooner or 
later, all central banks will start cutting rates. Still, the 
inflation issue has not disappeared yet, and monetary 
policy is expected to remain in contractionary territory 
around the globe until central banks are completely 
convinced that inflation will return to targets.
China´s economy lost momentum in the first semester of 
2023. The Chinese economy grew by a shy 0.8% in the 
second quarter, showing mounting evidence of a slow 
economic recovery since the economic reopening. This 
performance reflected falling exports, weak retail sales, 
and a slowing property sector. Consumer and business

Economic Activity – recent data confirm the 
economic slowdown
Rodolfo Margato
The economy grew only moderately in Q2. The main 
economic sectors have lost steam in recent months. 
Manufacturing continues on a downward trend, mainly due 
to falling capital goods output. Retail trade has stagnated, 
even though vehicle and foodstuff sales were positive due 
to government stimulus. Even the services sector, which 
led the post-covid recovery, seems weaker now. On the 
positive side are the sectors less sensitive to the economic 
cycle, such as agriculture and mining. All in all, we reinforce 
our estimate of a 0.3% increase in GDP for the second 
quarter compared to the previous quarter (2.6% compared 
to the same quarter of 2022).
In the labor market, the unemployment rate stood at around 
8% while real earnings stopped rising. The main 
employment and income indicators lost strength. According 
to our seasonally adjusted estimates, the monthly 
unemployment rate stands at around 8%, with a modest 
increase in the employed population and the labor force 
participation rate still well below pre-pandemic levels. In 
turn, real income metrics were practically stable between 
the end of 2022 and the end of the first half of 2023. We do 
not foresee any relevant changes in the labor market 
scenario until the end of this year. This dynamic is 
consistent with our scenario of a smooth deceleration in 
consumption, which is also supported by lower inflation in 
the short term and higher government income transfers

Public finance – No easy path for the 2024 
budget
Tiago Sbardelotto
Deficit in the first half of 2023. The public sector 
accumulated a deficit of BRL 20.4 billion (0.2% of GDP) in 
the first half of the year, reversing the surplus recorded at 
2022 yearend. The result was driven by the Central 
Government, which had a deficit of BRL 46.5 billion in June 
and accumulated a deficit of BRL 42.3 billion in the first half. 
The result can be explained by the 5.3% drop in net 
revenue, especially in concessions, dividends and 
corporate profit tax (IRPJ/CSLL), in addition to the 5.1% 
increase in expenses, mainly concentrated in social 
security and assistance benefits. In 12 months, the public 
sector accumulates a deficit of BRL 24.3 billion (0.2% of 
GDP).

External Accounts – we now see the FX at 4.70 
reais per US dollar at the end of 2023 and 4.85 
at the end of 2024
Rodolfo Margato
We revised our FX forecasts to 4.70 reais per dollar in 2023 
(5.00 before) and to 4.85 in 2024 (5.15 before). The BRL is 
some 10% up year-to-date, reaching the strongest level in 
15 months. In our view, this appreciation largely reflected 
the weaker US dollar globally and the rise (albeit moderate) 
in commodity prices. We believe that the international 
environment will remain relatively benign for Brazil in the 
coming quarters.
Stronger BRL forecast is backed by fundamentals. As we 
show in a recent publication, our models suggest that the 
fair value of the exchange rate would be within the 4.5-5.0 
reais per US dollar range (https://conteudos.xpi.com.br/wp 
content/uploads/2023/02/XP-Macro-Special-_-New-year-same-
question-Jan23-2.pdf).
Trade surplus at historic highs. Exports performed well in 
the first half, driven by primary goods. Imports contracted 
amid the slowdown in domestic demand. Thus, we 
estimate that the trade balance will reach the highest level 
in the historical series this year. This dynamic is consistent 
with our forecast of a USD 44.0 billion current account 
deficit at the end of this year (2.0% of GDP). Net inflows of 
FDI – Foreign Direct Investment – remain robust, despite 
some recent cooling. We project USD 75.0 billion at the end 
of 2023 (3.5% of GDP), a marginal reduction compared to 
last year due to the economic slowdown and the 
consequent reduction in corporate profitability.
For 2024, we project a current account balance of -2.1% of 
GDP, and FDI inflows of USD 73.0 billion.

2024 inflation reaps fruits from a more appreciated 
exchange rate, but it remains above the target... As we see 
now a stronger BRL for next year, we reduced our forecasts 
for monitored and industrial prices inflation. Thus, we 
reduced our 2024 IPCA forecast to 3.9% from 4.1%. 
Resilient labor market and the expansionary fiscal outlook 
should keep service inflation at relatively high levels. 
Regarding foodstuff prices, we are not considering any 
extreme scenario. On the one hand, El Niño could put 
pressure on fresh foods and sugar. On the other hand, 
global grain prices should remain at comfortable levels, as 
supply recovers in Latin America and the US.
Monetary Policy – stronger BRL allows for 
lower rates, but fiscal outlook still constrains 
monetary easing in 2024 (if the 3,0% target is 
for real, as we believe it is)

The Copom explicitly signaled 50-bps as the preferred 
pace for the monetary easing. This goes in line with our 
scenario, though we thought they would start with a 25-bps 
cut. Thus, we marginally adjusted our 2023 yearend Selic 
rate forecast to 11.75% from 12.00%.

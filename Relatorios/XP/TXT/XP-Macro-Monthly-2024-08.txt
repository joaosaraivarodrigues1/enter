Foreword – Amid high uncertainty, we stay put with our forecasts
Global economic outlook became more complex, with the increasing probability of a recession in the US, change of gears in
Japanese monetary policy and heightened geopolitical tensions. Brazilian asset prices reacted - aligned with other EM - with
BRL surpassing 5.8 reais per dollar and the stock markets erasing July’s gains.
Markets turmoil is certainly negative in absolute terms for Brazil, but it may have some positive collateral effects. It is difficult
to imagine the Copom hiking rates (as the markets are pricing in) under a global recession and while other central banks
(particularly the Fed) are cutting rates. On growth, Brazilian manufacturing is more dependent on domestic demand than on
exports and should not be severely hit. Moreover, geopolitical tensions tend to prevent commodity prices from falling much.
Domestically, good news and bad news. On the positive side, growth keep surprising to the upside, on both manufacturing
and services sector. Trade balance surplus will be solid once again this year and inflation readings remain benign, despite
running above target. On the negative side, fiscal expenditures are growing much above the legal limits. Revenues, despite
strong, have not been enough to balance the budget. Moreover, inflation expectations are on the rise, drifting further away
from the 3.0% target.
Our current forecasts look reasonable, considering the high degree of uncertainty. Thus, we barely changed the scenario this
month. We keep our forecast at 5.40 reais per dollar, a bit stronger than current price, which is consistent to still elevated
terms of trade and a widening interest-rate differential. Our forecast for 2024 IPCA inflation was slightly revised to 4.1% from
4.0%, while the projection for 2025 remains at 4.3%.
Under those circumstances, we believe the Copom may consider but will eventually decide to not hike rates. Unless the
exchange rate and inflation rise again due to (mainly) domestic factors.
What if the global scenario evolves to a hard landing one, triggering another spike in global risk aversion? On the one hand,
the BRL would depreciate further, pushing inflation to above the target range. On the other hand, the world economy would
be close or in a recession, with negative effects on commodity prices and cost-push inflation.
How would the Brazilian Central Bank react under those circumstances? Tough call. The Copom would find itself between a
rock and a hard place: missing the inflation target or risking a recession hiking rates while the rest of the world would be
cutting.
We prefer to see if that will indeed happen before risk answering.

Are the conditions for a tightening already there? The key
question among market participants has been: do the inflation
drivers need to worsen further for the Copom to hike rates? Or
do they need to improve to avoid a tightening? The official
communication is not clear about that.
In our baseline scenario, we see the BRL stabilizing below 5.50
- a bit stronger than today’s value - and inflation expectations
around 4.0%. According to our estimates, those inputs would
keep the Copom’s forecast not too much above the target in
the relevant horizon. This, along with the risk of a global
recession, would keep the Copom on pause for the rest of the
year (and 2025), in our view.
Thus, we keep our view of a flat policy rate until 2025 yearend.
What would trigger a hike? As highlighted in the minutes of the
last monetary policy meeting, the Copom (unanimously) would
not hesitate to raise the benchmark interest rate to ensure
inflation convergence to the target if it deems it appropriate. If
the exchange rate continues to weaken and/or inflation
expectations rise (due to the weaker FX, tight labor market,
higher current inflation, etc), the Copom’s inflation forecast
would move further away from the target, demanding a policy
reaction. This scenario is becoming more likely, particularly
with the asymmetric balance of risks for inflation.

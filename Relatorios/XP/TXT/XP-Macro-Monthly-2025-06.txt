The chances of a global recession induced by US tariffs seems to have diminished. 
However, fiscal risks now weigh on long-term yields in developed markets, which tends to 
impact EM.
• 
In Brazil, June begins with a focus on discussions regarding the package of measures 
negotiated between government and Congress as an alternative to the increase in the IOF 
(Tax on Financial Transactions), amid challenges in meeting the primary fiscal targets for 
2025 and 2026.
• 
We raised our GDP growth projections for 2025 (from 2.3% to 2.5%) and 2026 (from 1.5% 
to 1.7%). The labor market remains tight, and credit concessions are showing resilience.
• 
Considering the increase in the IOF (or something to replace it), we see the government 
meeting the primary fiscal target in 2025 and getting closer in 2026. As we approach next 
year’s budget discussions, however, the risks on the spending side increase.
• 
We maintained our forecast at 5.80 reais per dollar by YE25 and 6.10 by YE26. Meanwhile, 
we revised our projection for 2025 current account deficit from 3.4% to 3.0% of GDP, given 
improvements in services and primary income accounts.
• 
We reduced our 2025 IPCA inflation projection from 5.7% to 5.5%, reflecting a benign 
short-term outlook and moderation in wholesale prices. For 2026, we maintained our 
projection at 4.7%.
• 
On the back of Central Bank’s (dovish) official communication and the higher IOF rate, we 
have recently reduced our terminal Selic rate projection from 15.00% to 14.75%. A final 
25-bp hike is still on the table, nonetheless, particularly if the IOF rate hike is reversed, 
amid strong economic activity and elevated inflation expectations. After that, we see a 
long period of stable rates.

Foreword – Back to fiscal (in Brazil and in the US)
The chances of a global recession induced by Trump’s trade war seem to have diminished. Meanwhile, US Lower House 
passed a significant tax and spending bill that should provide further impulse for growth ahead. In that scenario, the Fed 
will have little-to-no room to cut rates ahead.
Easy fiscal and tight money is not a good combination for debt sustainability. It has put pressure on long-term yields in the 
US and other DM (as Japan), which represents a risk for Brazilian asset prices. Particularly amid lower oil prices, that did 
not pull back in response to a lower probability of a global recession.
With a lower tailwind from the global scenario, domestic issues come back to the radar scream, the most relevant being, 
as usual, fiscal. The government announced an expenditure freezing of BRL 30 billion, which was perceived as insufficient 
to comply with the legal spending cap. It hiked considerably the financial transaction tax rate, looking to increase revenue 
and meet the primary balance target. Congress has questioned the changes, and these could be replaced soon.
The event spurred the perception among market participants that the current fiscal framework is unsustainable, and that 
deeper reforms are needed following next year’s elections. Meanwhile, the government may continue to discuss and 
implement short-term measures to support consumers’ income and spending.
In this context, we reinforce our view that activity will continue to surprise consensus to the upside. We increased once 
again our GDP growth forecast for 2025 and 2026 and see no relief from inflationary pressures over the medium term. 
Despite reducing our 2025 IPCA forecast to 5.5% from 5.7% due to lower short-term pressure on food and industrials, we 
still see 2026 at 4.7% - above the upper bound of the target range.
The Copom, however, seems to have a more benign view than ours on the inflation dynamics. Board members have been 
stressing that rates are “high enough”, and that these should remain so for a “long period”. On top of that, the increase in 
the IOF rate (if maintained) helps to curb credit growth. Thus, we recently reduced our terminal Selic rate from 15.50% to 
15.00% (May 5), and then to 14.75% (May 23).
But we still see inflation as the biggest risk for Brazil in the medium-to-long term.

BRAZIL – Fiscal uncertainty strikes 
again
Political environment – Possible scenarios for 
the IOF
Paulo Gama, Bianca Lima e João Paulo Machado
June will bring into focus discussions about the political 
viability of new sources of revenue to maintain expectations of 
meeting the 2025 primary target, following the presentation of 
the first revenue and expenditure assessment report late last 
month. The new source of revenue found by the government 
— a decree that increased the financial transaction tax rate 
(IOF, in the Portuguese acronym) in various cases — faced 
severe resistance and was partially revoked. Negotiations with 
Congress for alternatives to the original proposal will remain in 
evidence at the beginning of the month.
The government's objective is to approve measures that could 
offset points of the decree throughout this year, such as the 
increase in the IOF on reversing factoring operations, for 
example. Alternatives for all measures included in the decree 
would only come into effect from 2026 onwards, according to 
the Executive’s projections. Therefore, the proposal may mix 
short-term alternatives, focused on boosting revenue faster, 
with more structural ones for next year.
Short term measures on the table include changes that do not 
require Congress, such as the anticipation of BNDES dividends 
and solutions involving the ANP (National Agency of 
Petroleum). One of the ideas presented by the Ministry of 
Mines and Energy, for example, would include a change in the 
reference price that serves as the basis for calculating royalty 
payments.
In the medium term, looking to 2026, more structural 
alternatives are being considered. These include a non-linear 
reduction in tax benefits and other politically controversial 
proposals, such as reducing spending growth on Fundeb (the 
basic education fund).
Other alternatives include changing the health and education 
spending floors (which would follow the fiscal framework 
spending rule capped at 2.5%). This, however, faces resistance 
among government members – as well as the alternative to 
curbing BPC’s expenditure growth.
In addition to the new measures expected to be presented to 
congressmen in the coming days, the government wants to 
use ongoing discussions to review some structural issues. In 
the case of administrative reform, the effort is to include the 
debate on "supersalaries". As for the income tax exemption bill, 
which is expected to have its first report by the end of the

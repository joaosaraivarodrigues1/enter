Foreword – Fiscal transition amid global risks
From the perspective of economic policy, the first-round campaign brought limited news. Both president Bolsonaro
and former president Lula signaled their intention to replace the constitutional spending cap as the main fiscal
anchor, but it is unclear what comes next. Considering that Brazil still has an elevated debt and debt service, the
fiscal policy guideline from 2023 onwards is critical for inflation expectations to fall and for the central bank to find
space to reduce interest rates going forward.
The relatively tight first round result may pressure Lula to give more details on his economic plan and team, to
attract the more moderated voters. President Bolsonaro, in turn, tends to reinforce the importance of the “Auxílio-
Brasil” cash-transfer program to revert part of Lula’s landslide advantage in the northeast region.
Still, for now, it is unlikely that much of the economic policy plans will be disclosed by either candidate.
In this background, markets tend to remain volatile. Especially with the rest of the world also bringing challenges.
Will the Fed raise interest rates beyond the 4.6% signaled by the FOMC members´ September projections? On the
one hand, inflation remains high, and the labor market is tight. On the other hand, there are initial signs that the rapid
rise in interest rates could pose a risk to global financial stability. In Europe, uncertainty regarding energy supplies
during the winter remains high. Moreover, tensions between China and Taiwan continue.
Current Brazilian economic indicators should remain positive, with solid growth, falling inflation, and healthy fiscal
and balance of payments results. But this has limited importance amid global and local risks for 2023.

Global Backdrop: Global disinflation will not 
be painless
Signs of global disinflation have become increasingly clear. First, 
leading indicators, such as the GSCPI and the 60% fall in freight prices, 
suggests significant improvements in global supply chains. Second, 
commodity prices have eased considerably after a prolonged uptrend 
period. Despite our expectations that commodity prices will remain 
high due to persistent supply pressures, the recent adjustment eases 
inflationary pressures. Last, tighter monetary policy around the globe 
is expected to soften aggregate demand considerably. We believe that 
the combination of these three factors will alleviate price pressures 
considerably going forward, and the global economy should soon enter 
a disinflationary process.
The Fed might have to force a recession in the US. The FOMC 
increased the Fed Funds rate by 75bps for the third consecutive time, 
marking an unprecedented tightening pace. We believe that if there is 
a positive surprise in the September inflation and labor market data, 
the Fed might find room to slow down the tightening pace and reach a 
terminal rate below the 4.5%-4.75% currently indicated by the “dots” 
projections. In that scenario, we believe that the US economy would 
slow down materially but not enter a recession, given that economic 
fundamentals remain solid. On the other hand, if negative surprises in 
the upcoming data force the Fed to take interest rates to levels 
currently priced by markets, the US economy would most likely enter a 
recession in 2023. In that scenario, we believe that US GDP would grow 
closer to 1.0% in 2022 and 0.5% in 2023.
The European economy shows emerging market characteristics. 
Inflation hit double digits in September, reaching yet another record 
high. Economic activity in the region is expected to weaken 
considerably amid lower purchasing power, tighter financial 
conditions, and ongoing geopolitical uncertainties. Moreover, the 
energy crisis in Europe is having a deleterious impact on fiscal 
accounts, which will be particularly concerning going forward as real 
interest rates increase. Consequently, currencies in the region have 
weakened considerably, which may also drive imported inflation. The 
UK was the highlight this month, and the GBP depreciated almost 10% 
in September against the USD, reflecting increased recession risk, 
fiscal uncertainty, the expectations that inflation will continue to rise, 
and a less hawkish central bank. In all, high-frequency indicators 
suggest that European economies will face a difficult period of 
contraction, high inflation, currency depreciation, and a deteriorating 
fiscal outlook.
China´s growth remains modest. The latest data on the Chinese 
economy was mainly bearish, suggesting continued slowdown amid 
weakness in the property markets and the persistence of Covid-19 
lockdown policies. We reiterate that inflation stands at low levels, 
allowing monetary and fiscal policies to remain expansionary. We 
believe that economic activity will pick up towards the end of the year, 
and China´s GDP will grow by 3.2% in 2022 and 5.6% in 2023.

Nevertheless, growth will remain hostage to pandemic-related policies 
and stimulus measures, and without these impulses, China would 
grow closer to 1.5% in the next two years.
Latin American assets continue to show strong resilience despite 
global uncertainties. Despite the dollar appreciating significantly due to 
increased risk aversion, Latam currencies have outperformed other 
reference currencies, especially the EUR, the GBP, and the CNY. The 
region continues to benefit from high commodity prices, high interest 
rate differentials from the US, and cheap valuations.
Brazil: Waiting for the runoff elections
Economic Activity – Continued growth, albeit 
more moderately
Brazil’s GDP is expected to show further increase in Q3, but there are 
signs of deceleration. The domestic economy remains on an upward 
path, with important sectoral differences. On the one hand, many 
service activities still benefit from the normalization of mobility and 
household 
consumption 
patterns, 
particularly 
in 
food, 
accommodation, 
tourist 
segments, 
transportation, 
and 
corporate/social events. Furthermore, the improvement in global 
supply chains allows for a gradual recovery of the industrial sector 
since the beginning of the year, after a long period of contraction. On 
the other hand, retail sales have weakened in recent months, especially 
for the most credit-sensitive goods. Tighter monetary policy and high 
household indebtedness have weighed more heavily on durable and 
semi-durable goods (e.g., vehicles; furniture and household appliances; 
building materials). For now, we estimate an advance of 0.5% QoQ for 
Q3 GDP (3.5% YoY).
Labor market indicators remain the protagonists. The net creation of 
formal jobs has positively surprised in the recent period, with 
widespread improvement across sectors and types of occupation. 
According to our calculations, the monthly unemployment rate is 
currently at 8.5%, around 3pp below the levels recorded just before the 
pandemic. Real wages have resumed growth, owing chiefly to the 
falling jobless rate and easing short-term inflation, despite remaining 
nearly 5.5% below the numbers seen at the end of 2019. The recovery 
in the labor market in tandem with additional fiscal stimulus – 
especially the higher monthly benefit of the ‘Auxílio Brasil’ welfare 
program – will likely maintain the household disposable income on the 
rise, a significant counterweight to the deterioration of financial 
conditions.
We continue to expect that total GDP will expand by 2.8% in 2022. In 
addition to the 0.5% QoQ advance in Q3, our base case considers a 
slight gain of 0.2% QoQ in Q4. Economic activity indicators released in 
recent weeks have reinforced the prognosis of a mild slowdown over 
the second half of the year. Based on these quarterly forecasts, the 
statistical carryover effect for next year’s GDP would be positive by 
0.7pp.

For 2023 GDP growth, our projection remains at 1.0%. We believe that 
the Brazilian economy will grow modestly next year. The impact of the 
contractionary monetary policy on local demand should be more 
noticeable in the coming quarters, leading to a weaker dynamic in 
consumption and investment. Greater risk aversion and worsening 
global economic activity (with growing concerns about recession in 
key regions) will likely be strong headwinds in 2023. Despite that, we 
do not anticipate a recession in the coming quarters. Confidence 
indicators remain at high levels, which reduces the likelihood of a 
sudden stop in the short term. In addition, local sectors less sensitive 
to the economic cycle, such as the mining industry and, above all, 
agriculture, should grow significantly in the period.
Fiscal – New framework will be key
The outlook for 2022 remains positive. Despite the one-off deficit in 
August, we expect a surplus of BRL 30.1 billion (0.3% of GDP) for the 
central government this year. For the consolidated public sector, we 
estimate a BRL 85 billion surplus (0.9% of GDP).
We expect expenditures to accelerate next year, pushing the primary 
balance back to negative territory. As we have discussed in our last 
reports, there will be a need to accommodate up to BRL 165 billion in 
additional expenses, such as the extension of Auxílio Brasil (BRL 60 
billion) and readjustments to public servants (BRL 32 billion), which will 
require a review of the spending cap in the amount of BRL 140 billion.
Thus, we maintain our 2023 deficit scenario for the Central 
Government at BRL 91.0 billion (-0.9% of GDP).
The consolidated public sector (Central Government, States and 
Municipalities and State-owned companies) should also worsen in 
2023. The fall in the ICMS tax collection verified in August should 
continue in the coming months and will affect revenues throughout 
2023. As a result, we estimate a significant decrease in the primary 
balance of subnational governments, which will fall to 0.2% of GDP. In 
aggregate, the public sector should reach a deficit of BRL 70 billion, or 
0.7% of GDP.
Debt-to-GDP ratio to resume its upward trend. The positive primary 
result in 2022 should reduce the General Government Gross Debt 
(GGDB) ratio to 79.2%. But from 2023, with the increase in expenses 
due to the change in the spending cap rule and the higher nominal 
interest, the public debt should grow again.
Fiscal policy in the coming years remains uncertain. With the 
presidential election undefined, decisions regarding what should be the 
expenditure level in the 2023 budget and, especially, the framework of 
fiscal rules for the coming years remain unclear. The challenge is to 
design a new fiscal anchor that accommodates new expenditures but 
offers debt sustainability in the following years.
The balance of public accounts in coming years requires a rigid 
spending rule. In our simulations, after incorporating an initial level 
adjustment for 2023, there will be little room for real expenditure 
growth in the coming years. In fact, expenditure growth north of 1% in 
real terms is sufficient to keep debt-to-GDP ratio on the rise for the next 
decade. An increase in the tax burden to finance higher spending can 
be counterproductive, given the adverse effects on economic growth.

Inflation – Better dynamics for 2022 and 2023
We revised our 2022 IPCA forecast from 6.1% to 5.6%. September Mid-
month IPCA-15 inflation came out below expectation, with clear 
deceleration on industrial goods and underlying services. Moreover, 
wholesale prices are falling, suggesting lower cost-push CPI inflation 
ahead.
Industrial goods inflation, which has risen 26% in the past two years, 
shows signs of moderation. Global supply chains have been 
normalizing, as demonstrated by the New York Fed indicator and the 
recently published Central Bank for Brazil indicator. The fall in domestic 
energy and fuel prices reinforces lower cost pressures. There are also 
signs of deceleration on the demand side.
Due to the lower inertia, we have adjusted the 2023 forecast from 5.3% 
to 5.2%. We strongly believe in global disinflation in 2023, due to tighter 
monetary policy in developed countries and the normalization of 
production chains. Food and industrial goods will be the groups that 
benefit most from this process.
IPCA forecast (YoY % ch.)
Service inflation should remain high, keeping the IPCA above the upper   
bound of the target range. We project a 6.2% rise for the group in 2023. 
Regarding the labor market, real growth of the disposable income and 
a more gradual deceleration of the economy contribute to this scenario 
of persistent price pressures in the first half of next year, despite tighter 
monetary conditions.

External Sector – BRL holding up well considering 
strong-dollar trend
The BRL, as most currencies, has been highly volatile. It weakened in 
September but pulled back to around 5.15 reais per dollar after the first 
round of the elections. As a result, the BRL is now some 8% stronger 
than in December last year. It is a remarkable performance, 
considering the US dollar is almost 20% up against major currencies 
(see chart in the global backdrop session above).
We maintain our forecast of BRL/USD 5.00 at the end of 2022. Our 
scenario incorporates some reduction in risk premiums after the 
elections, as well as clearer outlook for monetary policy in developed 
markets. For 2023 yearend, we project BRL/USD 5.30.
The current account deficit widens gradually, while foreign investment 
accelerates. The current account deficit reached USD 36.6 billion in the 
accumulated 12 months until July (-2.1% of GDP) after closing 2021 at 
USD 27.9 billion (-1.7% of GDP). In the financial account, the direct 
investment liabilities accumulated a surplus of USD 65.6 billion (3.7% 
of GDP) in the accumulated 12 months, a 46% increase relative to the

Monetary Policy – A credible fiscal anchor would 
allow rates at 10% by 2023 yearend
The Copom interrupted the tightening cycle that started in March 2021, 
keeping a hawkish tone. The committee said that monetary policy is 
already at a significantly restrictive level but pointed out that it would 
“not hesitate to resume the tightening cycle if the disinflationary 
process does not proceed as expected.”
Inflation expectations have eased, reinforcing Copom’s decision to 
wait and see. According to the Focus survey, the 2022 and 2023 IPCA 
forecasts have been declining almost continuously since early August. 
The 2024 forecasts have edged up, but in that case, we believe it is 
more related to medium-term fiscal uncertainties than monetary policy 
management.
Global disinflation should help the Copom in its task.  Monetary policy 
is adjusting fast in the western world, and global supply chains seem 
to be normalizing. Thus, from both demand and supply reasons, global 
inflation should decline in 2023.
Thus, we keep our scenario that the Selic rate will remain at 13.75% 
until June and decline to 10.00% by 2023 yearend.
Monetary easing, though, depends crucially on the new fiscal anchor. 
Both presidential candidates have signaled changes in the spending 
cap for next year. Considering Brazilian debt and debt service remain 
elevated, we consider a new (and credible) fiscal anchor a key 
condition for inflation expectations to converge to the target path.

Foreword – Armistice
The tensions between the Executive Branch and the Brazilian Central Bank (BCB) cooled down a bit.
President Lula has been somewhat less vocal, while the BCB governor Roberto Campos has repeatedly
said that the monetary authority and the government should “work together.”
The economic fundamentals behind the dispute, however, didn’t change. The government continues to
signal an expansionary fiscal policy, including measures to increase disposable income and to reduce
households’ and companies’ indebtedness. Those measures would dilute the impact of monetary
policy, which ultimately require an even tighter monetary stance ahead.
The global environment is also becoming a bit more challenging. Since our last Monthly report, growth
and inflation in developed economies are proving more resistant, which may force major central banks
to raise interest rates above what was previously expected. At the same time, commodity prices has
accommodated.
Under those uncertainties, we kept most of our forecasts for the Brazilian economy unchanged. We still
see GDP growth at 1.0% this year and 0.8% in 2024; and IPCA inflation at 5.5% this year and 4.5% next
year.
With inflation above the target, we continue to see the Selic rate at 13.75% until the end of the year. But
the political pressure over the BCB will continue to escalate. Thus, it is possible that the BCB opts to
begin a gradual easing in the second half as 2025 becomes relevant in the monetary policy horizon.
A key factor is the behavior of inflation expectations. So far, market forecasts - ours include - have
moved towards what is believed to be the next inflation target (4.0% - 4.5%). It is a plausible outcome,
but it would require a sustainable and credible fiscal policy for the years to come. Otherwise,
expectations will probably climb above the new target, reflecting the risk of an inflationary way out for
the rising public debt.
Finance Minister Haddad promised the guidelines of the new fiscal framework for March. Market
analysts look forward to it.

Global 
backdrop: 
Restrictive 
monetary policy for longer
War in Ukraine: One year and counting. What initially appeared 
would be a short-lived conflict has completed its first anniversary. 
On the economic front, the war led to significant surges in the 
prices of energy and agricultural commodities, aggravating the 
global inflation challenge. Although the impact on commodity 
prices has eased, there are no signs of a resolution to the conflict. 
The risk of the war intensity escalating could lead to further oil 
supply constraints and pressure prices. On the other hand, if 
China's reopening proves to be more modest than what is 
currently priced in, we see space for further price corrections, 
albeit small. Our base scenario remains that commodity prices 
accommodate around current (high) levels, although we see risks 
tilted slightly to the downside.
The global economy remains resilient. Activity in major developed 
economies continues to show unexpected resilience despite tight 
financial conditions. Fear of a global recession has eased, and the 
strong growth expected for China and India should partially offset 
the weakness in economic activity in the rest of the world. 
Nevertheless, lingering high inflation is expected to force central 
banks to maintain financial conditions tight for longer, and we 
believe that it is still too early to rule out the possibility of a 
recession in the US and Europe.
The Fed will have to keep rates high for longer. Data released over 
the past month revealed significant upward surprises in inflation, 
activity, and the labor market. Consequently, despite interest rates 
having already moved to restrictive territory, we believe that the 
Fed will need to do more. We expect the Fed to hike rates by 25 
bps at least three more times, reaching the terminal rate of 5.5%. 
It does not seem to be the case, however, for the tightening pace 
to go back to 50bps. Still, we believe that risks are tilted to the 
upside, and we now see limited room for rate cuts by yearend.
The question is: how deep would the recession be? After having 
fallen behind the curve, the Fed had to adopt an unusually 
aggressive monetary policy stance since the beginning of the 
tightening cycle 12 months ago. Still, given that the neutral interest 
rate is estimated at around 0.3%-1.0% in real terms, financial 
conditions entered contractionary territory only in September 
2022. Considering the lagged effect of monetary policy 
transmission of between 12 to 24 months, it is still unclear what 
will be the impact of the cumulative tightening on the aggregate 
economy. Our base case scenario remains that inflation 
converges to target after the US economy faces a moderate 
recession. However, if interest rates remain restrictive for longer 
than expected, the impact on economic activity and corporate 
earnings can become greater.
China's reopening: mixed initial signs, although solid growth is still 
expected. Initial data points to significant improvements in 
mobility and demand for oil, and the PMI indices have returned to 
solid expansionary territory in January and February (graphs).

Brazil: Fiscal visibility is key for 
rate cuts
Economic 
Activity 
– 
Ongoing 
slowdown
Brazil’s GDP expanded by 2.9% in 2022 despite a setback in Q4. 
Headline GDP dropped 0.2% QoQ in the last quarter, roughly in line 
with expectations. Even so, the domestic economy grew 2.9% in 
the full-year 2022, much above initial projections. The statistical 
carry-over effect for 2023 GPD growth stands at 0.2pp.
The economic slowdown will continue into 2023. Mounting signs 
point to slower growth this year. Industrial production and retail 
sales – especially of more credit-sensitive goods – have retreated 
in the recent period amid the contractionary monetary policy. 
Furthermore, the benefits of ‘post-Covid’ reopening on service 
activities are dissipating. Credit and labor market impulses, 
noticeable since 2H21, should not be present throughout 2023. 
Elevated household indebtedness and interest rates lead to rising 
delinquency rates, which intensify concerns about the financial 
balance of individuals and companies.
That said, some factors are expected to soften the activity 
deceleration in the short term.
Agricultural output plays a major role. Sectors less sensitive to the 
economic cycle will likely grow strongly in 2023, with emphasis on 
agriculture. Grain harvest should record a new all-time high, 
mostly driven by soybean production. We project an expansion of 
around 8% for the primary sector’s GDP this year, contributing 
~0.5pp to headline GDP growth. Additionally, commodity prices at 
high levels and government stimulus measures support domestic 
income. Regarding the latter, we call attention to the higher 
spending with the ‘Bolsa Família’ welfare program, the real 
increase in the minimum wage and government initiatives aimed 
at the credit market (for example, a program focused on 
renegotiating household debts). As per our calculations, 
household disposable income will rise around 3% in 2023, 
smoothing the deceleration of private consumption and, 
consequently, of total GDP.

Fiscal Policy – Waiting for the new 
framework
Short-term results remained positive. Federal tax collection grew 
6.4% YoY in January, a slowdown compared to 22Q4, mainly due 
to weaker economic activity. Still, revenues remain at historically 
high level. The central government posted a surplus of BRL 73.8 
billion in January, the second best in the historical series for the 
month in real terms. The overall public sector (including states 
and municipalities) reached a surplus of BRL 99.0 billion in the 
month (see chart).
Positive results in January do not change the challenging the 
outlook for the rest of the year. The central government revenues 
should remain solid, largely supported by high oil prices. However, 
expenditures already show a significant 6% YoY increase above 
inflation, boosted by the expansion of the Bolsa-Família cash-
transfer program and other discretionary expenditures. We see 
expenses above revenues ahead (see details below), which should 
be reflected in a deterioration of fiscal results throughout the year.
Recent measures should increase the deficit. The government 
announced that it will raise the minimum wage to BRL 1320 and 
the income tax exemption to BRL 2640 starting in May of this year. 
Taken 
together, 
these 
measures 
imply 
an 
impact 
of 
approximately BRL 10 billion. Additionally, the government 
announced further changes in the Bolsa-Família program, almost 
zeroing the room for savings previously announced by finance 
Minister Haddad.
Thus, we rose our Central Government primary deficit forecast 
from BRL 84.2 billion (0.8% of GDP) to BRL 107.4 billion (1.0% of 
GDP).
For 2024, we carried out a more comprehensive review, also 
considering the changes in the dividend policy of state-owned 
companies. As a result, we increased our estimate from a deficit 
of BRL 72.6 billion (0.7% of GDP) to BRL 116.6 billion (1.0% of 
GDP).
Risks for both revenues and expenditures remain. As we 
highlighted earlier, a considerable share of the revenue gain last 
year was due to oil. In our baseline scenario we consider oil prices

GDP. On the expenditure side, the main risks are due to expenses 
not included in the budget, such as the nursing minimum-wage

Debt fell in January but will probably resume its upward trend

general government's gross debt fell from 73.5% to 73.1%. 
However, with primary balance falling back to negative territory 
and market interest rates at high levels, we see gross debt 
reaching 77.3% in 2023 and 81.0% in 2024 (see chart).
The government is expected to present its fiscal framework soon. 
A credible proposal that leads to fiscal sustainability is a 
necessary condition for inflation expectations to begin to decline 
in the medium term and allow the central bank to reduce interest 
rates more quickly. However, it is necessary to consider whether 
this framework will be compatible with all the government's 
sectoral policy proposals. A more restrictive rule in terms of 
spending growth will hardly support raising the minimum-wage 
above inflation, increasing civil servants' salaries, and maintaining 
income transfers and the level of public investment. On the other 
hand, a looser rule will not be effective in guaranteeing fiscal 
adjustment over time.
Inflation – fiscal sustainability is key 
for expectations to remain close to 
the target path
We recently revised our 2023 inflation forecast from 5.7% to 5.5%. 
The government announced last week the partial resumption of 
federal taxes on fuel (that had been zeroed in 2022). We had 
considered that the tax would be fully restored. Thus, we 
marginally reduced our IPCA inflation forecast by 20bps.
Foodstuff and industrialized goods prices will lead disinflation this 
year. Despite the upward pressure on food at the beginning of the 
year, the outlook is that improved weather conditions, lower costs 
and increased supply will allow food at home inflation to fall to 
3.0% in the year (after 13.2% in 2022). On its turn, industrialized 
goods inflation should retreat to 2.2% due to lower domestic 
demand and better global supply conditions (chart).
Services inflation retreated compared to last year but remains at 
a high level. The 3-month seasonally adjusted annualized moving 
average of the IPCA services decelerated significantly (chart). But 
this was not enough to bring inflation to the center of the target 
(currently at 3.25%). From now on, pressure from inertia, a real 
increase in the minimum wage, and rising expectations should 
keep the group's inflation at a high level, retreating only 1pp 
compared to 2022.

For 2024, we maintain our forecast at 4.5%. Our baseline scenario 
implies a change in the target for 2024 and beyond by 1.5pp. But 
with inflation expectations remained anchored around the new 
target. If fiscal outlook deteriorates, expectations may continue to 
rise. This is an upward risk to our 4.5% forecast.
External Sector: BRL remains volatile 
amid local and external uncertainties
The Brazilian exchange rate depreciated last month, from 
BRL/USD 5.09 in January to BRL/USD 5.20 in February. 
Considering both structural and cyclical factors, our models 
suggest that the exchange rate could be between BRL/USD 4.50 
BRL/USD 4.85, but domestic risks, especially in the fiscal field, 
have contributed to maintaining the BRL undervalued. We believe 
that throughout the year the discussions in terms of economic 
policy will be difficult, with several comings and goings. Therefore, 
for now, we maintain the exchange rate projection at BRL/USD 
5.30 at the end of 2023, with an annual average of BRL/USD 5.15.
In January, the Brazilian current account recorded a deficit of USD 
8.8 billion. On the one hand, the Primary Income account posted 
a larger deficit relative to the same month last year, owing chiefly 
to sharper net expenditures on profits and dividends linked to both 
direct and portfolio. On the other hand, the Merchandise Trade 
Balance remains on a solid upward path, still reflecting elevated 
commodity prices. This behavior should persist throughout 2023, 
in our view, on the heels of China’s economic reopening, the 
resilience of activity in advanced economies, and, consequently, 
firm external demand for Brazilian exports. Moreover, total 
imports will likely grow at a slower pace this year amid the 
weakening of domestic demand. We anticipate deficits of USD 
50.5 billion (2.5% of GDP) in 2023 and USD 53.0 billion (2.6% of 
GDP) in 2024.
On the financial account side, net inflows in FDI (Foreign Direct 
Investment) totaled USD 6.9 billion in January 2023. It reached 
USD 92.3 billion in the 12-month rolling sum up to January 2023 
(4.78% of GDP), significantly above the USD 48.1 billion recorded 
for January 2022 (2.91% of GDP). Looking ahead, we expect net 
inflows in FDI to reach USD 80 billion in 2023 (3.9% of GDP) and 
USD 75 billion in 2024 (3.6% of GDP). In other words, levels below 
those recorded for 2022 amidst the cooling of economic activity, 
but still close to all-time highs.
Monetary Policy – Under Pressure
The outlook for inflation remains challenging. Recent readings of 
IPCA inflation showed the core component stubbornly high; the 
labor market remains tight, despite some recent deceleration; 
medium-term inflation expectations are on the rise; and the 
government is working on measures to sustain credit markets, 
which would water down the effects of tight monetary policy.
Against this backdrop, we would be comfortable with our scenario 
that the Copom will keep the Selic rate at 13.75% until the end of

Foreword – Can this really be the end?
President Lula said that Brazil does not need to achieve the zero fiscal target proposed by the government if a 
cut in expenditures is required. Many saw that as a signal that the fiscal framework is over.
We would not go that far. Some of the revenue measures are progressing in Congress, and, on the expenditure 
side, there is still a growth limit (2,5% increase in real terms) that prevents a looser target from allowing for even 
higher expenses.
In any event, the change in the target suggests that, under a binding constraint, the revealed preference is to 
give flexibility to the framework rather than to adjust the fiscal policy stance (this was not the first time that 
happened). It reinforces our long-held view that fiscal policy has an expansionary bias that will require monetary 
policy to stay in the restrictive territory for longer for IPCA inflation to stabilize within the target range.
Indeed, fiscal instability and higher long-term bond yields in developed markets should limit the room for lower 
rates in Brazil next year.
Having said that, we do not see reasons for the central bank to reduce the pace of monetary easing in the short 
term. The Selic rate is still quite high, and current inflation and activity data are on a downward trend.
Last but not least, we still see solid fundamentals supporting the Brazilian real. Beyond short-term volatility, we 
forecast the exchange rate below 5,0 reais to the US dollar over time.

The US Fed will proceed carefully following its November 
meeting. The FOMC maintained the target range for the

Treasury yields represents an additional tightening in credit

Source: BEA, XP
We still consider a final hike in our scenario, though recent 
data and still high Treasury yields may convince the FOMC 
that monetary policy is tight enough.  The latest Payroll 
report suggested that the labor market is finally cooling, 
while recent consumer price inflation readings were more 
benign. Meanwhile, despite the recent correction, long-
term Treasury yields remain close to their 15-year highs.
Short-term volatility does not change the view of higher 
rates for longer. In any case, the Fed is very close to the 
end of the tightening cycle, and keeping rates restrictive for 
longer will be more important than additional hikes, in our 
view. We reiterate our call that we think that the FOMC will 
have to wait until 3Q24 to start easing monetary policy, 
ending the year with the upper bound of the fed funds rate 
at 4.75%. In 2025, we expect rates to drop to 3.5%, which 
we think will be a new neutral level.
In Europe, central banks are more confident about the end 
of the tightening cycle. The ECB and the BoE left rates 
unchanged in their last meeting as the balance of risks 
between lingering high inflation and weakening economic 
activity has become more even amid tight monetary policy. 
The risk of a recession is mounting, and price pressures 
are gradually easing. Monetary authorities left doors open 
for more tightening if needed, given that inflation remains 
well above target, but we believe the bar is high for another 
rate hike. We expect rates to remain unchanged until the 
fourth or fifth meeting of 2024, followed by a gradual easing 
cycle.

Additional stimulus in China does not change weaker 
growth dynamics. Despite Q3 GDP having surprised to the 
upside, overall growth has disappointed this year. The 
government announced 1 trillion yuan (around USD 140 
billion) in additional stimulus to boost activity. Most of the 
resources will be used for water conservancy and flood 
prevention projects amid adverse weather conditions this 
year. We do not expect these stimuli to significantly alter 
China´s growth dynamics and our fundamental view 
remains: as China gets richer, its growth is expected to 
keep slowing. Growth drivers will likely shift from the 
property and external sectors to consumption and 
technology, and GDP will likely expand (considerably) 
below 5% per year.
Commodity prices remain sensitive to geopolitical risks. 
Brent oil prices hovered around USD 85-90 in the past 
month, at levels that we do not consider a threat to the 
global disinflation process. Still, commodity prices have 
been very volatile amid growing geopolitical uncertainties. 
We do not expect prices to reaccelerate, although the risk 
of the war in the Middle East dissipating poses upside risks. 
In any case, commodity prices remain at high historical 
levels, which has benefited exporters.

Rodolfo Margato
Exchange rate volatility remains high. The Brazilian 
exchange rate returned to levels close to BRL/USD 4.90 at 
the beginning of November, after reaching BRL/USD 5.15 
a month earlier. The strengthening of the Real – and other 
emerging currencies – in recent days was mainly due to the 
relief in US Treasury yields following signs of a slowdown 
in the local labor market. That said, the global economic 
environment remains quite uncertain. After all, the scenario 
of high interest rates for longer in developed economies 
has not gone off the radar, and geopolitical tensions 
persist. On the domestic side, fiscal risks have taken center 
stage, especially with the likely change in primary result 
targets. After incorporating recent movements, we adjusted 
our exchange rate projections for YE 2023, to BRL/USD 
4.90 from BRL/USD 5.10.

Monetary Policy – Next steps vs. finish line
Caio Megale
Economic activity and inflation are softening, and the 
risk is that they continue to surprise to the downside. 
The so-long forecasted deceleration of domestic demand 
is finally showing up in recent data, reinforcing our call of 
zero or negative GDP growth in the coming quarters. At the 
same time, recent IPCA readings show that the second 
stage of disinflation – related to services and core 
measures – is progressing.
This dynamic occurs with real interest rates still well 
above its neutral level (we consider 5.0% to be the long-
term equilibrium rate).
Against this backdrop, we see no reason for the 
Copom to consider reducing the easing pace in its next 
two or three meetings. As a matter of fact, we see a 75-
pbs cut more likely than a 25-bps, though none of them look 
likely.

Foreword – Geopolitical tensions have an ambiguous impact on 
Brazil
Global inflows into EM have reached record highs in 2026, boosting asset prices across different regions. In Brazil, the BRL 
strengthened further and the Ibovespa is up 20% year-to-date. Nominal interest rates declined as the Brazilian Central Bank 
(BCB) signaled the beginning of an easing cycle starting in March.
At the same time, global geopolitical tensions have intensified following the escalation of the US-Iran conflict. Oil prices are 
now roughly 30% up year-to-date, reaching around USD 80 per barrel. It remains difficult to assess whether these tensions will 
reverse EM inflows due to rising risk aversion; or, instead, reinforce them, particularly toward commodity-exporting emerging 
markets such as Brazil.
For now, we have maintained our Brent price assumption at USD 60 per barrel, given the high degree of uncertainty surrounding 
this outlook. A higher oil price scenario would boost Brazilian exports and fiscal revenues, while also exerting upward pressure 
on inflation (see table below).
In such an environment, global factors dominate market dynamics and idiosyncratic domestic drivers tend to lose explanatory 
power. Nonetheless, domestic fundamentals remain important, as they are likely to return to investors’ radar screens once (and 
if) global winds begin to fade.
On the domestic front, we see positive news, but also some relevant risks.
Inflation has remained benign, supported by declining prices of industrialized goods (reflecting cheaper imports from China) 
and foodstuff (helped by the stronger BRL and solid grain production). Economic activity experienced a soft patch in the second 
half of 2025 but is already showing signs of a gradual rebound. The BCB has felt comfortable signaling upcoming rate cuts, 
while maintaining a cautious tone given the scale of fiscal stimulus expected this year and the uncertainty surrounding BRL 
dynamics.
Political uncertainty is also increasing as the presidential campaign approaches. Flavio Bolsonaro’s candidacy gained 
momentum, suggesting a polarized election against President Lula. Nevertheless, an alternative center-right candidate cannot 
be ruled out. Regardless of the election outcome, the next administration will face fiscal challenges. The country continues to 
run a primary deficit even with tax revenues at record highs, which clearly points to the need to reform public spending 
dynamics.
Against this backdrop, we have not made material changes to our forecasts. In addition to the aforementioned oil price, another 
key variable is the exchange rate. Our structural models suggest that levels close to 5 reais per dollar are broadly consistent 
with fundamentals, particularly in such a supportive global environment. However, considering the political and fiscal risks 
ahead, it is reasonable to anticipate some increase in the risk premium. Accordingly, we keep our year-end 2026 forecast at 
5.60 reais per dollar.
Moreover, we project GDP growth of 2.0%, IPCA inflation at 3.8%, and that the BCB will deliver five consecutive 50-bp rate cuts 
starting this month, followed by a pause for evaluation, leaving the Selic rate at 12.50%. If oil prices remain on the rise, however, 
the BCB may opt to begin the easing cycle more cautiously, with a 25-bp cut.

BRAZIL – Wait and See
Political environment – Flávio Bolsonaro seeks to resolve pending issues, while the 
government tries to react to the polls
Paulo Gama and XP Politics
March is expected to keep the political spotlight on the early steps of Senator Flávio Bolsonaro’s pre-campaign and on the 
government’s response to the unfavorable opinion polls that followed the Carnival period.
Having consolidated his position among opposition voters, Flávio begins the month seeking to resolve pending issues. These 
include negotiations over state-level campaign alliances and the presentation of a government program intended to convey 
greater solidity to his candidacy. Topics such as the economy and public security are likely to gain prominence, while the 
Senator continues his search for a potential future Finance Minister, to be announced in the coming months.
Before that, however, the immediate focus lies on efforts to reestablish ties with figures considered strategically important for 
the campaign. After repairing relations with Pastor Silas Malafaia, of the Assembleia de Deus church, and appearing alongside 
the governor of São Paulo, Tarcísio de Freitas, a gesture of reconciliation between the senator and former First Lady Michelle 
Bolsonaro still remains pending. Such a rapprochement is widely regarded as essential for her to resume nationwide political 
engagements at the head of PL Mulher (the women’s wing of the Liberal Party – Partido Liberal), with the aim of attracting 
female and conservative voters, particularly in Brazil’s Northeast.
Another topic expected to gain momentum throughout the month is the fallout involving the subpoena of the records of 
President Lula’s son, known as Lulinha, within the investigation into fraud involving the INSS (Brazil’s National Social Security 
Institute), which has already been authorized by the Supreme Court (STF). From the perspective of Flávio’s pre-campaign, the 
bet is that the content of these documents could fuel the narrative used against the Planalto Palace on the campaign trail.
Beyond the INSS case, the political environment in Brasília remains affected by investigations involving Banco Master. The 
reassignment of the case rapporteur at the STF — now under Justice André Mendonça — has heightened concerns among 
political actors about the possibility of a plea-bargain agreement (in Brazilian criminal law, suspects may cooperate with 
authorities in exchange for reduced penalties).
Meanwhile, the government begins the month trying to understand why its approval ratings remain broadly unchanged from 
the levels observed at the end of last year. This occurs despite public security receiving less attention in the political debate —
compared with the end of the previous year — alongside favorable economic indicators and the implementation of a personal 
income tax exemption for individuals earning up to BRL 5,000 per month, as well as a reduction for those earning up to BRL 
7,350.
Aware of the gap between voters’ perception of the economy and the underlying economic indicators, the presidential 
administration’s strategy is to emphasize an aspirational policy agenda, particularly progress in discussions on revising 
working-time regulations and the proposal of free public bus transportation.
Although the issue of public-transport fares remains at a preliminary stage within the government, the proposal to eliminate 
the 6×1 work schedule (a labor arrangement common in Brazil involving six consecutive working days followed by one day of 
rest) has gained traction in Congress and could be approved in the Chamber of Deputies before the electoral period. The 
government is expected to use the month of March to reinforce its proposal with lawmakers, based on three pillars: reducing 
the schedule to 5×2, establishing a 40-hour workweek, and ensuring no reduction in wages.
There are also ongoing political discussions surrounding the first bimonthly fiscal report on revenues and expenditures, 
scheduled to be presented on March 22. Uncertainty remains regarding revenues, particularly the portion expected from the 
sale of the federal government’s rights in production-sharing contracts. Against this backdrop, the government is also 
discussing the possibility of issuing a decree imposing the IOF (Tax on Financial Transactions) on tax-exempt securities 
(financial instruments in Brazil currently exempt from income tax, such as certain infrastructure or agribusiness bonds), 
although the details and scope of the measure remain under internal discussion.

Monetary Policy – Fiscal policy and oil prices 
pose risks to the easing cycle ahead
Caio Megale
Easing cycle on the horizon. The inflation outlook remains 
benign, despite the upward surprise in February IPCA-15 (see 
inflation section above). Economic activity experienced a soft 
patch in the second half of 2025 but is already showing signs 
of a gradual rebound. In this context, the BCB felt comfortable 
signaling a rate cut in March, which will likely be followed by 
additional cuts in the coming months. Nevertheless, the 
monetary authority maintained a conservative tone amid 
government stimulus expected for this year and the 
uncertainty surrounding the BRL dynamics.
We continue to expect the Copom to begin the easing cycle 
this month, followed by a pause in the second half to evaluate. 
Our baseline scenario assumes five consecutive 50-bp cuts, 
bringing the Selic rate to 12.50%. In real terms, this would place 
the policy rate at around 8.0% - still above what we consider 
the neutral level – reflecting the fiscal challenges anticipated 
for the next presidential term.
Geopolitical tension is a key risk. The US-Iran conflict has 
pushed oil prices higher, currently about 30% above our 
baseline assumption (80 vs 60 dollar per barrel). If oil prices 
continue to rise, the BCB may opt to begin the easing cycle 
more moderately, with a 25-bp cut.
Balance of risks for inflation becomes more uncertain in the 
second half of the year. Climatic and other cyclical factors that 
boosted food supply are unlikely to repeat, while the BRL – and 
other Brazilian assets – tend to face pressure during election 
period. Thus, if the economy responds to electoral stimulus (as 
we believe it will), the inflation outlook for 2027 could 
deteriorate.
Fiscal sustainability represents an additional headwind for the 
2027 balance of risks. As discussed in the previous sections, 
our baseline scenario assumes that the next government will 
implement expenditure-side measures, but not at a scale 
sufficient to stabilize the debt-to-GDP ratio. Under this 
scenario, we see room for the central bank to resume its 
easing cycle next year, albeit with limited scope.
To sum up, we see the Selic rate at 12.50% by YE2026 and 
11.00% by YE2027.
Alternative scenarios will largely depend on fiscal policy after 
2027. If even the “insufficient adjustments” embedded in our 
baseline scenario fail to materialize, the Central Bank is unlikely 
to have room for further rate cuts next year. Conversely, a more 
comprehensive fiscal adjustment could allow the Selic rate to 
return to its neutral level – which we estimate at around 5.5% 
in real terms.

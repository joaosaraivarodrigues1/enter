The short-term global environment remains positive for emerging markets. The Fed will likely 
cut rates again in December. Institutional and geopolitical risks remain, however. A potential 
fall in energy and grain prices also poses a risk for commodity exporters as Brazil.
• 
Politics in November will focus on the trajectory of President Lula's popularity recovery and 
the outcome of the STF trial that convicted Bolsonaro, while the government tries to advance 
its economic agenda in Congress to ensure compliance with the 2026 fiscal target.
• 
Domestic activity has lost momentum in the second half of the year, in line with restrictive 
monetary policy. Nonetheless, income and credit stimuli should increase demand going 
forward. We project GDP will grow 2.1% in 2025 and 1.7% in 2026 (biased upwards).
• 
We see the government meeting its primary fiscal target this year and, with the help of 
extraordinary revenues, coming close to the lower bound of the tolerance range next year. 
However, government debt will continue to rise, reaching 83.6% of GDP in 2026.
• 
Our exchange rate projections remain at 5.30 reais per dollar for YE 2025 and 5.50 for YE 
2026. In the external accounts, better-than-expected exports have led to higher trade balance 
forecasts this year and next. However, an upward bias for local activity in 2026 is a risk.
• 
The favorable dynamics of goods inflation are expected to persist next year. As a result, we 
have lowered our projection for the 2025 IPCA inflation from 4.6% to 4.5%. For 2026, we 
project IPCA at 4.2%, compared to 4.5% in our previous monthly report.
• 
The inflation dynamics have been better than anticipated. But the medium-term balance of 
risks is worsening due to fiscal expansionary measures. We continue to see the Selic rate at 
12.00% by the end of 2026, after six consecutive 50-bp cuts starting in March.

Foreword – Short-term optimism, medium-term worries
The short-term scenario remains favorable for Brazilian asset prices. Globally, growth is losing steam but without 
recession, opening room for monetary policy easing in many regions – particularly in the US. Commodity prices have been 
volatile but remain at favorable levels.
Trade negotiations reinforced that a trade war should not be too harmful to global economic growth. Moreover, AI 
investment remains strong, keeping growth at a solid (though slower) pace.
In this environment, equity indexes have reached record highs across the globe.
In Brazil, growth is finally accommodating, driven by tight monetary policy. Inflation continues to trend down, benefiting 
from stronger BRL, lower gasoline prices, solid food production, and favorable weather conditions. Inflation expectations 
are falling gradually but consistently, indicating that the Central Bank’s persistence is paying off. The room for monetary 
easing next year seems clearer now, as reflected in the bull flattening of the futures market yield curve.
Analysts should take this positive environment, however, with a pinch of salt.
Globally, geopolitical, fiscal, and institutional risks are mounting, as we highlight in the ‘Global backdrop’ section below. In 
Brazil, the government is planning a relevant fiscal and quasi-fiscal expansion for next year, what should keep debt 
dynamics under pressure.
Our simulations suggest a significant reacceleration of economic activity as the stimulus kicks in, which should limit 
disinflation next year. The extent of the easing cycle, then, depends on the outlook for 2027 – the Central Bank´s relevant 
horizon by mid next year. The outlook for 2027, in turn, is contingent on the 2026 presidential election.
For the Selic rate to move closer to its neutral level – around 5.5% in real terms, as per our estimates – fiscal reforms that 
reduce the pace of expenditure growth are key. Without them, rising government debt may revive the debate over fiscal 
dominance that took place in Q4 2024.

BRAZIL – Copom remains cautious 
amid risky global optimism
Political environment – Lula's popularity, 
outcome of Bolsonaro's trial, and the 
economic agenda in Congress
Paulo Gama and XP Politics
In November, the political focus will remain on the trajectory of 
President Lula's recovery in popularity, following the police 
operation in Rio de Janeiro at the end of October.
Since the announcement of Brazil's inclusion in the tariff hike 
promoted by President Donald Trump — and an unsuccessful 
strategy undertaken by the opposition following that 
movement — the Brazilian leader recovered the positive 
approval ratings registered last year. This growth was also 
boosted by the easing of inflation, the advancement of the 
Income Tax reform on the public agenda, and a vacuum in the 
opposition since Tarcísio de Freitas decided to remove himself 
from the national scene.
Attention is now focused on understanding whether the 
negative impact that the Rio operation shows in this recovery 
trajectory will be sustained, or whether Lula's popularity will 
grow once again, after the intense focus of this initial moment, 
indicating greater resilience among the electorate.
The government, in the meantime, has been working to put 
back on the agenda topics that reinforce its narrative and 
boost Lula’s popularity. The President will rely on the publicity 
from the approval of the Income Tax exemption for those 
earning up to BRL 5,000 to reinforce the message that will drive 
next year's campaign.
Still in the electoral arena, there is also attention on the 
outcome of the trial that convicted Bolsonaro in the coup plot 
process. The former President has already filed appeals 
against the conviction, and the expectation is that they will be 
rejected by the Supreme Court First Panel — which opens the 
path for the execution of the sentence, with the possibility it 
will be served in a closed regime. Even so, his defense is 
considering the possibility of filing new types of appeals with 
the intention of overturning the conviction. Regardless of its 
legal viability, politically the new appeal, if confirmed, could 
give momentum to the strategy of part of the “Bolsonarista” 
group to insist on the possibility of the former President 
retaining influence over 2026 and further delaying the decision 
about his endorsement in next year's electoral race.
In this scenario, Governor Tarcísio de Freitas appears to 
continue seeing incentives to remain distant from the public 
confrontation, at least in the short term.

Returning to Congress, with the Income Tax reform approved, 
the government will focus on the revenue-raising and 
expenditure containment measures that are still in discussion.
With the rejection of Provisional Measure 1303, the 
government adopted the strategy of splitting the proposal, 
according to the level of resistance of each topic. The idea of 
discussing the taxation of exempt bonds, a point perceived as 
having the greatest resistance among congressmen, has been 
abandoned. However, there is still discussion about the 
possibility of tightening the rules for the issuance of some of 
these bonds.
Another chapter of the Provisional Measure — the one dealing 
with adjustments to Pé-de-Meia program and the seguro-
defeso benefit (an unemployment insurance paid to fishermen 
during the closed fishing season) and imposing restrictions on 
tax offsets — remains alive in a text already approved in the 
Lower House and under discussion in the Senate. A part of it 
(the one that imposes a budgetary limitation on the payment 
of the seguro-defeso benefit), was even sent via another 
Provisional Measure so that it could be computed in the 
bimonthly report on revenues and expenditures, to be 
published at the end of November. These points were seen as 
having the least difficulty in discussion with Congress, and 
they are expected to be approved by the end of the year.
There is also a third chapter, with an uncertain outcome. It 
deals with the taxation of betting companies, the Income Tax 
increase on JCP (Interest on Equity), and the increase in the 
CSLL rate on Fintechs. Senator Renan Calheiros, who reported 
on the Income Tax reform in the Senate, presented a bill 
addressing these topics, but there is still no decision on what 
the government will decide. The topics faced resistance during 
the processing of the Provisional Measure 1303, which is 
expected to be repeated in this new negotiation.
There is also the beginning of discussions on the bill that deals 
with the review of tax benefits — whose revenue is important 
for the government to maintain the expectation of meeting the 
2026 primary surplus target.
Furthermore, November is also expected to see the 
continuation of political discussions about the 2026 budget — 
embedded in the electoral context. Representatives and 
senators want the government's guarantee that pork barrel 
policies will be executed at the beginning of the year, thus 
preventing the Planalto Palace from being able to favor allies 
before the start of next year's race. Although it resists making 
this commitment, the government may be forced to make 
some concession to congressmen to be able to adjust the 
2026 Budget Guidelines Law (LDO) in a way that allows it to 
manage the budget based on the lower band of the surplus 
target, and not the center of the target.

The expiration of Provisional Measure 1303/25 demands further 
efforts on the revenue side for next year. The measures to 
increase tax collection contained in MP 1303/25, which has 
expired, would boost revenue by an estimate of BRL 20 billion. 
Following the expiration, the government included the least 
controversial measures of the MP - such as limiting tax 
compensation, increasing CSLL rates for digital banks, and 
increasing income tax on interest on equity capital (IoE) – in 
another bill. We have incorporated these changes into our 
projection and now expect a deficit of BRL 61.6 billion (0.5% of 
GDP) - BRL 58.3 billion (0.4% of GDP) previously. Considering the 
legal exemptions, this means a deficit of BRL 11.8 billion (0.1% 
of GDP). Thus, in order to reach the lower bound of the target 
range (zero), additional efforts to increase revenue and/or cut 
expenses remain necessary.

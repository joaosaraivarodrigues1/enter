Global uncertainty increased significantly after the US "Liberation Day". Central banks 
became more cautious. Two trends have consolidated, however: a weaker US dollar 
and lower oil prices. These are conflicting drivers for Brazil.
• 
In politics, recent signals indicate that the government remains focused on finding a 
positive agenda, using growth supporting measures to recover approval ratings.
• 
In Brazil, resilient labor market and government stimuli reinforce our scenario that the 
economy should not decelerate much in coming quarters. We expect GDP to grow by 
2.3% in 2025 and 1.5% in 2026.
• 
Lower oil prices will reduce fiscal revenue in 2025 and 2026, requiring additional effort 
to achieving the primary result target. The government Budged Guidelines Proposal 
(PLDO 2026) reinforces that the current fiscal framework is unsustainable in the long 
term.
• 
We have revised our exchange rate forecast from 6.00 to 5.80 reais per dollar for YE 
2025, reflecting a weaker US dollar. On the external front, we raised our projection for 
the current account deficit this year from 3.2% to 3.4% of GDP, considering lower oil 
prices.
• 
Given lower commodity prices and stronger BRL, we reduced our 2025 IPCA forecast 
from 6.0% to 5.7%. We kept 2026 forecast at 4.7%, with lower Selic rate and growth 
supportive measures offsetting less inertia from 2025.
• 
We believe the incoming data will convince Copom to raise the Selic rate one last time 
in June, to 15.00%. Interest rates should remain at this level for a long time thereafter. 
The global scenario and the probability of fiscal reforms will dictate the timing and 
intensity of monetary easing in 2026.

Foreword – Stagflation, then what?
It seems a consensus that the US economy will go through a period of stagflation in the next 6-12 months, on the back of 
Trump’s tariff hikes. But what comes next? Some believe in a period of rapid recovery, boosted by expansionary policies on 
both the monetary - the Fed would probably cut rates, even with inflation way above target - and fiscal fronts (the extension 
of tax cuts in Congress).  Others believe in a prolonged tariff turmoil, with more long-lasting consequences on global 
productivity.
In any case, it is negative short term, followed by a quite uncertain medium-term. US assets do not look attractive in this 
scenario, and investors are looking for alternative safe havens. It could be Europe, Japan or even… Latam. Indeed, the 
continent could benefit from higher Chinese demand for its commodities.
Even amid this cloudy environment, some trends are becoming clearer. The US economy should fall into recession, and the 
US dollar should stay weaker for longer (including against EM currencies). Commodity prices stabilized at lower levels. 
Stronger currencies and lower commodities should reduce goods inflation in many regions.
Against this backdrop, the Brazilian Central Bank needs to hike the Selic rate by less than previously forecasted. We have 
recently reduced the terminal Selic rate from 15.50% to 15.00%. However, we still see inflationary pressures going forward, on 
the back of still strong economic activity and fiscal stimulus measures announced recently. Thus, even reducing our IPCA 
forecast for 2025 (to 5.70% from 6.0%), we kept the projection for 2026 at 4.7%, above the upper bound of the target range.
Looking further ahead, the need for deeper fiscal reforms following the 2026 elections is getting clearer. This should be a key 
element for Brazilian asset prices as elections approach.

Paulo Gama, Bianca Lima e João Paulo Machado
In politics, recent signals indicate that the government remains 
focused on finding a positive agenda, using growth supporting 
measures to recover approval ratings - beyond those already 
presented at the start of the year.
Spillover effects from irregularities found in INSS payments — 
particularly if a Congressional inquiry takes place — may 
further intensify such efforts, as means of damage control.
Of note are ongoing deliberations over a credit line targeted at 
delivery workers and an energy sector reform, whose key 
feature includes expanding tariff exemptions for low-income 
families. Health and housing could also be targeted areas.
We expect Congress to start the debate on proposals 
submitted in the first quarter in May.
May will also be marked by legislative proceedings on the 
income tax reform. Public hearings are expected to broaden 
the debate, but early positioning from lawmakers involved in 
the discussion signal there should be little resistance to the 
BRL 5,000 exemption proposal. Debate is likely to intensify on 
how to offset the consequential revenue losses.
The committee’s rapporteur mentioned the possibility of 
taxing dividends to foreign investors. There are also ongoing 
discussions on raising the minimum taxable income threshold, 
as well as on the withholding tax on dividends, in addition to 
shifting revenue losses from subnational entities to the federal 
government.
Another theme gaining traction in Congress is the provisional 
measure that revised the rules for granting payroll-deductible 
loans to private sector workers. The measure was formally 
introduced and began to be debated in early May.
It is also worth noting that the legislative routine — particularly 
in the Lower House — is expected to remain affected by the 
opposition’s efforts to delay proceedings, as an attempt to 
pressure the House leadership into scheduling a vote on the 
amnesty bill for those involved in the January 8 events.
Meanwhile, the economic team is working to leverage the first 
bimonthly revenue and expenditure report — set to be released 
on May 22 — to reinforce the government’s commitment to

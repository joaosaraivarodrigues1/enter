Foreword – Positive short-term, cloudy medium-term
The short-term scenario for Brazil remains favorable. Globally, US president Donald Trump continues to generate geopolitical 
noise, prompting investors to increase exposure to EM assets. The BRL has appreciated some 4% YTD on the back of this 
movement.
Domestically, economic activity has lost some momentum but remains solid, supported by a strong labor market. In fact, we 
raised our 2026 GDP growth forecast to 2.0% from 1.7%. Inflation readings have been subdued, benefiting from a stronger BRL, 
solid food production in the local market, and cheap industrialized goods imported from China. Accordingly, despite the 
stronger growth outlook, we reduced our 2026 IPCA forecast to 3.8% from 4.0%.
Fiscal policy remains along the same lines. Revenues are growing briskly, outpacing the upward trend in expenditure. This 
should allow the government to meet its fiscal targets with relative ease – although this is of limited relevance, as the debt-to-
GDP ratio will remain on the rise. Quasi-fiscal expenditures are also increasing, as typically observed in an electoral year.
Thus, fiscal execution should not bring much market volatility this year.
For 2027, however, it is a different story. Fiscal reforms, particularly on the expenditure side, will be critical for Brazil’s economic 
performance during the next presidential term (2027-2030). At present, we have little visibility regarding potential candidates, 
let alone whether the eventual winner will implement the necessary reforms.
Moreover, fiscal stimulus in 2026 may give rise to renewed inflationary pressures in 2027, once (and if) the positive supply-side 
effects on consumer prices fade. That could limit the room for the easing cycle that is about to begin.
We continue to see the central bank cutting the Selic rate by 50 bps in March and in the following meetings, until the policy rate 
reaches 12.50%. Beyond this level, we believe a clearer (and more positive) scenario for 2027 onwards is required.

BRAZIL – Stronger growth, lower inflation
Political environment – Legislative year begins with a focus on the elections
Paulo Gama and XP Politics
The resumption of activities in Brasília in February — marking the effective start of the political year — takes place amid rising 
electoral tension. Below, we outline the outlook and key discussions on the national stage that begin in February and are 
expected to extend throughout the year.
Congress
The government began the legislative year by seeking a renewed rapprochement with congressional leadership. Both Hugo 
Motta and Davi Alcolumbre are working toward returning to the presidencies of the House of Representatives and the Senate, 
respectively, in 2027. The Planalto Palace assesses that the overlap with the electoral calendar could give Lula an advantage.
Nonetheless, the two lawmakers are expected to continue sending non-linear signals regarding alignment with the 
government's agenda, given the right-wing majority in both legislative houses — suggesting continuity with the stance adopted 
during the first year of their terms.
On the priority agenda, the government is considering advancing discussions on the proposal to revise the 6x1 workweek during 
the first half of the year. At the same time, the opposition remains focused on creating points of friction for the Planalto Palace, 
mainly through the advancement of public security themes and the INSS Joint Parliamentary Inquiry (CPMI).
Government electoral strategy
As this is Lula's final election, the Workers' Party (PT) is expected to center its campaign on aspirational themes, seeking to 
reconnect with its social base and secure support for the party in the post-Lula era. Labor-related proposals and the 
reinforcement of the narrative around tax justice are expected to feature prominently among campaign promises in a potential 
re-election bid. The government also bets on the economy as a key variable among non-partisan voters, especially the impact 
of inflation on the cost of living. The commitment to centrist alignment, symbolized by the ticket with Geraldo Alckmin, is 
expected to be preserved.
Right-wing candidates
In the first round, four candidates currently position themselves in opposition to Lula: Senator Flávio Bolsonaro (PL), Governor 
Romeu Zema (Novo) — whose name is also mentioned as a potential vice-presidential candidate — Renan Santos (Missão), 
and the nominee to be launched by the PSD. While Gilberto Kassab’s party is currently backing the pre-candidacy of Paraná 
Governor Ratinho Jr., a medium-term replacement by Goiás Governor Ronaldo Caiado cannot be ruled out. A final decision is 
likely to be postponed until closer to the formal nomination deadline.
As the political heir to his father's voter base, Senator Flávio Bolsonaro emerges as the leading figure within the right-wing bloc 
to potentially reach a second round against Lula. His challenge will be to reduce his rejection rate — the highest among the pre-
candidates — while positioning himself as the most moderate member of the Bolsonaro family. To mitigate perceptions of a 
radical candidate, the plan is to announce the vice-presidential nominee, the government program, and the prospective finance 
minister as early as the first half of the year.
Instability
The coming months should also be marked by ongoing investigations that continue to generate apprehension in Brasília. These 
include developments related to the Master bank, the potential extension of the INSS CPMI, and inquiries into the allocation of 
pork-barrel expenditures. Beyond raising institutional tensions, the resurfacing of these issues on the eve of the electoral period 
is likely to be exploited during the race.

the consolidation of robust harvests support a scenario of 
abundant food supply. Recent highlights include significant

both in spot and futures contracts. Second, the risk of an El 
Niño event in June has increased, which could jeopardize food 
production in the second half of the year.

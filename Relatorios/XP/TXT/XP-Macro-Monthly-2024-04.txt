Foreword – The uncertainty highlighted by the Copom
After its latest meeting, the Brazilian Monetary Policy Committee (Copom) highlighted “heightened uncertainty and 
the need for more flexibility in the conduct of monetary policy.” These uncertainties also make our job of 
forecasting the Brazilian economic outlook more difficult.
Globally, it is difficult to assess how much space DM central banks have to ease monetary policy this year. We still 
see the Fed cutting rates in the second half, but probably by less than initially expected. In Europe, risks are more 
balanced, and the ECB seems to have more space to cut. In China, in turn, activity appears to be recovering, but 
still amid high volatility and risks.
Against this backdrop, the BRL is not strengthening as we and most market participants expected at the beginning 
of the year.
Domestic factors also play a role. Despite better short-term economic forecasts highlighted in our previous report, 
pressure for higher fiscal and quasi-fiscal expenditures are mounting amid lowering government’s approval. 
Questioning about the relationship between the Executive branch and state-owned companies have also risen.
Higher expenditures tend to keep domestic demand heated and services inflation under pressure.
In our view, it is getting (even) more challenging for the Copom to meet its 3.0% inflation target next year.
Having said that, we haven’t made relevant changes to our central forecasts this month. We still see GDP growth 
at 2.0% in 2024, IPCA inflation of 3.5%, the Selic rate reaching 9.0%, and the BRL ending the year at 4.70 reais to 
the dollar.
But our confidence in those forecasts has fallen somewhat since last month.

April 2023
Foreword – We have the fiscal framework. Now what?
The Brazilian government announced the much-awaited fiscal framework that will replace the spending
cap. As its forerunner, the rule stablishes a cap for expenditures real growth rate, that will vary from
0.6% to 2.5% (instead of 0%). The government will also pursue a primary balance path that reaches
1.0% surplus by the end of Lula’s administration.
Good news: there is a limit for fiscal expansion ahead. Not so good news: this limit is wide enough for
fiscal policy to remain expansionary, unless a significant (and uncertain) increase in tax burden comes.
For monetary policy, we believe the new framework brings some visibility to the Copom, which is
positive. But we are not sure that it will lead to much lower interest rates over time. Particularly because
quasi-fiscal policy also tends to be expansionary going forward.
Thus, we see now the easing cycle starting earlier than expected because of the deceleration in credit
markets, but we still see the terminal Selic rate at 11.00%. Implicit in our Selic forecast are the
hypothesis that i) the Monetary Council will adjust the inflation target for 2024 to 4.5%; and/or ii) the
Copom will ultimately opt for a longer convergency to the target (2025).
Inflation, on its turn, remains challenging. We lifted our IPCA forecast this year to 6.x% (6.0% before);
and to 5.0% (4.5%).
Last but not least, despite the uncertainties on the banking system, the global economy continues
positive for Brazil. Europe and US central banks are probably approaching the end of the tightening
cycle; and China’s economy seems to be gaining traction, providing support for commodity prices.

Global backdrop: Inflation remain 
at the spotlight despite banking 
turmoil
Aggressive shift in the yield curve. After the outbreak in the global 
banking sector, swap markets shifted aggressively from pricing 
the Fed Funds rate "higher for longer" to pricing the start of the 
easing cycle as soon as July this year. Concerns of a systematic 
crisis have eased since central banks have acted promptly, and 
expectations have partially shifted back (graph). We expect 
concerns regarding the financial system to gradually fade, and 
lingering high inflation coupled with the tight labor market will still 
concern policymakers. However, the Fed is now expected to end 
its monetary tightening cycle soon. Our base case scenario is that 
the Fed delivers one more 25 bps hike, reaching the terminal rate 
of 5.25%. Also, we expect the easing cycle to start by the first 
quarter of next year as the disinflation process advances, and we 
see the upper bound of the Fed funds rate reaching 3.5% by the 
end of 2024.
Resilient activity and stubborn inflation, but the tide is changing. 
The US economy showed unexpected resilience at the beginning 
of the year. However, we highlight that financial conditions have 
only recently entered restrictive territory, and, considering the 
lagged effect of monetary policy on the aggregate economy, the 
impact should become increasingly evident. Core inflation still 
hovers way above the Fed’s target, which tends to keep monetary 
authorities alert. We believe that demand is finally losing steam, 
and with one additional rate hike in May, the inflation outlook will 
improve in the second half of the year.
In Europe, additional tightening will be required amid sticky 
inflation. Since the banking turmoil, the ECB has made its forward 
guidance vague, although we believe that additional hikes of 25 
bps will still be required in the upcoming meetings. It is clear from 
the data that core inflation remains stickier than anticipated, 
which is likely to keep policymakers hawkish. We believe that the 
ECB will still need to hike another 75-100 bps in order to reach its 
medium-term goal of price stability.
Economic activity in China has started to gain ground. Since 
China's announced the end of strict Covid restrictions, its 
economic activity was expected to accelerate aggressively due to 
the three yearlong pent-up demand and ongoing government 
stimulus. Despite mixed signs at the beginning of the year, recent 
data has started to show more evidence that the Chinese 
economy is strengthening. Retail sales, industrial production, and 
the PMI index have shown consistent expansions. Moreover, 
confidence indicators have improved since the beginning of the 
year. This information is consistent with our expectations that 
China's GDP will expand by around 5.6% in 2023. In addition, 
China's growth is expected to have positive spillover effects on 
other regions, mainly for China's trade partners.

Brazil: Fiscal rule brings visibility, 
but not necessarily stability
Economic Activity – Following the 
slowdown script
The Brazilian economy is decelerating gradually, as expected. 
Domestic activity has been weakening since 3Q22, reflecting the 
lagged and cumulative effects of the contractionary monetary 
policy. And this trend should not be reversed in the short term.
Thus, we continue to forecast a 1% gain for 2023 GDP, after an 
expansion of 2.9% in 2022.
On the one hand, a sharp rise in agriculture and income impulses 
is expected. We consider that expectation of modest GDP growth 
– our baseline scenario since mid-last year – as balanced. Soaring 
agricultural activities and higher household disposable income 
should prevent a more pronounced slowdown in local economic 
activity this year. Regarding the former, we anticipate an 8.3% 
jump for the primary sector’s GDP (contribution of around 0.5pp 
to total GDP), driven especially by the record grain harvest. Along 
with this, external demand for Brazilian goods is expected to 
remain solid, boosted by China’s economic recovery. With respect 
to disposable income, our updated calculations point to a real 
advance of 3.5% in the current year. The massive government 
cash transfer to vulnerable families (‘Bolsa Família’ welfare 
program plays a major role) and wage recomposition explain 
much of that projected increase. For more information, see our 
report XP Macro Special: Rising household disposable income will 
prevent a sharper slowdown in consumption throughout 2023.
On the other hand, financial conditions became even tighter. In 
addition to the high benchmark interest rate, adverse shocks in 
the Brazilian credit and capital markets coupled with turmoil in the 
global banking system led to more restrictive financial conditions 
in the recent period. We emphasize the increased risk perception 
and spreads under pressure. For instance, credit concessions to 
companies (with non-earmarked resources) have dropped 
significantly in recent months. Expectations of lower corporate 
profitability amid the cooling of activity, higher funding costs and 
growing indebtedness are the main reasons behind this worrying 
dynamic. In our view, the additional tightening of financial

recovery path, but clearly losing steam at the margin – we predict 
some stabilization in real wages from 2Q23 onwards.
Fiscal Policy – New fiscal rule brings 
predictability, but does not ensure 
sustainability
February 2023 central government primary balance registered an 
all-time monthly low. Tax collection remained positive in February 
(R$ 159.0 billion), but non-tax revenues (such as concessions and 
dividends) fell significantly, and social security expenditures rose 
markedly. Thus, the central government registered the worst 
result for February in the historical series: BRL -41.0 billion. In turn, 
the consolidated public sector showed a deficit of BRL 26.5 billion, 
the worst result since 2017, despite the positive outcome from 
states and municipalities (BRL 11.8 billion). In the year, the public 
sector maintains a surplus of BRL 72.6 billion and, in 12 months, 
BRL 93.2 billion (0.9% of GDP).
Larger primary deficit this year. Considering the current data and 
the economic slowdown expected ahead, we now estimate a 
larger primary deficit this year: BRL 119.6 billion (-1.1% of GDP) 
from a deficit of BRL 107.4 billion (-1.0% of GDP) last month. This 
change is mainly due to lower corporate tax collections (IRPJ and 
CSLL) and less revenues from the exploration of natural resources 
(royalties and special shares).
Risks are balanced going forward. On the downside, revenues 
may fall more than expected if economic activity decelerate too 
fast or if oil prices fall (although this risk has diminished recently 
due to OPEC+ action). Regarding expenditures, the main risks are 
related to laws approved in the past, such as the national nursing 
floor and the compensation of ICMS collection losses to the 
states. On the upside, government has just announced a BRL 50 
billion increase in revenues (more details on the measures below) 
that may be effective.

Regional governments’ balance returning to positive territory. 
After registering the worst historical results in the last months of 
2022, states and municipalities showed a surplus of BRL 33.6 
billion at the beginning of this year. In part, we believe this can be 
due to gains in the collection of the states with other taxes (IPVA, 
for example) and transfers from the Union, which offset losses 
with the ICMS limit for fuel, electricity, and communications. 
Looking ahead, changes in the electricity tax base and fuels 
provide support for a partial recovery of the revenue lost last year 
due to ICMS changes. As so, we revised our forecast of regional 
governments from a deficit of BRL 3.7 billion to a surplus of BRL 
23.1 billion in 2023.
The new fiscal rule was finally introduced. In a nutshell, the new 
rule combines a spending cap with primary balance targets. 
Lower and upper limits were established for real expenditure 
growth at 0.6% and 2.5%, respectively. These limits constitute a 
“countercyclical component” of the rule, preventing expenditure 
from falling when revenue is falling or rising too much otherwise.
The government will also pursue a primary balance path for the 
coming years. The primary result targets for 2024, 2025 and 2026 
will be 0%, 0.5% and 1% of GDP.
Considering XP macro scenario, the rule is not enough to reach 
the primary balance targets or to stabilize the debt/GDP ratio. 
With economic growth around 1.5% on the average of the next few 
years and real interest rates at around 4.0%; the government will 
not be able to deliver the promised primary results or to stabilize 
debt/GDP ratio by 2026. We estimate it will only be possible under 
two joint conditions: 1) an increase in the tax burden of 1.0 to 
1.5pp of GDP; and 2) an average economic growth of 2.5%.
Discussion of tax collection measures is the next chapter of the 
fiscal rule. The government has announced that it will seek 
between BRL 100 and BRL 150 billion in additional revenues in the 
coming months. The measures should include the taxation of 
online betting, the change in subsidies for the investment of ICMS 
(which affects the IRPJ/CSLL tax base) and the taxation of 
electronic commerce that can yield more than R$ 100 billion. 
Other proposals, such as the taxation on dividends and closed-
end funds may enter a second stage, within the income tax 
reform.

We anticipate a lower current account deficit in 2023, in the wake 
of a stronger performance of the trade balance. We are revising 
our expectation for the current account deficit this year, to USD 
40.0 billion (-1.9% of GDP) from USD 50.5 billion (-2.5% of GDP). 
This revision was mainly due to better prospects for exports and, 
consequently, for the trade balance. As per our estimates, the 
moderate decline in international commodity prices will be more 
than offset by the expansion in export volume. We call attention 
to the higher export values of grains (on the heels of a record 
harvest in Brazil), iron ore and crude oil. In turn, imports are 
expected to grow at a moderate pace in 2023, in the wake of the 
cooling of domestic demand – the weakening of manufacturing 
industry takes center stage. For 2024, we foresee a current 
account balance of USD -48.0 billion or -2.3% of GDP (prior: USD -
53.0 billion or -2.6% of GDP).     
 
On the financial account side, the Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) 
remains at very high levels. Net inflows in FDI reached USD 88.0 
billion in the 12-month rolling sum up to February 2023 (4.49% of 
GDP), well above the amount of USD 50.2 billion (3.0% of GDP) 
recorded a year earlier. We maintain our projections for FDI at USD 
80.0 billion this year (3.9% of GDP) and USD 75 billion next year 
(3.6% of GDP). Our expectation of lower FDI in 2023 versus 2022 
reflects the scenario of weaker economic growth and corporate 
profitability. All-in, FDI remains robust and allows for healthy 
financing of the current account deficit.  
Monetary Policy – Easing cycle to 
start earlier, but still landing at the 
same spot
High rates for longer. The Central Bank (BCB) kept the hawkish 
tone in its recently official communication. The BCB recognizes 
that economic activity is losing steam but remains concerned 
about rising inflation expectations. Thus, it continues to signal 
that its flight plan is to keep the Selic rate at current levels for “a 
long period.”
Inflation remains high. Recent IPCA consumer price inflation 
readings continue to show that inflation and core inflation are 
hovering around 6%. Inflationary pressures are taking longer than 
expected to fade. In fact, we lifted our forecast for this year and 
for 2024 (see the Inflation section above).
Weakening credit market, the canary in the coal mine. Having said 
that, credit markets are finally feeling the effects of monetary 
tightening (see chart). Additionally, exogenous events (such as the 
problems with Lojas Americanas) hit markets over and above 
what would be expected by tight monetary policy. A weaker credit

The new fiscal framework sets (comfortable) limits for fiscal 
expansion. As discussed in the fiscal session above, the 
government’s new fiscal framework does not stabilize the debt-
to-GDP ratio but brings some visibility, as it sets limits for fiscal 
expansion ahead. Thus, the framework tends to be helpful for 
monetary policy management; though it does not necessarily 
open room for a lower interest rate over time since the fiscal 
stance will remain expansionary.
All considered, we believe now that the BCB will start a gradual 
easing cycle earlier than we previously expected. We now see a 
25-bps cut in the August Copom meeting, followed by a sequential 
50 bps cut until the Selic reaches 11.00% by the 2024 first half.
Implicit in our Selic forecast are the hypothesis that i) the 
Monetary Council will adjust the inflation target for 2024 and 
onwards to 4.5%; and/or ii) the Copom will ultimately opt for a 
longer convergency to the target (2025).
As we believe fiscal and quasi-fiscal policies will remain 
expansionary in the coming years, we did not change our terminal 
Selic rate forecast.

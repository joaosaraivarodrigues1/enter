Foreword – One eye on the team, the other on the cap
Former President Lula won 51% of the valid votes and will serve a third presidential term, starting in January 2023.
The market awaits details on the economic team and on a strategy to address short-term fiscal challenges. In
particular, how to accommodate campaign promises that increase spending and reduce revenue; and what will
replace the spending cap as the medium-term fiscal anchor.
As stressed in our previous reports, a clear, credible and tight expenditure rule is key for debt sustainability and,
therefore, for the country’s macroeconomic stability. If successful, a renewed fiscal framework will give room for
interest rate cuts in 2023 and for higher potential GDP growth. Brazilian assets would perform well.
Having said that, the pressure for fiscal expansion is massive and there seems to be no political and social
consensus that fiscal balance is the correct path. Thus, risks persist.
Risks are also persisting in the international scenario. It remains unclear how much further interest rates will rise in
developed countries, while the war in Ukraine has escalated and political uncertainties in China have increased.
Our forecasts have changed little since last month. We expect the Brazilian and world economy to rebalance, after a
period of adjustment to correct post-pandemic excesses (inflation, overheating labor market). The upcoming
events, both globally and locally, will tell if this scenario will be confirmed.

Global 
Backdrop: 
Global 
economic 
slowdown amid persistent geopolitical risks
Disinflation is likely to be accompanied by a material slowdown in 
activity. Despite inflation remaining at high levels around the globe, 
signs of disinflation have become increasingly apparent. Lower 
commodity prices, contractionary monetary policy around the world, 
and normalizing supply chains are expected to contribute to easing 
price pressures. At the same time, with tighter financial conditions and 
persistent geopolitical risks, the global economy is expected to weaken 
considerably in 2023.
The Fed raised interest rates to 4.0% in its November meeting. The 
September inflation and labor market data gave no relief for the central 
bank to decrease the pace of monetary tightening. With high inflation 
and heated labor market, the Fed continues in "whatever it takes" 
mode. Financial conditions are now becoming much tighter than what 
was expected a few months ago, and markets are pricing the terminal 
Fed Funds rate at around 5.0%.
We expect the US to enter a mild recession in 2023. GDP expanded by 
2.6% in Q3, owing chiefly to the significant positive contribution from 
net trades. Nevertheless, private investment fell, and consumption has 
continued to soften. In addition, leading indicators suggest that the US 
economy is slowing down considerably, reflecting the initial effects of 
tighter financial conditions. Given the lagged effect of monetary policy, 
the US economy should weaken further in 2023 and most likely enter 
a mild recession.
Europe is on the verge of a recession, and winter is worrying. The 
conflict between Russia and Ukraine continues to hamper terms of 
trade in European economies, causing inflation to persist at historically 
high levels. With no prospects of a resolution, it has become 
increasingly likely that European economies, which are heavily 
dependent on imports from Russia, will have a shortage of energy 
supplies during the winter. Moreover, the ECB has been hiking interest 
rates at a fast pace in an attempt to reduce inflationary pressures. The 
combination of tighter financial conditions and inflation affecting 
purchasing power will likely drive economic activity into a period of 
contraction in 2023.
The Chinese economy remains weak, and geopolitical risks increased. 
GDP grew by 3.9% in Q3 on year-on-year terms, and China registered 
growth significantly below the 5.5% target in the first three quarters of 
the year. The result reinforces that, despite the considerable amount of 
monetary and fiscal stimulus, the economy continues to slow down 
amid weakness in the property market and the persistence of the zero-
Covid policy. On the political front, President Xi Jinping extended his 
mandate for five more years, and the Chinese Communist Party gained 
significant political power. Markets reacted negatively to the news, 
reflecting concerns that China may adopt less pro-market economic 
policies and focus on strengthening its military power.

Brazil: A Fiscal Challenge for the New 
Government
Economic Activity – On a positive trajectory, with 
signs of cooling
Mild slowdown in the second half. Domestic activity remains on an 
upward trend, despite the loss of steam and greater sectoral 
heterogeneity in recent months. On the one hand, retail sales and 
industrial production have been cooling down, especially on segments 
more sensitive to credit conditions (e.g., durable goods). Meanwhile, 
the service sector maintains its leading role, driven especially by 
activities linked to household demand, which still reflects the 
normalization of the consumption pattern after the economic 
reopening.
Labor market as a support factor. The unemployment rate continues 
to fall, albeit at a more moderate pace. According to our estimates, the 
seasonally adjusted monthly rate stood at 8.5% in September, around 
3pp below pre-pandemic levels. We expect this indicator to reach 
around 8.0% by the end of this year, which already incorporates the 
deceleration in job hiring seen in recent months. In turn, real labor 
income remains on a steady recovery trend (there was an 
uninterrupted increase between May and September), in line with the 
tight labor market and the short-term decline in inflation. The average 
real income, which is still circa 4% below pre-pandemic levels, is 
expected to expand further in the coming months.

Therefore, we reiterate the expectation of a 2.8% growth for 2022 GDP. 
We estimate that headline GDP will grow 0.6% QoQ in Q3 and 0.2% QoQ

For 2023 GDP growth, our forecast remains at 1.0%. The lagged effects 
of tighter monetary policy and the deterioration of the global 
macroeconomic outlook marked by increasing risks of recession 
should be relevant obstacles to activity in Brazil over the next quarters. 
These factors may have influenced, although partially, the worsening 
of business and consumer confidence indices last month. Conversely, 
we foresee robust results in 2023 for sectors that are less sensitive to 
the economic cycle, particularly the agriculture and mining industries.
Finally, our simulations point to an increase in Brazil’s potential GDP. 
Amidst the bullish surprises coming from the activity indicators in the 
recent period as well as uncertainties on the reform agenda in the next 
presidential term, discussions about a possible greater structural 
growth are gaining strength. Hence, we carried out simulations with 
different scenarios for the investment rate, total factor productivity and 
labor market conditions. In short, we consider long-term economic 
growth between 1.4% and 1.9% to be the most likely estimate. In our 
baseline scenario, Brazil’s potential GDP grows at an annual pace of 
1.8%. For details, see the report XP Macro Special: Discussions on 
cyclical recovery and structural economic growth.

Fiscal – New government, same challenges
Short-term results remain on a positive trend. In September, federal tax 
collection grew by 4.1%, reaching another monthly high, with revenues 
related to corporate profits as the highlight. The Central Government 
recorded a surplus of BRL 11 billion, the largest for the month since 
2010, owing chiefly to revenues from dividend payments from state-
owned companies. Finally, the public sector registered a monthly 
surplus of BRL 10.7 billion, leading the 12-month accumulated figure 
to BRL 181.3 billion (1.9% of GDP).
We revised our scenario for 2022. The September results and the 
perspective that collections will remain strong in the coming months 
support the revision of our scenario in the short term. Additionally, we 
include in our forecast a lower spending due to the “pooling” of 
resources (expenses appropriated but not commited), which has been 
recurrent in recent years. Thus, we now expect a surplus of BRL 64.8 
billion for the Central Government and a surplus of BRL 145 billion 
(1.5% of GDP) for the Public Sector in 2022.

We expect an increase in expenses of at least BRL 140 billion. In 
addition, the new government should resume current expenditures 
related to cultural and recreation transfers and health programs, 
among others.
First challenge will be to change the budget for next year. To fulfill its 
promises from 2023 onwards, the new government must approve a 
constitutional amendment within the National Congress this year with 
a temporary license to raise expenses above the limit imposed by the 
existing spending cap rule. Another option is to negotiate with 
Congress to approve the budget only in 2023, which would allow the 
new government to issue a provisional measure maintaining the value 
of BRL 600 for Auxílio Brasil welfare program from January 1st and 
work on approving a new fiscal rule in the first quarter. However, the 
latter involves a high risk of discontinuity in the Public Administration.

With the increase in expenses in 2023, public accounts should return 
to deficit. We expect an increase of approximately R$140 billion in 
expenses beyond the cap for next year, but our scenario does not 
include measures that may reduce revenues, such as the changes in 
personal income tax (PIT) brackets and the PIS/Cofins tax exemption 
on fuels.
Therefore, we estimate a deficit of BRL 91.1 billion (0.9% of GDP) for 
the Central Government and a deficit of BRL 67.5 billion (0.7% of GDP) 
for the public sector in 2023.
Public debt will rise again. The positive primary result and the transfer 
of financial resources from BNDES to the National Treasury should 
reduce the General Government Gross Debt/GDP ratio to 77.5% this 
year. However, as of next year, with higher expenses and higher 
nominal interest payments, the public debt is expected to grow by 3.7 
pp., reaching 81.2% of GDP in 2023.
Risks remain very high. In addition to the new expenses mentioned, 
there is also the possibility of a more significant increase in the salaries 
of civil servants, the payment of postponed court-ordered debts, the 
compensation to the states for the reduction of ICMS on fuel and 
electricity and the financing of the nursing minimum-wage. On the 
revenue side, we consider the risk that the PIS/Cofins rates on fuels 
will remain at zero next year.
The biggest challenge will be to implement a new fiscal anchor. As we 
argued in our last monthly report, there is no room for significant fiscal 
expansion in the coming years, as public debt is at a very high level and 
expected to resume growth in 2023. Thus, even if the new government 
promotes an expansion at first, it will be necessary to implement a new 
rule that allows the country to have predictability regarding the 
trajectory of public accounts from then on.
Inflation – Decline beyond tax cuts
Recent data support our scenario of lower inflation this year and next. 
We project IPCA inflation at 5.6% for 2022 and 5.2% in 2023. The 
deflation generated by tax reductions and fuel price cuts has clouded 
the deceleration of the rest of inflation. Industrial goods and food are 
already showing relevant cost decompression that translates into a 
drop in some prices. Demand is also showing signs of deceleration.
The variation in industrialized goods is still heterogeneous but 
generally more benign. This group, which suffered from a shortage in 
supply, rising costs, and overheated demand during the pandemic, has 
now started to revert. Prices of consumer durables, which rose 20% 
between March 2020 and August this year, are now falling. In addition 
to the supply's normalization, demand has been declining due to higher 
credit costs. Semi-durable and non-durable goods, which are more 
sensitive to income, continue to rise, but with a more moderate 
variation.
With contractionary monetary policy, the likely accommodation of the 
disposable income mass, and normalization in the global chains, we 
forecast inflation of 2% for industrial goods in 2023, after rising close 
to 9% this year.
Food registered deflation in September but should rise again in 
October. Climatic factors weigh on fresh food. The group, however, will 
continue to show deflation within the animal protein and milk & dairy

this year, as this additional revenue waiver is too heavy for a new

health insurance and public transport, will still be under inflationary 
pressure to fill the gap created during the pandemic.
Despite inflation exceeding the target in 2023, the IPCA will already be 
on a convergence path over the relevant horizon. In the balance of 
risks, we attribute a slight upward bias to this year's forecast if the 
removal of the TUST/TUSD tax from the ICMS calculation basis of 
electric energy is not confirmed. For 2023, we see a downward risk due 
to fuel taxation. If the tax exemption is maintained, our projection will 
fall from 5.2% to 4.7%.

Monetary Policy – Surveillance
In our view, domestic monetary policy is already at a significantly 
restrictive level. Thus, it makes sense for the Copom to stay in a “wait-
and-see” mode.
Moreover, global disinflation should help with the committee’s task.  
Monetary policy is adjusting fast in the western world, and global 
supply chains seem to be normalizing. Thus, due to both demand and 
supply reasons, global inflation should decline in 2023.
Vigilant 
Copom. 
Global 
and 
domestic 
uncertainties 
require 
surveillance. The Copom’s flight plan seems to be keeping the Selic 
rate at 13.75% for the coming quarters and start easing monetary 
policy only (and if) inflation convergence becomes clear. Meanwhile, if 
fiscal policy becomes more expansionary, jeopardizing the expected 
path of economic slack and inflation expectations, the Selic rate may 
well be hiked again. We cannot rule out this possibility.
In any event, we believe that the scenario is sent for rates to fall next 
year. Under the hypothesis that a new and credible fiscal anchor will be 
adopted, we keep our scenario that the Selic rate will stay at 13.75% 
until mid-next year and drop to 10.00% by 2023 yearend.

Foreword – Higher uncertainty, higher risk premium
Risk premium for Brazilian asset prices increased markedly in recent months. The BRL is almost 13% weaker year to date, 
underperforming most of its peers (see chart). The future DI market is pricing in more than 200-bp hike until 2025 yearend. 
Is this movement happening out of the blue? Not really.
Last month we wrote here that macro indicators for 2024 were okay, but some concerning trends, such as primary 
expenditures growth and the heated labor market, would demand some economic policy reaction (“Will ends meet?” Brazil 
Macro Monthly, June 24).
In order to comply with the fiscal law, we estimate that the government would need to block some BRL 16 billion in 
discretionary expenditures in the second half.  Signaling from the Ministry of Finance suggest a lower effort to do so. Some 
clarity would help to reduce uncertainties and risk premium.
On the monetary front, the increasing tension between the central bank and Executive Branch brings doubts on monetary 
policy independence after Governor Campos mandate ends in December. That uncertainty will not dissipate, even with the 
Copom acting technically in the short run.
For our baseline scenario, we see some reaction from policymakers and, thus, some risk relief. But part of the damage 
should be more persistent. Thus, we adjusted our FX forecast to 5.40 reais to the dollar this year, from 5.00 before. 
Consequently, we rose our IPCA forecast to 3.8% for 2024 (3.7% before) and to 4.3% for 2025 (4.0% before).
We believe that this will not be enough for the Copom to change gears and launch a tightening cycle. We see the committee 
keeping rates at the current (restrictive) level for a long period, extending the convergency horizon to 2026.
However, if the risk premium continues to escalate and the BRL stabilizes at around 5.80 reais per dollar or weaker, inflation 
forecasts will move significantly away from the target. Under those circumstances, the Copom may opt to hike rates in the 
second half of this year.

External Sector – Sharp exchange rate 
depreciation amid worsening risk perception
Rodolfo Margato
Exchange rate at its highest levels since the end of 2021. The 
Brazilian exchange rate depreciated around 8% in June and 
13% in the year to date, a much weaker performance 
compared to emerging peers. This dynamic reflects the 
worsening risk perception regarding the conduct of domestic 
economic policy. Concerns about the sustainability of the 
fiscal framework have intensified, given the higher-than-
expected increase in mandatory spending combined with 
signs of exhaustion in the adjustment of public finances based 
on revenue-raising measures (see section on Fiscal Accounts). 
Furthermore, there is uncertainty about the credibility of 
monetary policy in the coming years, with growing doubts 
about the Central Bank’s reaction to possible political 
pressures on interest rate decisions (see section on Monetary 
Policy).

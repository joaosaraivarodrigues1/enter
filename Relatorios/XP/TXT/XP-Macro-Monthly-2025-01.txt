Foreword – Global uncertainties meet local challenges
Brazilian markets ended 2024 on stress mode, reflecting the country’s difficulty to implement a deeper fiscal adjustment. The 
measures approved in Congress will help the government to meet the formal primary fiscal targets, particularly considering 
that revenue will remain strong. But meeting the target is not enough to stabilize fiscal expectations, as: i) central government 
primary balance will remain negative due to expenditures off the legal limit; ii) quasi-fiscal expenditures are increasing; iii) 
regional governments and SOEs fiscal position has been deteriorating. Thus, debt-to-GDP ratio will remain on the rise for the 
foreseeable future.
Rightly or not – some have been arguing that this has been an exaggerated movement – the perception on the country’s fiscal 
dynamics drove risk premium of Brazilian financial assets through the roof. The BRL lost some 25% of its value last year, and 
the future markets are pricing in the Selic rate at around 16% by mid-2025. Inflation expectations are moving further away 
from the 3.0% target reflecting heated domestic demand and higher production costs (weaker FX, higher taxes, rising wages).
Will spreads remain under pressure ahead? Two elements are key to answer this question, in our view. One is the global 
environment. Part of the recent deterioration was indeed driven by Trump’s election and a slower China. The pessimism could 
be quickly reversed if Trump proves more pragmatic than expected and/or if Chinese (intense) economic stimulus works.
The other element is the Brazilian Central Bank. The BCB has been selling reserves to net FX outflow, apparently reducing BRL 
volatility (we do not have the counterfactual). But this tug-of-war cannot last indefinitely. Eventually, the monetary authority 
will allow the currency to effectively float, revealing the actual price signal. Until then, interest rate differential will not work 
properly, leaving the future path of the BRL and other Brazilian assets (even more) difficult to predict.
For now, we kept our FX forecasts close to current levels (6.2 reais to the dollar). It is weaker than our previous number (5.85), 
what – along with other elements - pushed our 2025 IPCA forecast up to 6.1% (5.2% before). The monetary policy reaction, 
therefore, should be more intense: we see the Selic rate peaking at 15.50%, and economic activity losing steam throughout 
the year. Any monetary easing would only be possible in 2026, when (and if) the FX stabilizes and inflation starts to fall.
But what about fiscal policy? If this is to blame of the observed market deterioration, wouldn’t it be an important element for 
the performance of the Brazilian economy and asset prices? In theory yes. But, at least in the short run, we do not see any 
significant news on the fiscal front. As mentioned above, our numbers suggest that meeting the official fiscal target this year 
seems quite feasible. And the government seems to believe this is enough. This is as good (or as bad) as it gets.

Short-term results do not guarantee fiscal sustainability. 
Although projections indicate that the government should 
meet the primary fiscal targets for 2024 and 2025, the long-
term fiscal dynamics remain worrisome, increasing sovereign 
risk premium and volatility in asset prices. We understand this 
can be traced down to the design of the current fiscal 
framework, which is unable to generate the primary results 
needed to stabilize public debt in the long term. Once: i) it 
depends heavily on increasing revenue, which has proven to be 
limited by political and economic constraints; ii) it allows 
strong real growth in primary central government spending; iii) 
it poses no restrictions for increased quasi-fiscal spending 
(through public funds and banks). In this sense, we believe it is 
essential to review the parameters of the fiscal framework, 
particularly the growth allowed for total spending (2.5% above 
inflation), combined with reforms that limit the growth of 
mandatory and quasi-fiscal expenditure. This would enable

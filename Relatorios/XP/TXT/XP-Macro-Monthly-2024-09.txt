In Brazil, GDP exceeded expectations in 2Q, driven by domestic demand. As a result, we revised 
our projection for 2024 GDP, to 3.1% from 2.7%. We see a slowdown to 1.8% in 2025 (before: 
1.6%), reflecting tighter monetary policy and the downward trend in commodity prices.
• 
We forecast the central government's primary deficit at 0.3% of GDP (previously: 0.4%) due to

increased. For 2025, increasing revenue and controlling expenditures will be challenging.
•

US provides some relief. On the other hand, fiscal risks and falling commodity prices are 
worrying. In all, we maintain our forecast of 5.40 reais to the dollar by YE24 and YE25.
•

remains challenging, due to heated demand and higher production costs (taxes, exchange rate, 
wages). We forecast inflation (IPCA) of 4.4% in 2024 and 4.0% in 2025.
• 
Strong activity and elevated inflation expectations reinforce our view that the Copom will 
implement a 150-bp tightening cycle, starting with a 25-bp hike this month. This prompt

Foreword – Swimming against the tide
This month, the Federal Reserve will most likely reduce rates for the first time in almost five years. We also expect that a rate
cut will be the next step for central banks in the Eurozone, United Kingdom, Chile, Colombia, and Mexico, among others. The
Brazilian Central Bank, in turn, will probably hike rates in its September monetary policy meeting.
Why such a disparity?
Glass half full: the tightening cycle will come as a response to stronger-than-expected growth and labor market. We have
recently revised our 2024 GDP growth forecast to 3.1% – around 3.0% for the third year in a row – and our unemployment
rate projection to 6.5% for the end of this year, the lowest level (by far) in 15 years.
Glass half empty: fiscal and quasi-fiscal policy remains expansionary, which requires an even tighter monetary policy to
balance aggregate demand and supply. Moreover, production costs are on the rise due to increases in corporate tax burden,
the weaker exchange rate, and rising labor costs.
As a result, inflation expectations are on the rise, moving further away from the target. The response from the central bank
will eventually be hiking rates. We believe that both the current and the next BCB governor – Campos Neto and Gabriel Galípolo
– will agree on that.  Lower rates abroad won’t help much in reducing domestic inflationary pressures.
Under those circumstances, some weeks ago we incorporated a moderate tightening cycle into our baseline scenario (see
here). We see the Selic rate reaching 12.00% in January, or some 8.00% in real terms.
Considering the hypothesis used by the central bank that the neutral real interest rate is around 5.00%, this tightening cycle –
along with the recent decline in commodity prices and a likely less intense fiscal impulse ahead – should be enough to
stabilize IPCA inflation or even put it on a declining trend again.
If that works, the central bank will likely find room to normalize monetary policy by late 2025 or early 2026, heading to the
9.00-10.00% neutral level. We will monitor the evolution of the economic scenario before finetuning the timing of this process.

Fundamentals signal inflationary pressures ahead. Economic
activity and the labor market have been stronger than
expected. The BRL has underperformed its peers this year.
Inflation expectations are on the rise, drifting further away from
the 3.0% target. Producer Price Index (IPA, in the Portuguese
acronym) is picking up, and should further pressure consumer
prices in the coming months. This is evidence that monetary
policy may not be sufficiently restrictive.
Monetary policy reaction. Against this backdrop, we
incorporated a few weeks ago a higher Selic path into our
baseline scenario (link). We see the Selic rate reaching 12.00%
in January 2025, after a 25-50-50-25 hiking cycle starting in
September.
Tighter monetary policy, lower commodity prices and a less
intense fiscal impulse will probably lead to a more balanced
economy ahead and, as a consequence, a stable-to-declining
inflation trend.
If we are right, the Copom may find room to normalize
monetary policy by late 2025 or early 2026, heading to the
9.00-10.00% neutral level. We will monitor the evolution of the
economic scenario before finetuning the timing of this
process.

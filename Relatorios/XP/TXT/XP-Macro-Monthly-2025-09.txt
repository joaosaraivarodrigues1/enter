Foreword – One eye on disinflation, the other on political 
developments
Economic activity and inflation recent evolution improved have been positive for monetary policy in the US and in Brasil. The 
case is clearer for the former, and the Fed will probably resume cutting rates this month. For the BCB, the outlook is more 
challenging, even with likely support from its US counterpart.
Economic activity lost some steam in the second quarter, but government growth supportive measures should spur domestic 
activity after a soft patch. 2026 is an electoral year, after all. We believe growth over the next five to six quarters will average 
around potential. Thus, one should not expect much additional disinflation ahead coming from output gap.
IPCA inflation has come out below expectations in recent month, with an encouraging breakdown. Core inflation has reversed 
its upward trend and is now running around 4.5% (after peaking at 6.0% in the first half of the year), while inflation expectations 
have started to decline. But they are still far from the 3.0% target. The next stretch toward the BCB’s goal will be more difficult, 
given that the effects of a stronger BRL are fading, the economy is only gradually slowing, and the labor market remains tight.
We continue to expect the BCB to begin a gradual easing cycle in January, bringing the Selic rate to 12.00% by the end of 
2026. Monetary policy has to remain restrictive, since fiscal policy will probably remain expansionary. Further rate cuts that 
would bring the Selic closer to its neutral level (around 9.00–10.00%, in our view) depend on the outlook for fiscal reforms 
that curb the pace of government spending in the coming years.
The probability of those reforms is related to the campaigns for 2026 presidential elections. For that reason, markets will 
continue to be sensitive to polls and other events related to the electoral process, even with elections still far away.
Other political events, such sanctions from the US government on Brazil need to be closely monitored. We believe the 50% 
trade tariffs imposed on several Brazilian products will have a limited macroeconomic impact, especially given the recently 
announced government support measures. Still, other political measures have uncertain consequences and may escalate 
going forward.

Political environment – With Bolsonaro's trial 
at the STF, September will have relevant 
stages for the 2026 electoral scenario
Paulo Gama, Bárbara Baião, Bianca Lima e João Paulo 
Machado
September in politics will be guided by relevant stages to 
define the 2026 electoral scenario.
On September 12th, the last session of the Supreme Court's 
First Panel is scheduled to judge Jair Bolsonaro in the case 
regarding the attempted coup d'état in 2023. The publication 
of the decision should be followed by the former president's 
defense filing appeals that have the potential to prolong the 
discussion and, eventually, take the case to the full court. Even 
with the possibility of the process being prolonged before it 
becomes final and unappealable, the conviction itself is a 
relevant stage because, with it, there is an expectation from the 
political establishment that Bolsonaro will gain elements that 
could, in the future, lead him to recognize the impossibility of 
running for the Planalto Palace in 2026 and facilitate the 
decision to support an alternative candidacy.
At the same time, centrist parties are expected to reinforce 
Governor Tarcísio de Freitas's participation in the efforts to 
keep the amnesty in focus throughout the month, signaling 
solidarity with the former president, in order to strengthen their 
choice as the safest path for the 2026 race. The move reflects 
the group's perception that their support for the governor — 
without Jair Bolsonaro's consent — is not enough for the 
candidacy's consolidation. The vote on the amnesty bill still 
depends on broader agreements involving the leadership of 
Congress, including its scope, which have not yet been 
finalized.
However, the pro-Tarcísio movement is not happening without 
resistance from the Bolsonaro family, especially Eduardo, who 
is expected to reinforce his claim to be chosen by his father as 
the group's representative next year — a move that could even 
be encouraged if expectations for new sanctions from the 
United States are confirmed following the conclusion of the 
Supreme Court trial. Even so, there are limits to Eduardo's 
strategy, as made clear by the actions of the centrist parties 
and the Supreme Court.
September also concentrates relevant moments for defining 
the Planalto Palace's priority agenda for the remainder of 
Lula's term. The position of congressman Arthur Lira on the 
income tax reform is expected to be defined. The proposal, 
which exempts those who earn up to BRL 5,000 per month

Brazil’s GDP loses steam amid cooling domestic demand. 
Total GDP rose by 0.4% QoQ in Q2, following a significant 1.3% 
QoQ increase in the previous quarter. This result came roughly 
in line with expectations (our estimate was 0.3% QoQ). 
Breakdown data brought mixed signals. On the supply side, the 
services sector showed resilience, with expansion in most of 
its components. Besides, activities less sensitive to the 
economic cycle – such as agriculture and the extractive 
industry – performed robustly. Meanwhile, the manufacturing 
industry and construction remained on a falling trajectory. On 
the demand side, fixed asset investment dropped after a long 
period of growth, contrasting with the strength of household 
consumption.

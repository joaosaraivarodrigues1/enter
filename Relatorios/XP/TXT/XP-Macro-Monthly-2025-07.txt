The global environment remains volatile but favorable to Brazil, with a weak US dollar 
and relatively elevated commodity prices (except for oil). The Fed should resume the 
easing cycle in the second half, which is also positive for EMs.
• 
In Brazil, the government decided to appeal to the Supreme Court after the Congress 
overturned the IOF rate hike. Meanwhile, the economic team seeks (politically viable) 
alternatives to meet the fiscal targets.
• 
Even without the additional revenue from the financial transactions tax (IOF) rate 
increase, the government should reach the lower limit of the target in 2025. For 2026 
it will be more challenging to comply with the primary result target and, especially, the 
spending limit.
• 
Global and domestic factors explain the positive performance of the BRL. We revised 
our exchange rate forecast from 5.80 to 5.50 reais per dollar for YE2025, and from 
6.10 to 5.70 for YE2026. We kept our forecast for the current account deficit at 2.9% 
of GDP.
• 
We kept our projections for GDP growth at 2.5% this year and 1.7% next year. A robust 
labor market, short-term stimulus measures, and strong performance of non-cyclical 
sectors are expected to soften the slowdown ahead.
• 
We reduced our IPCA inflation forecast to 5.0% from 5.5% in 2025, reflecting the 
stronger exchange rate and wholesale prices deflation. We also cut our number for 
2026 but by a lesser extent, from 4.7% to 4.5%. Growth-supportive measures remain 
the main risk for inflation next year.
• 
With short-term inflation improving, the BCB should gradually gain confidence that 
monetary policy efforts are paying off. We now believe that the easing cycle will start 
in January (April before). We see the Selic rate at 12,50% by YE2026, but at the risk of 
a more intense rate-cut, if the outlook for fiscal reforms after elections gains traction.

Foreword – A weaker dollar and sound monetary policy improve the 
inflation outlook
The Financial Times reported on the day we wrote this foreword that the “US dollar suffers worst start to year since 1973”. 
This is quite different from what we – and most market participants – expected after Trump’s election. The dollar has 
weakened almost monotonically, regardless of US tariffs or conflicts in the Middle East and Ukraine. This happened even 
though US Treasury yields remained significantly wider than their European and Asian peers.
Against this backdrop, the BRL strengthened by around 12%, alongside most EM and DM currencies. A stronger currency and 
lower oil prices pushed Brazil’s PPI inflation into negative territory, with benign spillovers to short-term CPI (IPCA). Indeed, our 
2025 IPCA forecast, which was 6.1% at the beginning of the year, has been revised down month after month, in line with our 
BRL assumptions, and now sits at 5.0%.
Could it go further down?  Well, our hypothesis is that the greenback will stabilize going forward. However, we must admit, 
that our recent track record on this has not been so great. The USD could weaken further if the Fed resumes the easing cycle, 
as may be the case for the second half of the year.
Beyond global forces, the BRL also benefits from a solid and responsive domestic monetary policy. The BCB acted decisively 
in December amidst a bout of market turmoil that pushed the BRL beyond 6.2 per dollar and continued to adjust the policy 
rate throughout the first half of the year. This helped to stabilize inflation expectations, although they remain above the 3.0% 
target across different available gauges.
Lower short-term inflation reduces inertia for the following year. As a result, we also revised down our 2026 IPCA forecast to 
4.5%, from 4.7%. Additionally, we brought forward the expected start of the easing cycle from April to January.
However, monetary policy continues to lean against growth-supportive measures. Since the beginning of the year, the 
government has been discussing (and implementing) measures to keep demand heated. The downward revision to our 2026 
inflation forecast would have been larger were it not for these policies.
A key assumption behind next year’s monetary easing is that fiscal policy will become more sustainable after the elections. 
In the short run, we believe the government will find some form of revenue to meet its 2025 fiscal target. For 2026, the 
challenge will be greater. Yet by then, the presidential campaign debate tends to have a larger impact on market prices than 
meeting the fiscal target for that specific year.

This conflict is set against the backdrop of anticipation for the 
2026 electoral dispute. Since the (almost) defeated attempt to 
raise IOF rates, Lula’s administration has invested in a narrative

narrative has been used by the government as a rallying cry for 
next year’s presidential campaign and has been rejected by the 
Central parties bloc.
In this context, it will be important to monitor Congress 
reaction. An official break from the government by allied 
parties is unlikely at this time. However, there is growing 
support among allies of Speaker of the House Hugo Motta for 
Congress to double down on negotiations around topics of 
interest to the Executive. In practice, this means rhetorically 
advocating for expanding social benefits to counter the 
government’s narrative.
Within this tense environment, the judicialization of the IOF 
could have collateral effects on the government’s own 
legislative agenda. There is a possibility of delays and 
adjustments in measures to offset the revenue impacts of the 
income tax reform, as well as additional friction over the 
provisional measure that completes the Finance Ministry’s 
revenue package and is expected to face strong resistance in 
the second half of the year.
From a fiscal perspective, the economic team is not ruling out 
including the projected revenue from the IOF decree in the next 
bimonthly revenue and expenditure report, scheduled for July 
22. This would add BRL 12 billion to public accounts in 2025 
and help close the fiscal gap for 2026. However, this depends 
on obtaining a favorable injunction from the STF before the 
report is published.
The economic team continues to seek alternatives to meet the 
primary surplus targets. With Congress showing clear fatigue 
regarding new revenue-raising measures and expenditure 
pressures mounting, the Executive and Legislative are trying to 
reach a consensus on a proposal for a linear cut in tax 
expenditures. This idea is also being championed by President 
Lula as part of his social justice agenda.
The current proposal targets only ordinary tax benefits (i.e., tax 
expenditures that can be changed by ordinary law without 
requiring a constitutional amendment), given the assessment 
that a broader reduction would not be politically viable. 
According to Finance Ministry estimates, this could yield 
around BRL 15 billion this year and next.

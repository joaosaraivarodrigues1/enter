• 
Global conditions improved somewhat for Brazil following a slight recovery in commodity prices 
and Trump’s decision to postpone tariff hikes. But uncertainty remains high. Amid solid growth 
and sticky inflation, we no longer see the Fed cutting rates this year.

In Brazil, signs of an economic slowdown are getting clearer. We expect GDP to grow 2.0% in 
2025, after increasing 3.6% in 2024. The economic slowdown should continue into 2026, leading 
to a mild 1.0% GDP growth, on the back of lagged effects of contractionary monetary policy.
• 
We believe the government should face fewer challenges in reaching the fiscal target in 2025, 
thanks to a macroeconomic scenario that favors tax collection. Nonetheless, the continued 
upward trend of government debt will remain a key concern.
• 
In our view, the drivers behind the recent BRL appreciation remain fragile, and the forward-
looking scenario remains one of high volatility. Therefore, we maintained our exchange rate 
forecast unchanged - 6.20 reais per dollar by YE 2025 and 6.40 reais per dollar by YE 2026.
• 
Current inflation remains under pressure. We have maintained our projection for the IPCA this 
year at 6.1%. For 2026, a considerably contractionary monetary policy is expected to take effect. 
We project the IPCA inflation at 4.5% next year.
• 
The Copom kept a hawkish tone in its first meeting of the year, in response to rising inflation 
expectations. We continue to see the terminal Selic rate at 15.50%, considering a 100-75-50-bp 
hike path in the coming decisions. The Copom may interrupt the tightening cycle earlier than we 
expect if the BRL stabilizes and the economic slowdown intensifies.

Foreword – Skating on thin ice
After a quite volatile December, Brazilian asset prices outperformed their peers in January. We believe that there are economic 
reasons to justify this calm-after-the-storm moment.
Prices improved due to external and local drivers. On the external front, President Trump’s initial moves were less aggressive 
than markets feared. Moreover, commodity prices recovered somewhat, particularly crude oil. Thus, even considering no 
further rate cuts by the Fed this year, EM assets improved.
In Brazil, economic policy reacted to macro imbalances. Central Bank stepped up the tightening pace and intervened 
massively in the FX market. The government approved in Congress a set of (timid) measures to curb expenditure growth – in 
record time. Economic activity is showing initial signs of deceleration, indicating that tighter monetary policy is coming into 
effect.
Under this relatively more stable scenario, we see IPCA inflation peaking at 6.1% this year, before receding to around 4.5% 
next year.
The balance, however, still looks fragile.
Markets will closely monitor the guidelines of Brazilian economic policy. Weaker economic growth does not match well with 
falling government approval, particularly with a presidential election campaign approaching. Indeed, in a recent speech, 
President Lula said “2026 has already started”.
Moreover, the global economic and political outlook should remain volatile, to say the least, as the first days of February have 
already shown.
If risk aversion rises again, we see little room for further fiscal austerity measures or monetary response. In that case, we 
would see further BRL depreciation, which would keep inflation away from the target for longer. Central Bank would face a 
much more difficult inflation vs. growth trade-off.
In other words, the conditions are set for a relatively balanced scenario - a bridge to the 2026 elections. But inflation will 
remain above the target, government debt will remain rising, and growth is likely to lose steam. Markets will be skating on thin 
ice.

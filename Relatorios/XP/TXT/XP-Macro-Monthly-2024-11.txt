raise concerns about the global economic outlook ahead. We expect a gradual global 
monetary easing ahead, with the Fed funds rate stabilizing at 3.5% by 2025 YE.

In Brazil, we forecast that GDP will grow 3.1% in 2024 and 1.8% in 2025. This scenario

impulse, and the end of spare capacity of production factors.
• 
We expect the government to reach the lower limit of the primary result target this

framework.

explained by pressures on foodstuff and upward adjustments in fuel and electricity.

after four additional 50-bp hikes. The committee may opt to increase the pace to reach 
the terminal level earlier. This more forceful approach should open room for some

Foreword – BCB reaction needs to be more intense than anticipated
By August, when we first incorporated a tightening cycle in our baseline scenario, a 12.00% Selic rate seemed enough to
stabilize IPCA inflation within the target range. However, the inflation outlook has deteriorated further since then.
All inflation drivers worsened. Growth beat expectations, narrowing the output gap. Indeed, the unemployment rate and
capacity utilization in the manufacturing sector have been at their lower/higher level for many years. The exchange rate
reached its weakest level since 2021 (-14.6 YTD). 2025 inflation expectations rose, approaching the upper bound of the target
range.
Under those circumstances, IPCA over the relevant horizon (mid-2026) seems more likely to stand above the upper limit
(4.5%) than close to the 3.0% target – as a matter of fact, our forecasts for 2024 and 2025 are already above that level. This
requires an even tighter monetary policy. Thus, we now see the Selic rate reaching 13.25% (12.00% before). This prompter
approach opens room for some easing by the end of 2025 – we anticipate two 50-bps cuts in November and December.
Challenges also persist on the fiscal front. Meeting the zero-deficit target demands some BRL 200 billion in addition revenues,
which are hard to achieve. Quasi-fiscal expenditures are also on the rise, which is not incorporated in the target but puts
pressure on government debt, nonetheless. It does not bode well with the stable inflation pursued by the Central Bank.
At the end of the day, the old cliché holds: the picture is good (strong growth, relatively low inflation, comfortable trade surplus,
solid tax revenues), but the movie is worrying. The uncertain macro sustainability is reflected in the bad price action of
Brazilian asset prices in recent weeks. Buy opportunity? It depends on how effective the policy reaction will be.
And, naturally, on how the global economy will react to the Trump mandate in the US and to Chinese economic stimulus.

A shift in focus is key to ensuring fiscal sustainability. After
insisting on a strategy of fiscal adjustment by increasing
revenue, the government is finally due to announce a package
of spending cuts. We expect the changes to affect programs
such as unemployment insurance, wage bonus and BPC, as
well as the indexation of health and education minimums, with
potential savings of BRL 32.7 billion by 2026 (see our report
here). These measures are essential to slow the growth of
mandatory expenses so they do not crowd out all room for
discretionary spending, which could otherwise compromise
the fiscal framework. Although important, we believe these
adjustments will fall short of ensuring long-term fiscal
sustainability, as deeper changes will likely be needed,
including de-indexing benefits.

Foreword – What is the post-Covid steady-state?
In this Brazil Macro Monthly, we present our 2025 forecasts. Considering we are approaching the end of the 
Covid cycle, with global monetary policy at or close to its restrictive peak, the two-year ahead forecasts should 
be close to steady-state equilibrium. This equilibrium, however, is particularly uncertain since the pandemic dust 
has not settled yet.
Starting with a key variable for any economic scenario: what is the risk-free interest rate level? Long-term US 
Treasury yields are on the rise for different reasons, such as US fiscal deterioration and trade deglobalization. 
In the wake of a polarized and populist presidential election next year, these factors should not fade anytime 
soon. Thus, one should consider that global rates will probably be higher than pre-pandemic levels in the 
foreseeable future.
In Brazil, the fragility lies in public accounts’ dynamics. The new fiscal framework will lead to debt stabilization 
only if tax revenues increase significantly in the following years, which is quite uncertain (and unlikely). Fiscal 
instability makes forecasting long-term nominal variables more challenging. On the other hand, institutional 
strengths, such as central bank independence, help to bring some predictability.
Having said that, we forecast that inflation will eventually stabilize at 4.0%, above the target but still relatively low 
compared to Brazilian historical standards. That requires monetary policy to remain restrictive in 2024 and reach 
its neutral level only in 2025. Our neutral real interest rate (r*) assumption is 5.0%. Thus, we see the nominal 
Selic rate at 10.0% next year and at 9.0% in 2025.
This scenario is consistent with GDP growing below trend in 2024 (1.5%) and improving to 1.8% in 2025 – close 
to what we consider the potential growth rate.
The main risk to our view is inflation rising again after 2025, due to external and domestic factors. In this case, 
interest rates tend to be higher than our current forecast.

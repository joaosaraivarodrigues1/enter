Foreword – Adjustment in 2022, rebalancing in 2023. 
In Brazil, the focus will be on fiscal issues.
2022 was a year of adjustment for the world economy. At the beginning of the year, it was already clear
that the anti-Covid measures – on both social distancing and on stimulus to demand – were starting to
do more harm than good. Global inflation ballooned, further boosted by the war in Ukraine.
During the year, restrictions on transport eased, leading to the normalization of global supply chains
and freight prices. On the demand side, several central banks stepped up interest rate hikes, reducing
liquidity and bringing markets down.
The adjustment is not over yet, especially in monetary policy. But we believe it will eventually work.
Inflation should retreat in the second half, opening room for a gradual policy easing. The economy will
still suffer, especially services and the labor market. But it is possible that growth expectations for the
coming years will improve throughout 2023.
Two facts are particularly positive for Brazil under this scenario. Elevated commodity prices – which
should remain, due to supply issues and China´s economic reopening – and the global trend towards
‘greener economies’.
In Brasil, perhaps due to this favorable environment, and the fact that Brazilian assets were already
undervalued, the recent increase in fiscal uncertainties has not significantly hit the FX and equity
markets.
In the future interest rates market, it has been different. The market began to price in higher policy rates
for longer, in response to the more expansionary fiscal policy signaled by the future government.
Indeed, last month we changed our interest rate forecast from 10.00% to 13.75% at the end of 2023.
We see the fiscal expansion signaled by the future government as inconsistent with a stable inflation
target of 3%. We anticipate increasing tension between the Executive branch and the Central Bank. We
even do not rule out a discussion on raising the inflation target at some point throughout the year
(which would only fuel inflationary dynamics, as Argentina's recent experience has shown).
Other key themes for economic policy this year will be the possible proposals to increase the tax burden
(tax on fuels, on wealth, on dividends), the new fiscal framework to replace the constitutional spending
cap, and the tax reform that should create a national VAT.

Global Backdrop: Disinflation, with a cost
In the US, the Fed kept a hawkish stance. Despite the downside 
surprises in the latest CPI prints and most leading indicators 
suggesting that disinflation is underway, members of the FOMC 
continue to signal additional rate hikes in their economic projections. 
Given the Fed’s assessment that inflation is the largest threat to the US 
economy, we believe that members are trying to keep the hawkish tone 
to contain inflation expectations. We expect the Fed to hike rates in 
subsequent meetings until there are clearer signs that the economy is 
decelerating (including the labor market), and inflation is converging to 
the 2% target. But it seems to us that interest rate forecasts are 
peaking and will no longer keep shifting up as they did throughout last 
year (see chart).
The key question is: when will the Fed cut rates? Financial conditions 
have already become restrictive, which will likely drive the US economy 
into a recession. The intensity of the recession will depend on how long 
interest rates will have to remain in restrictive territory rather than how 
much further the Fed will tighten. We expect the disinflationary process 
to advance materially in the first half of 2023, which may allow the Fed 
to start cutting rates by the end of this year. Still, risks are tilted to the 
upside. Goods inflation has eased materially in recent months, 
although services inflation (57% of the CPI basket) remains pressured. 
We expect services inflation to stabilize in the coming months, and the 
evolution of this index will be key for the future of Fed monetary policy 
and of the US economy as a whole.
In Europe, positive surprises in economic activity, while the ECB has 
become (much) more hawkish. High levels of uncertainty amid the war 
in Ukraine and historically high inflation will most likely drive the 
European economy into a recession this year. Nevertheless, recent 
data show that, so far, the European economy is resisting entering 
deep contractionary territory, supported by improvements in global 
supply chains. Regarding monetary policy, the ECB decelerated the 
tightening pace to 50bps in its last meeting, in line with expectations. 
Still, the communication stated that “interest rates will still have to rise 
significantly at a steady pace to reach levels that are sufficiently 
restrictive to ensure a timely return of inflation to the 2% medium-term 
target.” Since then, the market pricing of the main refinancing terminal 
rate has increased from 2.75% to 3.50%. Tighter financial conditions 
will be an additional headwind for the European economy in 2023.
China’s reopening advances considerably. The Chinese government 
recently announced removing quarantine requirements for travelers, 
marking a significant advance toward easing Covid restrictions. Given 
that low inflation has allowed for expansionary monetary and fiscal 
policy throughout the whole of last year, we believe the reopening of 
the economy will boost consumption, and GDP should grow north of 
5% in 2023, partially offsetting the weakness in other regions. Still, the 
recent spike in Covid cases poses risk to these expectations.
Commodity prices should remain at elevated levels. The global 
disinflation process should gain traction in 2023, in line with the tighter 
monetary policy and the normalization of global supply chains. That 
said, the reopening of China and supply constraints on grains and 
energy should keep commodity prices at high levels.

Brazil: Fiscal and monetary policies on 
a collision course
Economic Activity – New year, new pace of 
growth
Brazil’s GDP grew above expectations in 2022, but it has been 
decelerating significantly in the recent period. We estimate that the 
Brazilian economy expanded by 3% in 2022, with a consistent recovery 
for most sectors. Nonetheless, activity indicators slowed sharply in the 
second half of the year. In this regard, headline GDP remained flat in 
Q4 compared to Q3 as per our forecasts (an increase of 2.3% from 
Q421). If that forecast proves accurate, the statistical carry-over effect 
for 2023 GDP growth will be 0.5pp.
Labor market recovery loses steam. The net creation of formal 
occupations (CAGED report) remains on a slowdown path. Based on 
our seasonally adjusted estimates, the balance of jobs creation 
dropped from an approximate monthly average of 200k between 
January to September to 120k in October and 70k in November. The 
latest data available from the Continuous PNAD (National Household 
Sample Survey) have also presented a weaker dynamic of the 
employed population, considering all categories of workers. On the 
brighter side, real labor earnings continue to rise, although still running 
below pre-pandemic levels.
Tighter credit conditions. Total credit concessions are still hovering at 
high levels, however with heterogeneous signals among categories. 
Regarding individuals, emergency lines of credit – such as overdraft 
and revolving credit cards – record a sharp increase, whereas lines 
linked to personal credit and purchase of durable goods are 
weakening. The household debt service ratio remains at worrying 
levels, and non-performing loans have risen in recent months. Market 
analysts will likely pay close attention to these indicators throughout 
the current year.
Amidst the restrictive monetary policy and the dissipation of the ‘post-
Covid’ impulse, GDP is expected to rise by 1% in 2023. The worsening 
of financial conditions – heightened risk perception added to rising 
interest rates – have weighed on domestic activity. For example, the 
latest figures of industrial production and retail sales were tepid, 
especially for semi-durable and durable consumer goods. Similarly, we 
have noticed signs of stabilization in the service sector after the strong 
‘post-Covid’ impulse. Furthermore, there have been mounting concerns 
about recession in the global economy. Conversely, some factors (in 
addition to the positive statistical carry-over effect from last year’s 
GDP, as mentioned before) should act as a counterweight and, as a 
result, allow for a modest increase in 2023 GDP.
First, sectors less sensitive to economic cycles, such as agriculture, 
livestock, and extractive industries, are expected to grow sharply this 
year. Investments in infrastructure (e.g., sanitation and transport) 
should also be resilient. Additionally, the gradual recovery of wages and 
government cash transfers should help ease the weakening of 
household spending (we anticipate real that household disposable 
income should grow by 2.5% versus 2022), especially through the 
consumption of non-durable goods and services. Lastly, the economic

The deteriorating fiscal outlook impacts medium-term economic 
growth projections. The likely rise in primary expenditures and 
government debt in the coming years may lead to higher inflation and 
interest rates for longer, as discussed in upcoming sections of this 
report. This scenario explains, to a large extent, the expectations of low

We forecast 2024 GDP growth at 0.8%. For now, we continue to 
anticipate a modest performance for the local activity next year. In our 
view, the maintenance of the monetary policy in contractionary 
territory counterbalances the prospects of a rebounding global 
economy. Therefore, GDP growth should remain below potential 
(which we estimate nearly 2% per year) over the projected horizon, 
resulting in increased idle capacity. For a more in-depth discussion on 
this subject, see the report XP Macro Special: Discussions on cyclical 
recovery and structural economic growth.
Fiscal – Debt-to-GDP ratio to increase by 10pp in 
two years
Public sector should record a primary surplus of 1.2% of GDP in 2022. 
The central government ended November with a deficit of BRL 16.5 
billion, due both to a slowdown in tax collections and a significant 
increase in social expenses. States and municipalities, in turn, kept the 
recent negative trend caused by ICMS-tax cuts on fuels and electricity, 
reaching a deficit of BRL 3.7 billion in the month. Taking November 
numbers into account, we reduced our primary surplus projection for 
2022 from BRL 55.2 billion to BRL 52.8 billion (0.5% of GDP) for the 
central government, and from BRL 67.5 billion to BRL 65.9 billion for 
regional governments (0.7% of GDP).
Strong spending growth in 2023... The 2023 budget was approved 
considering an inflation projection much higher than the market 
consensus (7.2% against 5.6%), which “inflated” the spending cap by 
about BRL 24 billion. In addition, the constitutional amendment 
approved at the end of 2022 allowed the new government to increase 
expenditures by approximately BRL 170 billion above the spending cap 
rule this year.
...with no funding. The PIS/Cofins tax rates exemption on fuels, at the 
cost of BRL 54 billion per year, was partially extended. As a result, only 
BRL 29 billion is expected in revenues (from these sources) this year. 
So far, there has been no announcement of additional measures that 
could rebalance the budget in 2023, either on the revenue or 
expenditure side.
Thus, we raised our 2023 Central Government deficit forecast from 
BRL 106.9 billion (1.0% of GDP) to BRL 167.0 billion (1.6% of GDP).
For 2024, considering that expenses will continue to grow above 
inflation and that the approval of significant increases in the tax burden 
seems unlikely (though we believe the government will try), we 
estimate a deficit of BRL 129.8 billion (1.2% of GDP) for the Central 
Government.
States will also have a tough year. There has been a deterioration in the 
state’s fiscal position largely due to ICMS-tax cuts on electricity and

revert that measure, but the outcome of it remains unclear. Thus, we 
estimate a substantial deterioration in the primary result of regional

Public debt on the rise. Gross General Government Debt is expected to

above current levels.
New fiscal framework will be key to reducing additional uncertainties. 
The government announced it will propose a new fiscal anchor to 
replace the current expenditure cap. For that to be credible, we believe 
it must contain a spending control mechanism similar to the current 
cap, albeit with more flexibility to accommodate shocks. The success 
of the new framework is key for Brazil’s sustainable growth in the 
coming years.
Discussions on tax reform may advance, which would be good news. 
We believe the government will push for the national VAT proposal 
embodied in the Constitutional Amendment 45/2019. The Senate 
speaker, Mr. Rodrigo Pacheco, signaled recently that this should be a 
priority for the year. There is, however, the risk that the need to increase 
tax revenues forces the government to propose a “too-elevated” VAT 
rate, which could make the reform´s approval more difficult.
Inflation – Fiscal expansion may jeopardize 
disinflation
The inflationary dynamics was more benign at the end of 2022, which 
should continue in the coming months. Goods inflation is on a clear 
declining path and services prices have risen less than expected. We 
estimate IPCA inflation at 5.6% in 2022 and 5.4% in 2023 (with a 65bps 
contribution from the reversal of tax cuts on fuels).
Food and industrialized goods to be the drivers of disinflation 
ahead. Favorable weather conditions and lower production costs 
should boost agricultural output this year. Industrialized goods benefit 
from the normalization of global supply chains and lower domestic 
demand. China reopening and the war in Ukraine are risks to this 
scenario.
On the demand side, high interest rates and a slowdown in economic 
activity have become more intense. The effects of contractionary 
monetary policy are becoming more evident. Tighter credit conditions 
already affect aggregate demand and limit price increases, especially 
for consumer durable goods.
We estimate 6.2% services inflation this year. We replicated the 
exercise presented by the Central Bank in its latest Inflation Report, that 
estimates the effects of the economic cycle and the stickiness (inertia) 
on services inflation. Our estimates suggest that inflation will fall 
because of both reasons, but only gradually, remaining above the 
upper limit of the target range (see chart). In fact, even with economic 
activity losing steam, the expected fiscal expansion should prevent a 
more significant decompression on services prices.
Higher taxes are an additional risk for inflation in the short term. We 
maintain in our baseline scenario the reversal of temporary tax 
exemptions on PIS/Cofins and Cide (federal taxes on fuels) this year,

For 2024, the main risk lies in the more expansionary fiscal and quasi-

External Sector: Brazilian exchange rate diverges 
from emerging peers
The increase in fiscal uncertainties continues to pressure the 
exchange rate. Despite being the best performing currency among 23 
emerging market peers in 2022, concerns about economic policies 
under the new government, with emphasis on the dynamics of public 
finances, led to a strong depreciation in the Brazilian exchange rate in 
the last two months. When compared to 23 other emerging currencies 
against the US dollar (see chart), only the Russian Ruble and the 
Argentine Peso depreciated more than the Brazilian Real in the period, 
with 18 currencies recording appreciation. In other words, the increase 
in domestic risks seems to have more than offset the improvement in 
external drivers, such as the virtual stabilization of international 
commodity prices at high levels and the weakening of the US dollar 
against a basket of strong currencies (DXY).
Recent dynamics reinforce our forecasts for a depreciated exchange 
rate in 2023 and 2024. The worsening of the fiscal outlook and its 
potential effects on inflation, interest rates and economic activity are 
likely to maintain the Brazilian exchange rate at high levels. In turn, we 
believe that the economic reopening in China and global disinflation 
should act as a counterweight, preventing a more significant 
depreciation compared to current quotes. We forecast BRL/USD 5.30 
at the end of 2023 and BRL/USD 5.40 at the end of 2024 (the exchange 
rate ended 2022 at BRL/USD 5.22). According to our estimates, had it 
not been for the increase in domestic risks, the exchange rate could 
have reached levels below BRL/USD 5.00 at the end of last year.
The favorable dynamics of the Brazilian external accounts persist, 
despite global and domestic uncertainties. We project that the current 
account deficit will reach USD 54.0 billion by the end of 2022 (-3.0% of 
GDP), against the projection of USD 57.8 billion. In turn, we forecast 
that FDI reached USD 84.5 billion this year (4.4% of GDP), the same 
projection as the previous month. For 2023 and 2024, the current 
account deficit is forecasted to increase to USD 60.5 billion (-3.0% of 
GDP) and USD 63.0 billion (-3.1% of GDP), respectively. Meanwhile, FDI 
is expected to yield USD 77.0 billion (3.9% of GDP) at the end of 2023 
and USD 76.0 billion (3.7% of GDP) in 2024.
Monetary Policy – Fiscal expansion should keep 
pressure on rates
We continue to see short-term inflation on a positive trend, keeping the 
Central Bank comfortable for now. Global disinflation and domestic 
economic deceleration should keep consumer inflation on a downward 
trend. Recent IPCA data show decompression even in stickier groups, 
like services prices.
However, as we pointed out in our December monthly report, the fiscal 
expansion signaled by the constitutional amendment approved 
recently (see more in the fiscal section above) should eventually put

challenge. In fact, the central bank incorporated this risk in its recent

Considering these two opposite forces, we continue to see the Copom 
keeping the Selic rate at current contractionary levels throughout this 
year, in order to offset the expansionary fiscal policy.
Under the hypothesis that i) the next fiscal framework will lead to more 
sustainable debt dynamics, ii) the role of public banks in the credit 
market will be limited, and iii) the inflation target for 2024 and 2025 will 
remain at 3.0%, we see room for the Selic rate to fall to 10% in 2024

Against this background, we see increasing tension between the 
Central Bank and the Executive Branch this year. As the economic 
activity loses steam and government debt dynamics worsens, the 
pressure on the BCB to cut rates tends to increase. We even do not rule 
out a discussion on raising the inflation target at some point 
throughout the year (which would only fuel inflationary dynamics, as 
Argentina's recent experience has shown).

Foreword – Heaven on Earth?
The Brazilian economic outlook for 2024 has improved. We now expect stronger GDP growth (2.0% vs 1.5% before) 
and lower IPCA inflation (3.5% vs. 3.7%) in 2024. Moreover, tax collection hit a record high in January, and the trade 
surplus keeps topping expectations. Who could ask for more?
Still, Brazilian asset prices are not doing so great so far this year. Challenges, indeed, remain.
Globally, inflation seems to be stabilizing at a level still relatively elevated. The labor market remains tight in several 
Western economies, keeping services prices under pressure. Commodity prices rebounded somewhat, softening 
cost-push disinflation. Under those circumstances, the Fed and the ECB will not cut rates so soon, reverting the 
market´s (over)optimism created late last year.
In Brazil, economic growth has been solid, but with a worrisome composition. Consumption, boosted by 
government transfers and rising real labor earnings, is the main driver, along with commodity exports. Gross fixed 
capital formation, in turn, fell by 3% last year. It does not bode well for medium-term sustainable growth. Indeed, 
labor market may already be entering overheated territory.
Fiscal dynamics also remain a risk despite a good start of the year. Even considering the better performance in 
primary revenue, we still see a fiscal deficit north of 0.5% of GDP in 2024, and that will probably widen in 2025 
without this year’s one-off revenue gains.
Against this backdrop, the Copom should continue to gradually cut interest rates (by 50bps per meeting, eventually 
slowing to 25bps) until the Selic reaches around 9.00%. Currently, a higher terminal rate looks more likely than 
otherwise.

The timing of the US easing cycle remains uncertain. 
Economic indicators reinforce that the labor market 
remains tight, consumption growth remains robust, and 
inflation lingers above the 2% target. Of particular concern,

show a clearer reinflationary trend. The services sector 
tends to be more sensitive to the tight labor market and is, 
therefore, expected to remain sticky in the short term. 
Hawkish data has driven markets to significantly reprice 
monetary easing expectations over the past two months 
from 6-7 cuts in 2024 to only 3-4 cuts.

Rodolfo Margato
Brazil’s GDP increased by 2.9% in 2023, despite stability in 
the second half. Domestic activity advanced, for the second 
consecutive year, well above initial projections. This 
occurred despite the stability recorded in 3Q and 4Q. 
Sectors less sensitive to the economic cycle contributed 
substantially to overall GDP expansion last year. We 
estimate that agriculture, livestock, and extractive industries 
together accounted for 1.3pp of total economic growth. The 
record grain harvest – registered in the first half of the year 
– and the sharp increase in oil production played a leading 
role in the period. Furthermore, the real household 
disposable income jumped by around 6.5% in 2023, in line 
with the massive fiscal transfers and the tight labor market, 
which boosted consumption (especially of services). For a 
detailed analysis of the 4Q23 GDP, click here.

External Accounts – The trade balance 
continues to surprise to the upside, while 
exchange rate remains under pressure
Rodolfo Margato
In the short term, the global scenario continues to pressure 
the exchange rate... The market’s repricing of monetary 
easing expectations in the United States (see the "Global 
Backdrop" section), along with the recent drop in agricultural 
and metallic commodity prices, is keeping the exchange 
rate under considerable strain. The DXY index, which tracks 
the dollar’s performance against a basket of major 
currencies, has increased by nearly 3% since the end of last 
year. This dynamic is further intensified by geopolitical 
tensions, which increase global risk aversion.
…still, the robustness of the balance of payments 
underscores a pathway for appreciation until the end of the 
year. Our exchange rate forecast remains unchanged at 
R$/US$ 4.70 by the end of 2024, despite higher depreciation 
in the first half (around R$/US$ 5.00). The strong 
performance of Brazil's external accounts reinforces our 
confidence in the potential for currency appreciation. 
Looking ahead to 2025, we anticipate an exchange rate of 
R$/US$ 4.90, closely mirroring the inflation differential 
between Brazil and the United States for that period.

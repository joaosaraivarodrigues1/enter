The Fed´s easing cycle began with a larger cut than we expected. Still, we see a slower pace ahead 
and a terminal rate of 3.5%. In China, aggressive stimulus should boost growth, although structural 
challenges persist. That, along with geopolitical tensions, may pressure commodity prices.
• 
In Brazil, income and credit growth are keeping demand strong. We continue to expect GDP to 
increase by 3.1% in 2024. Economic growth should be close to potential in 2025 (1.8%), in line with 
tighter monetary conditions and the end of spare capacity in production factors.
• 
We assign a high probability that the government will meet the lower limit of the fiscal target this 
year. For 2025, however, we continue to see a scenario of major challenges, given the need to raise 
uncertain revenues. We do not see debt-to-GDP stabilizing in the foreseeable future.
• 
The FX remains volatile, with forces in both directions. Thus, we keep our projection of 5.40 reais 
per dollar for 2024 and 2025.
• 
The relief in September's inflation data is likely to be temporary, given tighter spare capacity, 
adverse weather conditions and elevated inflation expectations. We raised our 2024 IPCA projection 
to 4.6% from 4.4% due to energy tariffs. For 2025 IPCA, we forecast 4.1% (from 4.0%), reflecting 
higher pressures on protein prices.
• 
We keep our terminal Selic rate forecast at 12.00%, with the hiking pace increasing to 50 bps in 
November. Risks seem to be tilted to the upside (higher Selic rate) if the narrower spare capacity in 
the economy effectively pushes inflation up. A possible FX appreciation, in its turn, would be a 
positive risk.

Foreword – Where there is a will, there is a way
The government is on track to achieving its primary fiscal balance target this year (the lower bound of the target range, 
actually). In January, very few analysts believed this was possible. On top of that, Moody’s upgraded the Brazilian sovereign 
rating.
Still, “fiscal risks” continues to be mentioned as a factor behind the underperformance of Brazilian assets this year. One reason 
is that the target will be met, partially due to one-off revenue (some 60 billion reais), meaning that the fiscal problem was 
merely postponed to next year.
The other reason is that measures to increase expenditures beyond the legal limits have arisen, such as a social program 
beyond the budget (“Gás para todos”), transference from public funds to finance BNDES lending, expenditures not considered 
in the legal limit to address the effects of September fires. Those measures do not represent a relevant amount of money, but 
they reveal a fiscal and quasi-fiscal expansionary bias that the fiscal framework established last year is not sufficient to 
contain. Where there is a will, there is a way.
At the end of the day, it is becoming clear that, despite being important, the fiscal rules will probably not be sufficient to 
stabilize the debt-to-GDP ratio.
Meanwhile, economic activity remains heated, fueled by fiscal expansion, credit growth (from public banks and heated fixed-
income capital market), and the export of commodities. The indicators of idleness, such as the unemployment rate and 
manufacturing capacity utilization, suggest virtually no spare capacity in the economy. It is a risk for future inflation, as the 
central bank has pointed out.
Against this background, we keep our view that the Copom will hike the Selic rate until reaching 12.00%. The risk is tilted to 
the upside, unless the BRL - pushed by higher interest rate differential amid a more dovish Fed, by the recovery of China and 
by higher commodity prices - strengthens significantly.

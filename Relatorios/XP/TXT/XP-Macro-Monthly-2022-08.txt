August 2022
/ Macro Research
Foreword – Global monetary policy and domestic fiscal outlook: Two 
uncertainties that should not go away anytime soon
The uncertainties about how much the Fed and the ECB will tighten and about the future of fiscal policy in
Brazil have caused volatility in Brazilian assets.
The Fed hiked by 75 bps once again in July, taking interest rates to the range of 2.25%-2.50%, and signaled
that the next decisions will be data dependent. The economy is slowing, which has encouraged markets to
believe rates will not rise much beyond 3%. But inflation remains high, and the labor market seems
overheated. From this perspective, inflation may not recede before interest rates exceed 4% or 5%.
If the second scenario proves to be correct, a global recession becomes more likely, and risky assets may fall
further. We suspect that this uncertainty will last at least until the end of the year, when (and if) inflation starts
to subside.
On the domestic side, the future of fiscal policy is key to sustainable growth. Despite the recent improvement
in tax collections, public debt remains high. Since June, we have implemented almost R$150 billion in
expansionary measures, among tax cuts and spending increases.
The next government will face enormous pressure to extend these measures and adopt new ones (such as
increases in civil servants' salaries). In the fiscal section of this report, we showed that this could mean a new
Constitutional Amendment authorizing almost R$200 billion in permanent expenditure increases. It is difficult
to stabilize the debt dynamics in this scenario, especially because interest rates would remain at high levels.
As is the case for global monetary policy, the future of fiscal policy is also unlikely to be clear until the
presidential elections in October.
These uncertainties make the job of economic forecasting even more challenging. In our baseline scenario,
we assume that US interest rates will not exceed 4% and that there will be some kind of fiscal anchor in the
next presidential term. In this scenario, inflation fades throughout 2023, opening room for some monetary
easing. The exchange rate stabilizes around current levels.
The degree of conviction, however, is limited. Easier than projecting the Brazilian economy in 2023 is
anticipating that the market will remain volatile in the second half of the year.

Global Backdrop:  Risk to global economic 
activity has increased
The US GDP falls but signs of overheating remain. As we had 
anticipated for a while, the US economy contracted for the second 
consecutive quarter, marking a technical recession. The slowdown 
was mainly driven by a downturn in investment and government 
appending, as well as a considerable slowdown in personal 
consumption expenditure growth. Nevertheless, the US economy does 
not present clear recession characteristics, given that the labor market 
is tight, demand remains high, and companies’ profits continue to 
grow. Therefore, despite a range of indicators pointing to weakening 
economic performance, we do not anticipate a deep recession. We see 
the US GDP growing by 1.6% in 2022 and 1.5% in 2023.
The Fed hiked 75 bps again and will take interest rates into restrictive 
territory in their next meeting. We believe that the FOMC´s decision to 
maintain the strong tightening pace was mainly driven by the upward 
surprise in the June CPI print, which showed continued widespread 
price pressures, and the latest data suggesting that the labor market 
remains very tight. Interest rates have now moved to the level that we 
consider neutral (2.5%), and any additional hikes will take the policy 
rate into restrictive territories. We expect that the Fed will slow down 
the tightening pace from now on and deliver 50bps in September, 
followed by 25bps in November, reaching the terminal interest rates of 
3.25%. We see room for rate cuts in Q4 2023 on the back of material 
disinflation and a slowdown in economic activity.
Economic activity continues to disappoint in China. The Chinese Q2 
GDP showered a weaker than expected performance, suggesting 
continued deceleration and that the economy is taking longer to 
rebound from the strict lockdown measures imposed at the beginning 
of the year. Despite the ongoing fiscal stimulus, we believe the 5.5% 
growth target has become increasingly unlikely. We see China´s GDP 
expanding by around 3.2% in 2022, although risks remain to the 
downside given uncertainties regarding the zero-covid policy and the 
effectiveness of the stimulus measures.
Risks to the Euro Zone remain high. Despite economic activity having 
positively surprised market expectations, growth was mainly driven by 
the post-covid rebound in consumption and tourism. Nevertheless, the 
medium-term outlook is worrying, given uncertainty regarding 
restrictions on gas imports from Russia as winter approaches, as well 
as the impact of tighter monetary policy.
The outlook for Latin American assets worsens. Commodity prices 
have dropped in recent weeks, reflecting lower global demand. Despite 
our expectations that commodity prices will remain high throughout 
the year compared to historical levels, easing commodity prices 
combined with increased global risk aversion weakens our call for a 
solid performance of Latin American assets in 2022. Moreover, 
domestic political uncertainty and deteriorating current accounts are 
aggravating factors for some countries of the region.

Brasil: Fiscal pressure limits benefits of 
global disinflation
Economic Activity – For now, no signs of trend 
reversal
Brazil´s economic activity remains on an upward path. Household 
consumption expanded solidly in the first half of this year, driving up 
the goods and services sectors. In addition to the benefits of loosening 
coronavirus-related restrictions, personal spending has been boosted 
by the improvement in labor market conditions, increased government 
cash-transfers to vulnerable people, and the use of ‘circumstantial 
savings’ accumulated during the pandemic. For example, the 
seasonally adjusted jobless rate (own monthly estimate) dropped to 
nearly 8.9% in June 2022 from 11.5% in December 2021, with a 
widespread expansion across sectors and types of occupation. At the 
same time, the government’s new social protection program (‘Auxílio 
Brasil’, with higher monthly benefit) added to short-term fiscal 
stimulus, such as the release of extraordinary FGTS withdrawals and 
the advance payment of bonus monthly wage guaranteed by law.
We estimate that Brazil’s GDP increased 0.9% QoQ in 2Q22 (2.7% YoY), 
a growth rate very close to that recorded in 1Q22 (1.0% QoQ). The 
official results for 2Q22 will be published on September 1st.
The household disposable income should continue at high levels in the 
coming months. The broad concept of income available to families 
rose by around 7% in the first half of 2022 compared to the second half 
of 2021, as per our estimates. Looking ahead, we expect the indicator 
to stay virtually flat in the current semester. For more information, see 
the report XP Macro Special: Monitoring the pace of growth in 
household consumption. We draw attention to the impact of the 
Constitutional Amendment 123/22, which brings additional fiscal 
stimuli that are expected to contribute to a milder deceleration of 
domestic activity in the short term than initially estimated. In our view, 
all income impulses should more than offset the downward effects of 
the rising interest rates and higher risk perception regarding both the 
global and local economic environments.
Hence, we reiterate the forecast that Brazil’s GDP will grow by 2.2% in 
2022, with a positive bias. In our baseline scenario, the rising trend in 
headline GDP should be interrupted in Q422. Nonetheless, we see the 
balance of risks around that annual projection as tilted to the upside, in 
line with the bullish surprises from recent releases for the labor market.
Lastly, we maintain the expectation of a 0.5% expansion in 2023 GDP, 
currently without bias. The stronger increase in household disposable 
income than previously thought (which considers higher benefits from 
the ‘Auxilio Brasil’ program on a permanent basis) offsets downward 
biases from the weaker global economy and mounting fiscal risks in 
the domestic environment, which deteriorate financial conditions and 
could keep interest rates high for even longer.

Public Accounts – We incorporated in our baseline 
scenario the permanent increase in expenses 
from 2023
Short-term results continue very positive. In June, federal tax collection 
grew 18% in real terms relative to the same month of the previous year, 
supported by the substantial growth of income linked to the profit and 
the payroll. In the accumulated 12-month period, the growth in 
collections has been driven by gains and IRPJ and CSLL, which rose by 
more than 24% in real terms. In addition, extraordinary revenues as 
concessions and dividends lead the Central Government to reach a 12-
month surplus of BRL 75.1 billion, returning to positive territory for the 
first time since October 2014.
Dividends should boost revenues. The change in the dividend policy of 
state-owned companies requested by the federal government should 
result in significant additional revenues this year, which may exceed R$ 
100 billion, mainly from Petrobras and BDNES. Thus, we estimate a 
surplus of R$ 15.3 billion for the Central Government this year.
We have incorporated into our scenario that expenses that are 
currently transitory will become permanent. Political discussions have 
indicated the maintenance of Auxílio Brasil at BRL 600 in 2023, with an 
estimated cost of approximately R$59 billion. In addition, the 
readjustment of public servants, the payment of postponed court 
orders and the creation of new expenses must be incorporated into the 
budget for next year.
Thus, we have adopted the hypothesis of a reform of the spending cap 
fiscal rule, with an increase in the limit in 2023 to accommodate new 
expenditures and corrections in line with inflation in the coming years 
(see table).
As a result, the Central Government will once again show a deficit of 
R$ 126.6 billion, or -1.2% of GDP. However, we recognize that there is 
an additional risk of exemptions on fuel taxes being maintained in 
2023, which could lead to a loss of revenue of up to R$52 billion.
States are expected to have significant revenue loss in the second half 
of the year. The effects of Complementary Law No. 194/2022, which 
established 
a 
ceiling 
for 
ICMS 
rates 
on 
fuel, 
electricity, 
communications, and public transport, should lead to a loss in revenue 
of around BRL 46 billion in the second half of this year.
Thus, the consolidated public sector (Central Government, States and 
Municipalities, and State-owned companies) should close the year with 
a smaller surplus, of R$ 56 billion or 0.6% of GDP. For 2023, we 
estimate a deficit of BRL 106.5 billion, or -1.0% of GDP.
With the improvement in the primary result in 2022, the General 
Government Gross Debt (GGGD) ratio should fall this year, reaching 
79.5%. But from 2023 onwards, with the increase in expenses due to 
the change in the spending cap rule, and the higher nominal interest 
account due to the maintenance of the Selic above double digits for a 
longer period, the public debt should grow further (see graph).
Despite the change in the rules, we believe in maintaining some fiscal 
anchor (adjusted ceiling, debt target, etc.) as of next year. Any change 
in the spending cap rule should focus on stabilizing and reducing public 
debt in the long term. The rise in expenditures may lead to an increase

Inflation – Higher spending, slower disinflation
Fiscal 
stimuli 
increase 
demand 
and 
diminishes 
inflationary 
deceleration. We maintain our inflation projection for 2022 at 7.0%, in 
line with the effect of tax cuts, but we revise our forecast for 2023 IPCA 
from 5.0% to 5.5%, due to higher pressures on services and 
administered goods.
The 2022 IPCA should close at 7.0%. Since our last inflation revision 
(link), when we incorporated the effects of tax cuts on electricity, fuel 
and telecommunications, the data have confirmed deflation, 
consistent with this scenario. We have already observed significant 
drops in fuel and energy prices, while telecommunications have not yet 
registered a decline. We are projecting deflation for July and August of 
this year, and the 12-month accumulated IPCA should return to single 
digits.
Without these tax cuts, inflation would be between 9% and 10% this 
year. Despite short-term deflation, the fall in prices is concentrated on 
a few items. Inflation diffusion should pull back a bit from the current 
level of 70%. Despite slowing industrial goods and services inflation, 
according to our projections, prices should continue rising given the 
environment of persisting high costs and demand, amid additional 
stimulus (increased benefit from the Brazil Aid and other measures 
that will inject more than 40 billion into the economy starting in 
August).
In our base case, we consider a 5% increase in the price of gasoline by 
Petrobras in the last quarter of the year. This represents a downside 
risk to our forecast of approximately 0.20pp.
For 2023, inflation convergence to the target will be even more difficult. 
Even if the recent fall in commodities in Brazilian reals brings some 
relief ahead, higher government spending, mainly through monetary 
transfers (as discussed in the fiscal section) next year, should mitigate 
the slowdown in consumption and keep inflation expectations high. 
Thus, we have raised the services inflation forecast for next year from 
6.5% to 7.2%, considering that monetary policy should be tightener 
during the year.
The inflation of administered goods will rise again in 2023. In addition 
to the higher inflationary pressure on services, we have revised our 
projection for managed goods in 2023 from 6.7% to 7.7%. Within the 
subgroups, we raised the forecast for medicines, health insurance and 
public transportation. Besides, we maintained in our scenario the 
reversal of federal taxes reductions on gasoline and ethanol as of 
January 2023, which implies an impact close to 0.40pp. It is an 
important downside risk for our baseline scenario.
Thus, our projection for 2023 increased from 5.0% to 5.5%.

External Sector – More volatile exchange rate 
amid global and domestic uncertainties
The exchange rate remains volatile. The Brazilian Real (BRL) 
appreciated around 7% against the US dollar (USD) since mid-July, 
when it reached the level of 5.50. Higher global risk appetite, 
particularly with the pricing of a less aggressive Federal Reserve’s 
monetary policy, explains a significant part of that recent movement. 
On the other hand, growing uncertainties regarding the domestic fiscal 
outlook have maintained the Brazilian exchange rate under pressure 
and highly volatile. The BRL is among the emerging currencies that 
have weakened the most against the USD in recent months.
We maintain the expectation that exchange rate will end 2022 at 
BRL/USD 
5.00. 
Our 
baseline 
scenario 
considers 
further 
decompression of risk premiums after the local elections, in addition 
to relatively high levels of commodity prices and clearer signs of 
disinflation (albeit gradual) in the global economy by the end of this 
year. For the end of 2023, in turn, we Project the exchange rate at 
BRL/USD 5.30, in line with concerns about the fiscal consolidation 
agenda in Brazil.
External accounts are not a source of concern. The latest official data 
for the Brazilian balance of payments refer to March 2022, due to the 
strike by Central employees that was interrupted a few weeks ago. 
Nonetheless, the main messages persist, namely: (I) the trade balance 
shows a high surplus - around USD 50 billion in the 12-month rolling 
sum – with sharp expansion in both the value of exports (especially 
agricultural products) and imports (fuels play a major role); (II) the 
deficits in the primary income and services accounts are increasing, in 
the wake of the economic reopening and solid recovery of domestic 
activity, which boost the demand for international travel, transport 
services and equipment rental; (III) net inflows of Foreign Direct 
Investment (FDI) remain at satisfactory levels, above 3% of GDP.
Therefore, the outlook of sound sources of funding for the (relatively 
low) current account deficits is unlikely to change anytime soon. We 
expect the current account balance to reach -USD 20.5 billion (-1.1% of 
GDP) in 2022 and -USD 36.0 billion (-1.8% of GDP) in 2023. That is, 
considerably better results compared to those recorded in the pre-
pandemic period (-USD 65.0 billion or -3.5% of GDP). Meanwhile, the 
FDI should amount to USD 55.0 billion (2.9% of GDP) this year and USD 
65.0 billion (3.3% of GDP) next year, levels close to the figure seen in 
2019 (USD 69.0 billion or 3.7% of GDP).
Monetary Policy – Wait-and-see now, cuts in 2023. 
But with a big fiscal uncertainty.
With global inflation pressures beginning to fade, we believe the 
Copom will finally move to the “wait-and-see” mode it has been 
signaling for a while. In our baseline scenario, the Copom keeps the 
Selic rate at 13.75% until mid-next year.
We recognize, though, that it may opt to go a bit further, to ensure that 
IPCA will fall in coming quarters.
Under normal circumstances, the Copom should find room to start 
easing monetary policy next year. Real interest rates (considering 
inflation expectations) are close to 8%, which is very high even for

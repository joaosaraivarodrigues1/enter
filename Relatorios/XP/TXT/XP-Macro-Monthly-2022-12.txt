Foreword – The Brazilian economy in 2023
If someone had told us a year ago that the Fed Funds rate would go beyond 5%, there would be a war in
Europe, the Brazilian Selic rate would reach almost 14%, and the presidential election would be decided
by the narrowest margin ever; we would surely say that 2022 would be a tough year for the Brazilian
economy and asset prices.
Quite on the contrary, GDP grew above expectations, inflationary pressures are starting to fade, and the
Brazilian real was one of the few currencies that appreciated against the US Dollar.
What is necessary to keep this relatively good momentum in 2023?
From a global viewpoint, Brazil will probably continue to be a highlight. The economy is geographically
far from geopolitical tensions, benefits from higher commodity prices and China´s reopening, and is one
of the greenest economies in the world. Moreover, elected President Lula has been well received by
most international leaders.
Global risks surely are present, as we point out in the global session of this report. But Brazil seems well
positioned for them. On the domestic front, the investment-to-GDP ratio reached 7-year highs,
suggesting stronger potential growth ahead (we estimate close to 2% per year). Inflation is gradually
decelerating, thanks to the Central Bank's good job and global cost-push disinflation. This scenario,
however, depends crucially on fiscal stability. Here lies the country's main risk ahead.
Despite recent improvements, the debt-to-GDP ratio remains at elevated levels, and expenditure
pressures are mounting. The new government members have repeatedly said that the constitutional
spending cap is no longer desirable but is yet to present an alternative. In our view, without a clear and
credible rule for expenditures, public debt will not stabilize in the foreseeable future, putting pressure on
business confidence and on inflation expectations.
Indeed, in the years before the constitutional cap, inflation was above 6%, and the Selic rate was 14%.
This time around, with an independent Central Bank that pursues an inflation target of 3%, interest rates
could be even higher, with a negative feedback loop on debt dynamics.
But it is still early to call on the fiscal policy under Lula's third mandate. When we wrote this report, the
new economic team was not even announced, let alone the economic policy guidelines. Beyond fiscal,
other economic themes like tax reform, public-private partnership models, and the role of BNDES and
Petrobras will also be key for Brazil´s economic performance in 2023 and the following years.
It seems to be a scenario full of opportunities for Brazil, but it will not materialize without sound
economic policies.

Global Backdrop: Tight monetary policy… 
brace for impact
The disinflationary process is expected to gain ground in 2023. Global 
inflationary pressures are likely to ease significantly in 2023 on the 
back of lower commodity prices, contractionary monetary policy, and 
normalizing supply chains. Global economic activity is likely to weaken 
materially next year, and some countries may enter a contractionary 
period.
Commodity prices should stabilize, although at high levels. Despite the 
material increase during the past two years, commodity prices have 
eased recently, reflecting improvements in global supply bottlenecks 
and weaker demand. We expect Brent oil prices to end the year at 
around USD 90 per barrel. Metals might still fall further, reflecting the 
weakening of the global economy, although the potential reopening of 
the Chinese economy may increase its demand. Agricultural 
commodities are expected to remain elevated due to inelastic demand 
and weather conditions affecting the supply side.
We believe the US will likely fall into a mild recession in 2023. With 
inflation not being as “transitory”, the Fed was forced to increase 
interest rates aggressively this year compared to the historical conduct 
of monetary policy. The Fed is almost certain to end 2022 with the 
upper bound of the Fed Funds rate at 4.5%. How much further up the 
Fed Funds rates will go next year will depend on the upcoming data 
related to prices, the labor market, and activity.
The labor market remains tight, and inflation remains significantly 
above the 2% target, though it has started to ease. Most leading high-
frequency indicators suggest that the US economy is slowing 
materially, especially the housing market. If the upcoming data proves 
to be benevolent, we believe that the Fed might find space to pause the 
tightening cycle sooner than the market expects. Otherwise, we are 
likely to see subsequent 25bps hikes next year until the data starts to 
“collaborate.” In any case, financial conditions have already become 
significantly restrictive, which will likely drive the US economy into a 
mild recession.
We believe that 2023 will prove to be another very challenging year for 
the European economies. Despite recent improvements in global 
supply chain bottlenecks and the mild winter start in Europe helping fill 
gas storage tanks, uncertainty remains high amid persistent 
geopolitical tensions in Ukraine, and conditions could deteriorate again 
next year during cold months. Also, interest rates are expected to 
increase further, given that core inflation is still expected to continue 
increasing in upcoming months as there is likely still more pass-
through from high wholesale energy prices to consumer prices to 
come. Against this backdrop, we anticipate a deeper recession in 
Europe in 2023.
In China, all eyes are on the zero Covid policies. The persistence of 
strict lockdown measures throughout the year and the weakness in the 
property market have hampered economic growth materially this year. 
We believe that GDP will grow by around 3.3% in 2022, considerably 
below the 5.5% target the government set at the start of the year. Given 
that low levels of inflation in China have allowed for expansionary 
monetary and fiscal policy, we believe the Chinese economy has the 
potential to grow above 5% and partially offset the weakness in global

Brazil: A bumpy beginning
Economic Activity – Increasing signs of 
slowdown
Brazil's GDP increased at a moderate pace in Q3, as expected, but 
revisions to the historical data series should lead to slightly stronger 
growth in 2022. IBGE (national statistics office) reported last week that 
headline GDP rose by 0.4% in Q3 compared to Q2 (and 3.6% compared 
to the same quarter last year), roughly in line with estimates. 
Nonetheless, upward revisions to GDP performance in previous 
quarters are likely to lead to greater economic growth this year.
Accordingly, we are revising our forecast for 2022 GDP growth to 3.0% 
from 2.8%.
That said, signs of cooling in domestic activity have been increasingly 
clear. Employment data continue on a positive trajectory but has lost 
steam in recent months. For instance, total employed population 
remained virtually flat in October, with shrinkages in the informal 
workers´ categories. Credit concessions have also been losing 
strength, especially in the lines more sensitive to the economic cycle. 
Additionally, household indebtedness remains at historically high 
levels, and non-performing loans have been rising, albeit moderately.
Industrial production and retail sales weaken amid tighter monetary 
policy. Sectors more sensitive to credit conditions (e.g., durable 
consumer goods and capital goods) show a downward path, which 
should not be reversed in the short term. In turn, sectors more sensitive 
to income still record positive numbers, particularly non-durable 
consumer goods (e.g., food, beverage, pharmaceuticals, personal 
hygiene items, and fuels). The increase in the real household 
disposable income – real wages resumed growth in the wake of a 
tighter labor market and easing inflation – and short-term fiscal stimuli 
support that dynamic.
Services and agribusiness continue to play a leading role. The tertiary 
sector remains on the rise on the back of the economic reopening and 
normalization of the consumption pattern. Transport, information, 
communication, accommodation, and food services have been

Fiscal – The risk of a political transition without 
fiscal consolidation
Positive outcome in 2022... In October, the consolidated public sector 
reached a surplus of BRL 27.1 billion thanks to a positive result of BRL 
30.2 billion from the central government and BRL 0.7 billion from state-
owned companies, partially offset by a deficit of BRL 3.9 billion from 
subnational governments. Year-to-date, the public sector shows a 
surplus of BRL 157.9 billion. Considering that expenses tend to rise 
during the latter months of the year, we expect the consolidated public 
sector to end 2022 with a surplus of BRL 128 billion (1.3% of GDP), of 
which BRL 55.2 billion (0.6% of GDP) comes from the central 
government and R$67.5 billion (0.7%) from subnational governments.
...followed by a spending increase in 2023. The newly elected 
government sent to Congress a proposal for a constitutional

Inflation – Expansionary fiscal policy should 
pressure medium-term inflation
Recent data show the beginning of the disinflation process. The IGP-M 
price index for November fell by 0.56%, after falling by 0.97% in the 
previous month. The wholesale inflation component (IPA) registered 
deflation for the fourth consecutive month. Costs decompression 
tends to reach the consumer later on.
Service inflation is also starting to show more favorable dynamics. In 
the November inflation preview, as measured by the IPCA-15, 
annualized services inflation cooled to 3.93%, from 5.91% in October 
(3-month moving average, seasonally adjusted). The group of 
underlying services – prices more sensitive to the economic cycle – 
retreated from 8.08% to 7.21% on the same basis of comparison.
The economic slowdown, as a response to the monetary adjustment 
implemented by the Central Bank, reinforces the downward trend in 
inflation in the coming quarters.
Thus, we maintain our projection for the 2022 IPCA at 5.8%, after 
registering 10.1% in 2021.
Disinflation should continue in the first quarters of 2023. We adjusted 
the IPCA projection for next year slightly, from 5.2% to 5.4%, due to 
occasional adjustments in the projection of some items, such as 
education and fuel.
However, medium-term projections already reflect expansionary fiscal 
policy. As already discussed in the activity section, the expansion of 
government expenditure represents a short-term income boost. In 
addition to the effect on aggregate demand, expansionary fiscal policy 
raises asset prices, the degree of uncertainty and inflation 
expectations. For more information, see the report “Medium-term 
expectations are key for the Copom's strategy to work.”
We forecast the IPCA at 4.0% in 2024, above the target but within the 
target range. A fiscal policy that is more expansionist than expected 
tends to contaminate agents' expectations for longer terms and may 
raise the neutral interest rate. We understand that this pressure on 
expectations will lead the Copom to maintain the Selic rate high for a 
longer period (see monetary policy session). However, even with 
interest rates at a contractionary level for longer, we believe that 
inflation will not converge to the target in 2024.

External Sector: domestic risks weigh heavily on 
the exchange rate
The Brazilian exchange rate has shown high volatility recently in the 
wake of higher fiscal risks. The prospects of deteriorating public 
accounts have exerted significant upward pressure on the exchange 
rate, which has oscillated between BRL/USD 5.05 and BRL/USD 5.40 
in recent weeks. The increase in local risks has offset the positive 
effects from external factors, such as the rise in commodity prices and 
the recent weakening of the US dollar (the DXY index, which measures 
the value of the US currency against a basket of six strong currencies, 
fell by 5% last month).
We adjusted the forecast for the exchange rate at the end of 2022, to 
BRL/USD 5.25 from BRL/USD 5.00, while maintaining the expectation 
of BRL/USD 5.30 for the end of 2023. In our view, the worsening of the 
fiscal outlook will likely continue to weigh on the exchange rate 
dynamics next year. On the other hand, signs of relief in the global 
environment, particularly the disinflation in several economies 
(potential for greater risk appetite) and the economic reopening in 
China (maintenance of commodity prices at high levels), should 
prevent a sharper depreciation of the exchange rate in Brazil. 
Considering a similar dynamic, we forecast BRL/USD 5.40 for the end 
of 2024.
Balance of payments statistics published in October brought ordinary 
and extraordinary revisions, with emphasis on changes in the 
Transportation - Freight account. As a result, the current account 
deficit for 2021 widened from USD 27.9 billion (-1.7% of GDP) to USD 
46.4 billion (-2.9% of GDP). In turn, the current account deficit from 
January to September 2022 was revised from USD 29.6 billion to USD 
39.4 billion (changes in the Transportation group accounted for 
approximately 90% of the total revision). FDI transactions have 
presented minor changes – for example, from USD 70.7 billion to USD 
68.4 billion for the Jan-Sep 2022 period.
Incorporating the October results and methodological changes in the 
balance of payments, we now project a (much) wider current account 
deficit and (slightly) smaller FDI surplus in 2022. We forecast that the 
current account balance will reach -USD 57.8 billion in 2022 (-3.0% of 
GDP), previously at -USD 41.6 billion (-2.4% of GDP). In turn, we project 
FDI at USD 84.5 billion this year (4.4% of GDP), previously at USD 89.0 
billion in 2022 (4.7% of GDP).
For 2023, we estimate the current account balance at -USD 60.5 billion 
(-3.0% of GDP) and the net FDI inflows at USD 78.3 billion (3.9% of 
GDP). Our previous estimates were -USD 46 billion (-2.3% of GDP) and 
USD 77.0 billion (3.9% of GDP), respectively. For 20234, the current 
account deficit should widen moderately, amounting to USD 63.0 
billion (-3.1% of GDP), while the FDI surplus is expected to decline to 
USD 76.0 billion (3.7% of GDP).
The favorable dynamics of the Brazilian external accounts persist, 
despite global and domestic uncertainties and the stronger-than-
anticipated increase in the current account deficit. FDI inflows will likely 
continue at high levels, slowing smoothly in the coming years. 
Meanwhile, the widening of the current account deficit should lose 
strength, in line with the cooling of domestic demand and the exchange 
rate at depreciated levels.

Monetary Policy – More expansionary fiscal policy 
postpones monetary easing
The central bank’s strategy seems to be working. The Selic rate at a 
contractionary level is taming domestic demand and short-term 
inflation expectations. The effect of the long-expected global 
disinflation on domestic prices is becoming clearer, particularly with 
the BRL reacting well to the global monetary tightening.
However, the increasing fiscal risk may jeopardize inflation 
convergency over the medium term. More expansionary fiscal policy 
expected for 2023 onwards (see the fiscal session of this report) tends 
to keep 2024 inflation expectations above the target path.
If debt sustainability is under threat, long term inflation expectations 
tend to reflect the risk of some degree of debt monetization. The 2024 
forecasts, which are already above the target according to the Focus 
survey and the futures markets (break-even inflation rate) – may move 
further up.
Under this scenario, we postponed the easing cycle that we previously 
expected to happen in 2023 to 2024. We now believe that the Copom 
will keep the Selic rate at 13.75% until 2023 yearend. Our simulations 
using the Copom’s model suggest it would be enough to offset the 
increase in inflation expectations that will probably come along with a 
more expansionary fiscal policy.
Monetary policy in 2024 will crucially depend on the new fiscal anchor. 
With more predictability on debt dynamics, the Selic rate could 
gradually converge back to a more neutral level (around 5% in real 
terms).
Thus, we forecast the Selic rate at 10.00% by 2024 yearend. However, 
we emphasize that the neutral level also depends on the debt 
dynamics and fiscal risks. Before the spending cap was established in 
2016, the Selic rate was 12-14% and inflation hovered around 6%.
In other words, if the fiscal regime is changing and fiscal policy proves 
to be even more expansionary than we now contemplate in our 
baseline scenario, the equilibrium level of the Selic rate may also 
change both in real and nominal terms. It makes forecasting monetary 
policy in Brazil today an even more challenging task.
